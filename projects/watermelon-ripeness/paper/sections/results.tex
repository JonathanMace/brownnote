% 3.1  Forward model predictions

\subsection{Forward model: tap-tone frequency predictions}
\label{sec:forward}

We evaluate the forward model for the canonical Crimson Sweet geometry
($a = \SI{0.158}{\metre}$, $c = \SI{0.123}{\metre}$,
$R_{\mathrm{eq}} = \SI{0.1453}{\metre}$) at four ripeness stages
spanning the full maturation trajectory.  The parameters and predicted
tap-tone frequencies are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:forward_results}.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Forward model predictions of the $n = 2$ flexural mode
    frequency~$f_2$ for the canonical Crimson Sweet watermelon at four
    ripeness stages.  The rind modulus~$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$, rind
    thickness~$h$, loss tangent~$\eta$, and flesh density~$\rho_f$ vary
    with ripening; all other parameters are held at their canonical values.}
  \label{tab:forward_results}
  \begin{tabular}{lcccccc}
    \toprule
    Stage & $E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ [MPa] & $h$ [mm] & $\eta$ &
      $f_2$ [Hz] & $Q$ & $f_{\mathrm{d}}$ [Hz] \\
    \midrule
    Unripe   & 200 & 20 & 0.08 & 203.2 & 12.5 & 203.0 \\
    Turning  & 120 & 17 & 0.11 & 146.7 &  9.1 & 146.5 \\
    Ripe     &  50 & 15 & 0.15 &  89.9 &  6.7 &  89.6 \\
    Overripe &  15 & 13 & 0.20 &  46.4 &  5.0 &  46.2 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The tap-tone frequency drops monotonically from $\SI{203.2}{\hertz}$ for
the unripe fruit to $\SI{46.4}{\hertz}$ for the overripe fruit---a
factor of~4.4 reduction driven primarily by the decrease in
$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ from \SI{200}{\mega\pascal} to
\SI{15}{\mega\pascal}.  The quality factor~$Q$ also decreases from~12.5
to~5.0 as the loss tangent rises with pectin degradation, reflecting the
increasingly lossy mechanical response of the softening rind.

A central prediction of the model is that $f_2^2$ is exactly linear in
$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ when turgor pressure is negligible.  We verify this
by computing $\alpha = f_2^2 / E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ across the full
modulus range $\SIrange{10}{250}{\mega\pascal}$ for the canonical ripe
geometry ($h = \SI{15}{\milli\metre}$).  The proportionality constant is
\begin{equation}
  \alpha = \SI{161.5 \pm 0.1}{\hertz\squared\per\mega\pascal}
  \label{eq:alpha}
\end{equation}
with a coefficient of variation of \SI{0.07}{\percent}, confirming
near-perfect linearity.  The residual variation arises from the weak
turgor pressure contribution ($P_{\mathrm{int}} = \SI{500}{\pascal}$),
which introduces a small constant offset in the stiffness.  This
linearity validates the closed-form inversion of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:inversion}: no iterative solver is required.

We also verify that the inversion is self-consistent.  For each
ripeness stage, we compute $f_2$ from the forward model and then
invert the frequency back to $E_{\mathrm{rind}}$.  In all four cases
the round-trip error is less than $\SI{0.01}{\percent}$, confirming
that the inversion formula (Eq.~\ref{eq:inversion}) is algebraically
exact within the model framework.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig_frequency_vs_E}
  \caption{Predicted tap-tone frequency~$f_2$ as a function of rind
    elastic modulus~$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ for the canonical watermelon
    geometry ($a = \SI{0.158}{\metre}$, $\zeta = 0.78$,
    $h = \SI{15}{\milli\metre}$).  The squared frequency is exactly
    linear in~$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ (inset shows $f_2^2$ vs.\
    $E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ with $R^2 > 0.9999$), confirming that the
    inversion formula~\eqref{eq:inversion} is well-posed.}
  \label{fig:frequency_vs_E}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Sobol sensitivity analysis}
\label{sec:sobol}

We perform a variance-based global sensitivity analysis
\cite{Sobol2001, Saltelli2002} to identify which physical parameters
most strongly influence the tap-tone frequency.  Nine input parameters
are varied simultaneously over physically realistic ranges
(Table~\ref{tab:sobol_inputs}), and the Saltelli sampling scheme
generates $N_{\mathrm{base}} = 512$ base samples (total model
evaluations: $N(2D + 2) = 10{,}240$).

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Input parameter ranges for the Sobol sensitivity analysis.
    Ranges span the full variability expected across commercial
    watermelon cultivars and ripeness stages.}
  \label{tab:sobol_inputs}
  \begin{tabular}{llcc}
    \toprule
    Parameter & Symbol & Lower bound & Upper bound \\
    \midrule
    Rind modulus    & $E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ & \SI{10}{\mega\pascal}   & \SI{250}{\mega\pascal} \\
    Rind thickness  & $h$                 & \SI{10}{\milli\metre}   & \SI{25}{\milli\metre}  \\
    Semi-major axis & $a$                 & \SI{0.090}{\metre}      & \SI{0.220}{\metre}     \\
    Semi-minor axis & $c$                 & \SI{0.080}{\metre}      & \SI{0.160}{\metre}     \\
    Rind density    & $\rho_w$            & \SI{1000}{\kilogram\per\metre\cubed} & \SI{1100}{\kilogram\per\metre\cubed} \\
    Flesh density   & $\rho_f$            & \SI{920}{\kilogram\per\metre\cubed}  & \SI{990}{\kilogram\per\metre\cubed}  \\
    Poisson's ratio & $\nu$               & 0.35  & 0.45  \\
    Turgor pressure & $P_{\mathrm{int}}$  & \SI{100}{\pascal} & \SI{800}{\pascal} \\
    Loss tangent    & $\eta$              & 0.05  & 0.25  \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:sobol_results} presents the first-order ($S_1$) and
total-order ($S_T$) Sobol indices.  The rind elastic modulus dominates
the frequency response with $S_1 = 0.49$ and $S_T = 0.54$, confirming
that the rind stiffness---and hence ripeness---is the primary
determinant of the tap-tone frequency.  The semi-major axis~$a$ is the
second most influential parameter ($S_1 = 0.33$, $S_T = 0.38$),
reflecting the strong geometric scaling $f_2 \propto R_{\mathrm{eq}}^{-3/2}$.
Rind thickness ranks third ($S_1 = 0.07$, $S_T = 0.09$), and the
semi-minor axis fourth ($S_1 = 0.05$, $S_T = 0.06$).

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Sobol sensitivity indices for the tap-tone frequency~$f_2$.
    Parameters are ranked by total-order index~$S_T$.  The rind
    modulus~$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ dominates, contributing over half the
    total variance.}
  \label{tab:sobol_results}
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    Parameter & $S_1$ & $S_T$ \\
    \midrule
    $E_{\mathrm{rind}}$     & 0.494 & 0.542 \\
    $a$                     & 0.331 & 0.375 \\
    $h$                     & 0.072 & 0.085 \\
    $c$                     & 0.047 & 0.059 \\
    $\nu$                   & 0.001 & 0.001 \\
    $\rho_f$                & $< 0.001$ & $< 0.001$ \\
    $\rho_w$                & $< 0.001$ & $< 0.001$ \\
    $P_{\mathrm{int}}$      & $< 0.001$ & $< 0.001$ \\
    $\eta$                  & $< 0.001$ & $< 0.001$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The remaining parameters---densities, Poisson's ratio, turgor pressure,
and loss tangent---contribute negligibly ($S_T < 0.001$ in all cases).
This has a critical practical implication: the inversion from $f_2$ to
$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ is robust to uncertainty in parameters that are
difficult to measure \emph{in situ} (e.g.\ flesh density, turgor
pressure).  Only the easily measurable fruit geometry ($a$, $c$, $h$)
introduces significant model sensitivity beyond the target
parameter~$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$.

The gap between $S_1$ and $S_T$ for each parameter is small (typically
$S_T - S_1 < 0.05$), indicating weak parameter interactions.  This
confirms that the model is well approximated by a sum of univariate
contributions, consistent with the near-separable form of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:f2_squared}.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig_sobol_sensitivity}
  \caption{First-order ($S_1$) and total-order ($S_T$) Sobol
    sensitivity indices for the tap-tone frequency~$f_2$.  The rind
    elastic modulus~$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ dominates ($S_T = 0.54$),
    followed by the semi-major axis~$a$ ($S_T = 0.38$).  Densities,
    Poisson's ratio, turgor pressure, and loss tangent are negligible.}
  \label{fig:sobol}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Universal ripeness curve}
\label{sec:universal_curve}

The dimensionless ripeness parameter
$\Pi_{\mathrm{ripe}} = f_2 \, R_{\mathrm{eq}} \sqrt{\rho_{\mathrm{eff}} / E_{\mathrm{rind}}}$
(Eq.~\ref{eq:Pi_ripe}) is evaluated for all four cultivars at each of
the four ripeness stages, yielding 16 data points.
Table~\ref{tab:Pi_ripe} presents the results.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Dimensionless ripeness parameter~$\Pi_{\mathrm{ripe}}$ for
    four cultivars across all ripeness stages.  The near-constant value
    ($\overline{\Pi}_{\mathrm{ripe}} = 0.062$, CoV~$= \SI{3.3}{\percent}$)
    confirms cultivar-independent collapse.}
  \label{tab:Pi_ripe}
  \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
    \toprule
    Cultivar & Unripe & Turning & Ripe & Overripe \\
    \midrule
    Crimson Sweet      & 0.0601 & 0.0601 & 0.0601 & 0.0601 \\
    Sugar Baby         & 0.0655 & 0.0655 & 0.0654 & 0.0655 \\
    Charleston Gray    & 0.0621 & 0.0620 & 0.0620 & 0.0620 \\
    Seedless (Tri-X 313) & 0.0607 & 0.0607 & 0.0607 & 0.0607 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent
A key observation emerges: $\Pi_{\mathrm{ripe}}$ is \emph{invariant
with respect to ripeness stage} for a given cultivar.  This is
a direct consequence of the linear dependence $f_2^2 \propto
E_{\mathrm{rind}}$, which cancels the modulus from the numerator and
denominator of~$\Pi_{\mathrm{ripe}}$.  The residual variation across
cultivars is small: the mean value is
$\overline{\Pi}_{\mathrm{ripe}} = 0.062$ with a coefficient of
variation of~\SI{3.3}{\percent} and a maximum deviation
of~\SI{5.4}{\percent} from the mean (Sugar Baby).  This variation
arises solely from differences in the geometric factor
$\mathcal{G}_2(\nu, \zeta)$ across cultivar aspect ratios.

The practical consequence is that $\Pi_{\mathrm{ripe}}$ serves as a
\emph{universal calibration constant}: once its value is known for a
given cultivar geometry, any measured $f_2$ can be directly mapped to
$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ without detailed knowledge of the flesh properties
or internal pressure.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig_universal_curve}
  \caption{Dimensionless ripeness parameter~$\Pi_{\mathrm{ripe}}$
    for four cultivars at four ripeness stages.  All 16 points fall
    within a narrow band ($\Pi_{\mathrm{ripe}} = 0.062 \pm 0.002$),
    confirming that $\Pi_{\mathrm{ripe}}$ is a universal descriptor
    of the shell--fluid system geometry.}
  \label{fig:universal_curve}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Multi-cultivar comparison}
\label{sec:cultivar}

We compare tap-tone predictions across four commercially important
cultivars evaluated at the canonical ripe modulus
($E_{\mathrm{rind}} = \SI{50}{\mega\pascal}$).
Table~\ref{tab:cultivar} summarises the geometry and predicted
frequencies.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Multi-cultivar comparison of predicted tap-tone
    frequency~$f_2$ at the canonical ripe modulus
    $E_{\mathrm{rind}} = \SI{50}{\mega\pascal}$.  All four cultivars
    use $\nu = 0.40$, $\rho_w = \SI{1050}{\kilogram\per\metre\cubed}$,
    $\rho_f = \SI{950}{\kilogram\per\metre\cubed}$.}
  \label{tab:cultivar}
  \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
    \toprule
    Cultivar & $a$ [m] & $c$ [m] & $h$ [mm] & $R_{\mathrm{eq}}$ [m]
      & $f_2$ [Hz] \\
    \midrule
    Crimson Sweet       & 0.158 & 0.123 & 15 & 0.145 &  89.9 \\
    Sugar Baby          & 0.100 & 0.095 & 12 & 0.098 & 143.4 \\
    Charleston Gray     & 0.200 & 0.110 & 18 & 0.164 &  82.0 \\
    Seedless (Tri-X 313) & 0.140 & 0.120 & 14 & 0.133 &  99.1 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The predicted $f_2$ ranges from \SI{82.0}{\hertz} (Charleston Gray,
the largest cultivar) to \SI{143.4}{\hertz} (Sugar Baby, the smallest),
illustrating the strong geometric scaling.  Despite this factor-of-1.7
spread in absolute frequency, all four cultivars yield
$\Pi_{\mathrm{ripe}}$ values within \SI{5.4}{\percent} of the
mean~$0.062$ (Table~\ref{tab:Pi_ripe}), demonstrating that the
dimensionless parameter effectively normalises out cultivar-specific
geometry.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig_cultivar_comparison}
  \caption{Predicted tap-tone frequencies at canonical ripe modulus
    ($E_{\mathrm{rind}} = \SI{50}{\mega\pascal}$) for four commercial
    cultivars, sorted by~$f_2$.  The inverse relationship between
    equivalent radius~$R_{\mathrm{eq}}$ (grey overlay) and frequency
    demonstrates geometric scaling.  Hatching ensures greyscale
    legibility.}
  \label{fig:cultivar_comparison}
\end{figure}

We also compute the full ripening trajectory for the Crimson Sweet
cultivar by sweeping the rind modulus from
$\SI{250}{\mega\pascal}$ (very unripe) to $\SI{10}{\mega\pascal}$
(very overripe) in 20~stages, simultaneously thinning the rind from
\SI{22}{\milli\metre} to \SI{12}{\milli\metre} and increasing the
loss tangent from~0.06 to~0.22.  The tap-tone frequency decreases
monotonically from \SI{239.3}{\hertz} to \SI{36.4}{\hertz}, spanning
a factor of~6.6.  The optimal ripeness window
($E_{\mathrm{rind}} \approx \SIrange{40}{80}{\mega\pascal}$)
corresponds to $f_2 \approx \SIrange{84}{108}{\hertz}$---a comfortable
range for human auditory discrimination
\cite{Yamamoto1980}.
