\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

We organise the discussion into three tiers of decreasing certainty:
model predictions (Section~\ref{sec:model_predictions}), which follow
directly from the mathematical framework; biological hypotheses
(Section~\ref{sec:bio_hypotheses}), which connect model outputs to
plausible physiological mechanisms; and clinical conjecture
(Section~\ref{sec:clinical_conjecture}), which speculates on
implications that would require experimental validation.


\subsection{Tier~1: Model Predictions}
\label{sec:model_predictions}

The following results are direct, quantitative outputs of the model and
do not depend on biological or clinical interpretation.

\paragraph{U-shaped frequency--volume relationship.}
The competition between geometric softening (increasing radius and wall
thinning) and detrusor strain-stiffening produces a non-monotonic $f_2(V)$
curve with a minimum of \SI{13.5}{\hertz} at \SI{222}{\milli\litre}.  This
is a robust prediction: the analytical condition
$\mathrm{d}(\ln K)/\mathrm{d}V = \mathrm{d}(\ln m)/\mathrm{d}V$
(equation~\ref{eq:f2_min}) provides a necessary and sufficient condition for
the minimum, and the sensitivity analysis (Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity})
confirms that only the wall elastic modulus $E$ can shift the minimum
substantially.  The U-shaped curve is the signature of fill-dependent
resonance and distinguishes the bladder from fixed-geometry resonators.

\paragraph{Coupling disparity.}
Mechanical vibration is $\sim$$6{,}400\times$ more effective than airborne
sound at exciting the bladder's $n = 2$ mode.  This ratio follows directly
from the $(ka)^2$ scaling in the Rayleigh regime and the pelvic
transmissibility chain, and is consistent with the analogous
$\sim$$66{,}000\times$ ratio computed for the abdominal cavity.

\paragraph{Sub-resonant dominance.}
At occupational WBV frequencies (\SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz}), the product of
seat displacement amplitude, pelvic transmissibility, and bladder
sub-resonant amplification produces wall displacements of
$\sim$\SI{160}{\micro\metre}---2--4$\times$ larger than the at-resonance
displacement ($\sim$\SI{47}{\micro\metre}).  The sub-resonant regime is
therefore the practically dominant response mechanism.  This is a
quantitative prediction of the model and does not require any assumption
about biological sensitivity.

\paragraph{Displacement magnitudes.}
The predicted wall displacements (\SIrange{47}{160}{\micro\metre}) are
approximately three orders of magnitude smaller than normal filling
displacements ($\sim$\SI{19}{\milli\metre}).  The equivalent quasi-static
intravesical pressure perturbation from the resonant wall strain is only
$\Delta p \approx E\varepsilon \approx \SI{80}{\pascal}$
($\sim$\SI{0.8}{cmH_2O}), well below the \SIrange{5}{15}{cmH_2O}
threshold at which myelinated A$\delta$ afferents begin firing in response
to slow bladder distension~\cite{Habler1993,Fowler2008}.


\subsection{Tier~2: Biological Hypotheses}
\label{sec:bio_hypotheses}

The following hypotheses connect the model predictions to known
mechanotransduction pathways.  They are plausible but unverified.

\paragraph{Dynamic strain rate.}
The critical distinction between WBV-induced deformation and normal filling
is temporal: WBV delivers its displacement cyclically at
\SIrange{6}{17}{\hertz}, producing peak strain rates of
$\dot{\varepsilon} \approx \SI{0.1}{\per\second}$ at resonance.  This is
approximately 500 times faster than the strain rate during normal filling
($\dot{\varepsilon}_\mathrm{fill} \sim 2 \times 10^{-4}\;\mathrm{s}^{-1}$
for a typical one-hour fill cycle).  We hypothesise that this rate
difference, rather than absolute displacement magnitude, is the relevant
stimulus for mechanosensitive transduction.

\paragraph{PIEZO2 mechanotransduction.}
Recent work has identified PIEZO2 as the principal low-threshold stretch
sensor in the bladder urothelium and sensory
neurons~\cite{Marshall2020}.  PIEZO2 mediates the earliest sensation of
bladder filling and is essential for normal micturition reflexes; loss of
PIEZO2 function produces impaired bladder-filling sensation and voiding
dysfunction in both mice and
humans~\cite{Marshall2020,Dalghi2021}.  Crucially, PIEZO channels
are tuned to rapid membrane deformation---they inactivate under sustained
stretch but respond robustly to dynamic, cyclic
stimuli~\cite{coste2010piezo1}.  The cyclic strain delivered by WBV, though
small in absolute magnitude, falls within the class of stimulus to which
these channels are optimised.  However, whether the WBV-induced strain rate
of $\sim$\SI{0.1}{\per\second} exceeds the PIEZO2 activation threshold
\emph{in vivo} remains unknown: the dynamic thresholds of bladder
mechanoreceptors have not been characterised at infrasonic frequencies.

\paragraph{Silent C-fibre recruitment.}
The unmyelinated C-fibre population in the detrusor is normally quiescent
during physiological filling but can be recruited by pathological
stimuli~\cite{Fowler2008,Habler1993}.  It is conceivable that sustained
cyclic loading could lower the C-fibre activation threshold through temporal
summation, but this hypothesis is speculative and would require
electrophysiological validation.


\subsection{Tier~3: Clinical Conjecture}
\label{sec:clinical_conjecture}

The following scenarios are speculative extensions of the model results
and biological hypotheses.  They are offered as testable predictions, not
established findings.

\paragraph{Urgency in vehicle occupants.}
Professional drivers exposed to broadband WBV at
\SIrange{4}{16}{\hertz} report increased urinary urgency and
frequency~\cite{Seidel1986}.  The model suggests that cyclic bladder wall
deformation of \SIrange{50}{160}{\micro\metre} may occur across this
frequency band at the ISO~2631 action level.  The fill-dependent frequency
minimum (\SI{13.5}{\hertz} at \SI{222}{\milli\litre}) means the bladder
becomes maximally susceptible to near-resonant excitation precisely as it
begins to fill---a ``dynamic vulnerability window.''  \emph{Whether this
displacement is sufficient to trigger urgency through the PIEZO2 pathway
remains to be demonstrated experimentally.}

\paragraph{Pelvic floor weakness.}
Individuals with pre-existing pelvic floor weakness (lower
$\zeta_\mathrm{pelvis}$, higher $T$) would, according to the model, receive
larger displacements at the bladder wall.  This could contribute to symptom
aggravation during vibration exposure, though the model provides no direct
evidence for or against this hypothesis.

\paragraph{Temporal summation.}
The common experience of urgency during prolonged vehicle travel may have a
biomechanical contribution: hours of sustained sub-threshold cyclic loading
could, in principle, sensitise mechanoreceptor pathways through temporal
summation.  This remains entirely conjectural.


\subsection{Comparison with Other Pelvic Organs}
\label{sec:pelvic}

The bladder is not the only pelvic organ exposed to WBV.  The prostate,
uterus, and rectum are all fluid-containing or fluid-adjacent structures
embedded in the pelvic floor.  The present framework could, in principle,
be adapted to any of these organs by substituting appropriate geometric
and material parameters.  The bladder is simply the cleanest example: a
single-chamber, fluid-filled, roughly spherical shell with well-characterised
mechanical properties.


\subsection{Therapeutic Whole-Body Vibration}
\label{sec:therapeutic}

WBV at \SIrange{20}{40}{\hertz} is increasingly used for pelvic floor
rehabilitation in post-prostatectomy and postpartum
patients~\cite{Pelvic2019}.  The bio\-electrical activity of pelvic floor
muscles increases during synchronous WBV~\cite{Lauper2009}, and clinical
trials report improvements in continence
symptoms~\cite{NCT03325660}.  The model predicts that these therapeutic
frequencies (\SIrange{20}{40}{\hertz}) lie above the bladder's $n = 2$ mode
for most fill states, placing the bladder in a supra-resonant regime where
wall displacement decreases with frequency.  If the model is correct, the
therapeutic effect would be more likely mediated by pelvic floor muscle
activation than by direct bladder wall resonance.


\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several simplifications warrant discussion.

\paragraph{Spherical geometry.}
The real bladder is slightly oblate and irregularly shaped, particularly at
low fill states.  A more realistic geometry would modify the mode shapes and
could split the degenerate frequencies of the spherical model.  However, at
moderate-to-high fill the spherical approximation is reasonable, and the
errors are of order $(1 - c/a)^2$ for the eigenfrequencies.

\paragraph{Pelvic constraint.}
Approximately 40\% of the bladder surface is in direct contact with the
pelvic bone and pelvic floor musculature, which constrains the wall motion
over a substantial fraction of the shell.  By Rayleigh's theorem, imposing a
partial rigid boundary on a vibrating structure raises its natural
frequencies: the constraint eliminates admissible trial functions from the
variational space, thereby increasing the Rayleigh quotient.  The free-shell
model therefore provides a \emph{lower} bound on the resonant frequencies.

We can estimate the magnitude of this effect.  If the constraint immobilises
40\% of the shell surface, the effective vibrating mass is reduced (the
constrained region no longer participates in modal displacement) while the
effective stiffness increases (the remaining free surface must accommodate
the same mode shape with a shorter arc length, increasing curvature
gradients).  For the $n = 2$ mode, these combined effects would raise the
natural frequency by an estimated 15--25\%, shifting the predicted range from
\SIrange{13.5}{17.1}{\hertz} to approximately \SIrange{15.5}{21.4}{\hertz}.  The mode
shapes would also be modified: instead of the symmetric quadrupolar
deformation of a free sphere, the constrained bladder would exhibit
asymmetric modes concentrated on the free (superior) dome, with nodal lines
forced to lie near the constraint boundary.

This frequency shift reinforces rather than undermines the clinical
relevance of the model.  The upward shift moves the predicted resonance
further from the ISO~2631 peak (\SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz}), deepening the
sub-resonant character of the bladder's response under occupational WBV.
However, the shifted range (\SIrange{16}{21}{\hertz}) overlaps with
frequencies produced by vibratory industrial equipment and rough-road
driving.  The free-shell predictions should be interpreted as conservative
(lower-bound) estimates; the true in-vivo frequencies are likely somewhat
higher.

\paragraph{Nonlinear elasticity.}
The exponential strain-stiffening model (equation~\ref{eq:E_fill}) is a
phenomenological fit to vibrometry data.  A hyperelastic constitutive model
(Mooney--Rivlin or Ogden) would be more physically appropriate for large
deformations, though for the small-amplitude vibrations considered here,
a linearised modulus is adequate.

\paragraph{Layered wall structure.}
The bladder wall comprises distinct layers (mucosa, submucosa, detrusor
muscle, serosa) with different mechanical properties.  The present model
uses a single effective modulus.  A multilayer shell model would capture
the bending--stretching coupling between layers, potentially modifying
both frequencies and damping.

\paragraph{Surrounding tissue.}
The bladder is embedded in the pelvic cavity, surrounded by fat, connective
tissue, and adjacent organs.  This surrounding medium would add both
damping and mass loading, likely shifting the resonant frequencies downward
and broadening the peaks.  The free-shell model therefore represents a
lower bound on frequencies with respect to boundary constraints (pelvic
contact raises frequencies) but an upper bound with respect to added-mass
effects from surrounding tissue (which would lower them).  The two effects
act in opposite directions, partially cancelling.
