\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Flexural Mode Frequencies Across Fill Volume}
\label{sec:freq_vs_volume}

Figure~\ref{fig:freq_vs_volume} presents the $n = 2$ and $n = 3$ flexural
mode frequencies across the physiological fill range
(\SIrange{50}{500}{\milli\litre}).  The $n = 2$ frequency follows a
distinctive U-shaped curve, with a minimum of approximately \SI{12}{\hertz}
near \SI{170}{\milli\litre} and higher values at both extremes of the fill
range.

The physical mechanism behind this non-monotonic behaviour is a competition
between two opposing effects:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Geometric softening.}  As the bladder fills, the radius
    increases (equation~\ref{eq:radius}), which lowers the natural frequency
    through the $R^{-4}$ and $R^{-2}$ dependence in the bending and membrane
    stiffness terms (equations~\ref{eq:Kbend}--\ref{eq:Kmemb}).  The wall
    also thins (equation~\ref{eq:h_solve}), further reducing both bending
    rigidity ($D \propto h^3$) and membrane stiffness ($\propto h$).
  \item \textbf{Strain-stiffening.}  The elastic modulus of the detrusor
    muscle increases exponentially with fill volume
    (equation~\ref{eq:E_fill}), from \SI{10}{\kilo\pascal} at
    \SI{50}{\milli\litre} to \SI{200}{\kilo\pascal} at
    \SI{500}{\milli\litre}.  This twenty-fold increase eventually dominates
    the geometric effects.
\end{enumerate}
At low fill volumes the geometric effect dominates and the frequency
falls; at high fill volumes the strain-stiffening wins and the frequency
rises.  The crossover produces the minimum near \SI{170}{\milli\litre}---a
fill state corresponding to the early ``need to go'' sensation in most
individuals.

\subsection{Coupling Pathways}
\label{sec:coupling_results}

Figure~\ref{fig:coupling} compares the airborne and mechanical coupling
pathways to the bladder at a representative fill of \SI{300}{\milli\litre}
($f_2 = \SI{13.4}{\hertz}$).  Three key observations emerge.

First, the airborne acoustic coupling scales as $(ka)^2$ and is vanishingly
small across the entire frequency range of interest: at \SI{13.4}{\hertz},
$(ka)^2 \approx 1.0 \times 10^{-4}$.  The bladder, like the abdominal
cavity, is deep in the Rayleigh regime at all physiologically relevant
frequencies.

Second, the mechanical coupling---the product of seat-to-pelvis
transmissibility and bladder modal amplification---peaks near the bladder
resonance frequency.  Even though pelvic transmissibility is attenuated
at \SI{13.4}{\hertz} ($T \approx 0.31$), the bladder modal amplification
($Q = 2.5$) partially compensates.

Third, the coupling ratio $\mathcal{R} \approx 7{,}600$ at $f_2$
(equation~\ref{eq:R_coupling}) confirms that mechanical vibration is the
dominant excitation pathway by nearly four orders of magnitude.  This is
quantitatively consistent with---though somewhat smaller than---the
$6.6 \times 10^4$ ratio found for the abdominal
cavity~\cite{Mace2025browntone}, the difference arising primarily from
the higher $f_2$ (and hence smaller $Q$ contribution from pelvic
transmissibility) of the bladder.

\subsection{Comparison with the Abdominal Cavity}
\label{sec:comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:comparison} compares key quantities between the bladder and
abdominal cavity models.  The bladder is smaller, softer, and more heavily
damped, yet the two organs share the same qualitative physics: fluid-dominated
breathing modes at kilohertz frequencies, low-frequency flexural modes
governed by the competition between shell stiffness and fluid added mass, and
a severe airborne coupling penalty in the Rayleigh regime.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Comparison of bladder and abdominal cavity models.}
  \label{tab:comparison}
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    Quantity & Bladder (300~mL) & Abdomen \\
    \midrule
    Equivalent radius & \SI{4.2}{\centi\metre} & \SI{15.7}{\centi\metre} \\
    Wall thickness & \SI{1.8}{\milli\metre} & \SI{10}{\milli\metre} \\
    Elastic modulus & \SI{53}{\kilo\pascal} & \SI{100}{\kilo\pascal} \\
    Poisson's ratio & 0.49 & 0.45 \\
    Loss tangent & 0.40 & 0.25 \\
    Quality factor $Q$ & 2.5 & 4.0 \\
    Breathing mode $f_0$ & $\sim$\SI{9500}{\hertz} & $\sim$\SI{2500}{\hertz} \\
    Flexural $f_2$ & \SI{13.4}{\hertz} & \SI{4.0}{\hertz} \\

    Coupling ratio $\mathcal{R}$ & $\sim$7,600 & $\sim$66,000 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

The principal quantitative differences are the higher flexural frequency of
the bladder ($f_2 = \SI{13.4}{\hertz}$ vs \SI{4.0}{\hertz}) and the smaller
coupling ratio ($\sim$7,600 vs $\sim$66,000).  The higher $f_2$ is a direct
consequence of the smaller radius: the stiffness-to-mass ratio scales
roughly as $1/R^2$, and the bladder is approximately four times smaller
than the abdomen.  The smaller coupling ratio reflects the fact that the
bladder's $f_2$ sits above the pelvic transmissibility peak, so the
mechanical pathway receives less amplification from the body's own
resonance structure.
