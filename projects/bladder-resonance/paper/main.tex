\begin{highlights}
\item Bladder $n{=}2$ flexural mode at 12--18~Hz across physiological fill states
\item Frequency minimum ($\sim$12~Hz) at 150--200~mL due to competing geometry and stiffening
\item Mechanical (WBV) coupling exceeds airborne acoustic coupling by $\sim$7,600$\times$
\item Same analytical framework as abdominal cavity model, with fill-dependent parameters
\item Results consistent with occupational reports of vibration-induced urinary urgency
\end{highlights}

\begin{abstract}
Occupational exposure to whole-body vibration (WBV) is associated with
increased urinary urgency among vehicle operators, yet no first-principles
model of bladder resonance exists to explain this observation.  We model the
human urinary bladder as a fluid-filled viscoelastic spherical shell whose
geometry, wall stiffness, and internal pressure vary with fill volume
(\SIrange{50}{500}{\milli\litre}).  The framework---an adaptation of an oblate
spheroidal shell model developed for the abdominal cavity---predicts that the
fundamental flexural mode ($n = 2$) lies between \SI{12}{\hertz} and
\SI{18}{\hertz}, with a minimum near \SI{12}{\hertz} at approximately
\SI{170}{\milli\litre}.  This minimum arises from the competition between
increasing radius (which lowers frequency) and strain-stiffening of the
detrusor muscle (which raises it).  Coupling analysis shows that mechanical
vibration transmitted through the pelvis is approximately $7{,}600$ times
more effective than airborne sound at exciting bladder wall modes, consistent
with the dominance of the WBV pathway reported in the epidemiological
literature.  The predicted frequency range sits above the ISO~2631 peak
pelvic transmissibility band (\SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz}) but within the broader
WBV-affected spectrum, suggesting a forced sub-resonant response mechanism
rather than direct resonance.
\end{abstract}
