\begin{highlights}
\item Bladder $n{=}2$ frequency follows a U-shaped curve: minimum \SI{13.5}{\hertz} at \SI{222}{\milli\litre}
\item Minimum arises from competition between geometric softening and detrusor strain-stiffening
\item Sub-resonant forced response at \SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} produces larger wall displacements than resonance
\item Mechanical (WBV) coupling exceeds airborne acoustic coupling by $\sim$6,400$\times$
\item Same analytical framework as abdominal cavity model, with fill-dependent parameters
\end{highlights}

\begin{abstract}
We model the human urinary bladder as a fluid-filled viscoelastic spherical
shell whose geometry, wall stiffness, and internal pressure vary with fill
volume (\SIrange{50}{500}{\milli\litre}).  The fundamental flexural mode
($n = 2$) follows a distinctive U-shaped frequency--volume curve: as the
bladder fills, increasing radius and wall thinning lower the frequency
(geometric softening), while exponential strain-stiffening of the detrusor
muscle raises it.  These competing effects produce a minimum of
\SI{13.5}{\hertz} at \SI{222}{\milli\litre}---a fill state corresponding
to early urge sensation---with higher frequencies at both extremes of the
physiological range (\SIrange{13.5}{17.1}{\hertz} for fills above
\SI{150}{\milli\litre}).  Because the predicted resonance lies above the
ISO~2631 peak pelvic transmissibility band (\SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz}), the
bladder responds to occupational whole-body vibration (WBV) primarily as a
forced sub-resonant oscillator.  At \SI{6}{\hertz}, where pelvic
amplification is maximal, the sub-resonant wall displacement
($\sim$\SI{160}{\micro\metre}) exceeds the at-resonance displacement
($\sim$\SI{47}{\micro\metre}) because the larger pelvic transmissibility
more than compensates for the reduced modal amplification.  Coupling analysis
confirms that mechanical vibration transmitted through the pelvis is
approximately $6{,}400$ times more effective than airborne sound at exciting
bladder wall modes, consistent with the epidemiological dominance of the
WBV pathway.  These results provide a first-principles physical basis for
the fill-dependent resonance behaviour of the bladder wall and identify
the sub-resonant forced response as the regime most relevant to occupational
WBV exposure.
\end{abstract}
