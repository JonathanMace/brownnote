# Suggested Reviewers — Paper 9: Lifting Theorem

The following reviewers are suggested for their expertise in inverse problems,
spectral methods, shell dynamics, and fluid–structure interaction. All are
independent of Microsoft Research and the University of Auckland.

## 1. John E. Mottershead

- **Affiliation:** Department of Mechanical, Materials and Aerospace
  Engineering, University of Liverpool, UK
- **Expertise:** Structural dynamics model updating, inverse methods,
  parameter identification from vibration data
- **Why a good fit:** Professor Mottershead's extensive work on finite
  element model updating and sensitivity-based identification methods is
  directly relevant to the Jacobian-based identifiability analysis in this
  paper. His understanding of conditioning and regularisation in structural
  inverse problems makes him an ideal reviewer.
- **Source:** https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=xVJg7vEAAAAJ

## 2. Marco Amabili

- **Affiliation:** Department of Mechanical Engineering, McGill University,
  Canada
- **Expertise:** Shell vibrations, nonlinear dynamics, fluid–structure
  interaction
- **Why a good fit:** Professor Amabili is a leading authority on the
  vibration of shells and plates, including fluid-loaded configurations. His
  work on the nonlinear dynamics of shells and experimental validation
  provides the structural acoustics context needed to evaluate the oblate
  versus prolate distinction central to this paper.
- **Source:** https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=hPGImhEAAAAJ

## 3. Michael I. Friswell

- **Affiliation:** Faculty of Science and Engineering, Swansea University, UK
- **Expertise:** Finite element model updating, structural health monitoring,
  inverse problems in structural dynamics
- **Why a good fit:** Professor Friswell's foundational contributions to
  model updating and damage identification from spectral data are closely
  aligned with the identifiability analysis here. His work on sensitivity
  methods and the role of parameterisation in inverse problems is particularly
  relevant.
- **Source:** https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=O5xKMUkAAAAJ

## 4. Maarten V. de Hoop

- **Affiliation:** Department of Computational and Applied Mathematics,
  Rice University, USA
- **Expertise:** Inverse problems, spectral theory, microlocal analysis,
  geophysical imaging
- **Why a good fit:** Professor de Hoop's work on inverse spectral problems
  and the recovery of material properties from eigenvalue data connects
  directly to the Kac-type identifiability question posed in this paper.
  His mathematical rigour would strengthen the review of the near-sphere
  asymptotic analysis.
- **Source:** https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=Ih7jbhAAAAAJ

## 5. Francesco Pellicano

- **Affiliation:** Department of Engineering "Enzo Ferrari", University of
  Modena and Reggio Emilia, Italy
- **Expertise:** Nonlinear shell dynamics, vibrations of cylindrical and
  spheroidal shells, fluid-filled structures
- **Why a good fit:** Professor Pellicano has published extensively on the
  vibration of fluid-filled shells and cylindrical structures, making him
  well placed to assess the physical plausibility of the oblate–prolate
  asymmetry and the Ritz model formulation used here.
- **Source:** https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=DYxTC2kAAAAJ

## 6. Alexander N. Norris

- **Affiliation:** Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering,
  Rutgers University, USA
- **Expertise:** Structural acoustics, elastic wave theory, inverse
  scattering, cloaking
- **Why a good fit:** Professor Norris's work on acoustic scattering from
  elastic shells and inverse problems in structural acoustics provides the
  theoretical foundations that underpin this paper. His expertise in the
  interplay between geometry and wave behaviour is directly relevant to the
  curvature-dependent identifiability mechanism discussed here.
- **Source:** https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=rqJRdasAAAAJ
