\section{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:conditioning-summary} collects the conditioning results that
matter for the present argument.  The first point is a baseline: the
equivalent-sphere model is singular because it collapses two geometric degrees
of freedom into the single radius $R_\mathrm{eq}$.  Its reported numerical value
$\kappa_\mathrm{sphere}\approx\num{1.37e10}$ is therefore not a meaningful
finite condition number; it is the floating-point shadow of an exact rank
defect \cite{BrowntoneP8}.

The second point is the genuine lifting result.  Within the \emph{same} Ritz
model, moving from the near-spherical oblate limit to the canonical oblate
geometry improves conditioning from
$\kappa_\mathrm{floor}\approx\num{269}$ to
$\kappa_\mathrm{oblate}=\num{69.4}$.  That is a reduction by a factor of about
\num{3.9}, achieved without changing the inverse variables or the measured mode
set.  It is therefore a geometric effect rather than a bookkeeping effect.

The third point is the asymmetry.  A prolate continuation does not show a
comparable gain.  Across the informative part of the prolate branch, the
condition number remains in the range
$\kappa_\mathrm{prolate}=\numrange{561}{729}$, substantially worse than both
the oblate floor and the canonical oblate point.  In plain terms, oblate
lifting exists, but there is no matching prolate improvement waiting politely on
the other side of the sphere.

The singular-value fit in \eqref{eq:sigma-min-expansion} explains why the
near-spherical oblate branch has a floor rather than a divergence.  Since
$\sigma_0>0$, the smallest singular value does not collapse to zero as
$\varepsilon\to 0$ within the Ritz model.  Since
$\varepsilon_c=\num{1.48}>1$, the intercept remains important over the physical
oblate range, so the conditioning is bounded before it improves.  The canonical
oblate geometry then pushes $\sigma_{\min}$ far enough upward to reach
$\kappa_\mathrm{oblate}=\num{69.4}$.

The practical meaning is straightforward.  The second flexural mode
$f_2=\SI{3.95}{\hertz}$ already lies in the low-frequency band used by the
companion studies, and the higher modes in the set respond differently to the
same curvature perturbation.  That differential response separates the
sensitivity directions for $a$, $c$, and $E$ in the oblate family.  The
prolate family does not deliver the same separation, so ``non-spherical'' is
not, by itself, a useful identifiability category.
