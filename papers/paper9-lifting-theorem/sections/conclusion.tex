\section{Conclusion}

Within the five-mode spectral inverse problem for a fluid-filled shell, the
equivalent-sphere reduction is singular ($\sigma_3=0$), and this rank deficiency
persists at the exact sphere because spherical symmetry forces the geometric
Jacobian columns to be proportional.  The positive $\sigma_3$ returned by a
finite Ritz basis at $a=c$ is a discretisation artefact that grows with basis
size.  The correct near-sphere expansion is
$\sigma_3(\varepsilon)=\lambda_1\varepsilon^2+O(\varepsilon^4)$, so the oblate
branch increases $\sigma_3$ quadratically and reaches
$\kappa_\mathrm{oblate}=\num{69.4}$ at the canonical geometry.  A prolate
continuation at $c/a=\num{1.5}$ gives $\kappa_\mathrm{prolate}=156$, with
$\kappa_\mathrm{prolate}$ remaining in the range 156--244 across the tested
prolate branch and showing no systematic improvement with increasing
asphericity.

The conclusion is therefore specific rather than universal: identifiability in
this spectral inverse problem is geometry-dependent.  Oblate curvature
redistribution lifts the weak inverse direction; prolate distortion does not
deliver the same improvement.
