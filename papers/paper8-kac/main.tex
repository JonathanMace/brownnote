\begin{abstract}
We investigate the practical identifiability of material and geometric
parameters of a fluid-filled elastic oblate spheroidal shell from its
resonant frequencies --- the inverse counterpart of Kac's celebrated question
``Can one hear the shape of a drum?'' applied to vibroacoustic organ models.
Using an oblate Ritz flexural forward model --- with the equivalent-sphere
reduction as a degenerate comparator --- we compute the scaled
Jacobian of the frequency--parameter map, its singular values, its condition
number, and the associated Fisher information matrix for the parameter triple
$(a,c,E)$.  The smallest singular value of the scaled Jacobian admits a
\emph{two-channel} regular expansion
$\sigma_{\min}(\varepsilon) \approx \sigma_0 + \lambda_1\varepsilon^2 +
\lambda_2\varepsilon^4$, where $\sigma_0$, $\lambda_1$, $\lambda_2$ are
regression coefficients (not asymptotic limits) obtained over the
intermediate eccentricity range $\varepsilon\in[0.15,\,0.85]$.  The
condition number approaches a finite floor
$\kappa_\mathrm{floor} \approx 269$ (at $\varepsilon = 0.01$, five modes)
rather than diverging, and drops to $\kappa = 69.4$ at the canonical
oblate operating point, an improvement by a factor of approximately $3.9$.
For comparison, the equivalent-sphere model gives
$\kappa$ numerically singular ($\kappa > 10^{10}$ in finite-difference
evaluation with step $\delta = 10^{-6}|\theta_j|$), reflecting algebraic rank deficiency rather
than geometric conditioning.  A Newton-type inverse solver recovers
$(a,c,E)$ to relative errors below $10^{-12}$ in round-trip tests, while
Cram\'er--Rao bounds at $1\%$ frequency noise yield uncertainties of
$\pm 22.5\%$ in $a$, $\pm 6.0\%$ in $c$, and $\pm 30.8\%$ in $E$ (five
modes).  The two-channel framework leads to a three-class classification of
shell inverse problems: \emph{singular} (equivalent-sphere, rank-deficient),
\emph{regular bounded} (oblate Ritz, $\kappa$ approaches a finite floor and
generally improves with eccentricity, exhibiting local non-monotonicity from
mode crossings), and \emph{non-lifting} (prolate,
$\kappa$ flat and high).  The oblate--prolate asymmetry arises from
curvature--mode anti-correlation: in oblate shells, peak curvature and modal
peaks occupy different latitudes, diversifying the Jacobian columns; in
prolate shells, both concentrate at the poles, leaving the columns nearly
proportional.  Within the present Ritz model and local identifiability framework,
one can ``hear the shape'' of an oblate organ, but not a
spherical or prolate one.
\end{abstract}
