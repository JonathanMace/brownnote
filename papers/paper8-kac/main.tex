\begin{abstract}
We investigate the practical identifiability of material and geometric
parameters of a fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shell from its
resonant frequencies --- the inverse counterpart of Kac's celebrated question
``Can one hear the shape of a drum?'' applied to vibroacoustic organ models.
Using an equivalent-sphere flexural forward model, we compute the scaled
Jacobian of the frequency--parameter map, its singular values, its condition
number, and the associated Fisher information matrix for the parameter triple
$(a,c,E)$.  At the spherical operating point the scaled Jacobian is
effectively rank deficient (condition number $\kappa = 1.37 \times 10^{10}$);
at the canonical oblate operating point the same analysis gives
$\kappa = 69.4$, an improvement by a factor of $2.0 \times 10^{8}$.  A
Newton-type inverse solver recovers $(a,c,E)$ to relative errors below
$10^{-12}$ in round-trip tests, while Cram\'er--Rao bounds at $1\%$ frequency
noise yield uncertainties of $\pm 22.5\%$ in $a$, $\pm 6.0\%$ in $c$, and
$\pm 30.8\%$ in $E$.  Five-mode inversion is robust; three-mode inversion is
vulnerable to local minima.  The result has implications for non-invasive
tissue characterisation and fruit quality assessment: one can ``hear the
shape'' of an oblate organ, but not a spherical one.
\end{abstract}
