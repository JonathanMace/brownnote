\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Connection to Kac's question}

Kac's original problem asked whether one could infer geometry from a spectrum.
Our answer is deliberately narrower and more practical.  For the present
elastic shell model, the results suggest that, within the present Ritz model,
one \emph{can} hear the shape
of an oblate organ, in the sense that the local inverse problem for
$(a,c,E)$ is well conditioned and statistically finite.  At the spherical
limit, one cannot: the Jacobian becomes rank deficient and the Fisher matrix
loses invertibility.

The analogy with Kac should therefore be handled with care.  We do not prove a
global uniqueness theorem of the kind established by Sylvester and
Uhlmann~\cite{SylvesterUhlmann1987} for the Calder\'on inverse conductivity
problem, nor do we exclude the possibility of remote isospectral parameter sets.
Our claim is about \emph{local practical identifiability} --- whether, at a
given operating point, finite noisy measurements contain enough independent
information to constrain the parameters to useful precision.  That is a less
grand claim, but also the one an experimentalist can actually use.

\subsection{Mechanism: mode-dependent curvature sampling}

The analytical results in Section~\ref{sec:theory} clarify why oblate geometry
restores identifiability.  Proposition~\ref{prop:chain_rule} shows that \emph{any}
model built on $R_\mathrm{eq} = (a^2 c)^{1/3}$ alone has a rank-deficient
Jacobian, because the chain rule forces the $a$- and $c$-columns to be exactly
proportional for all modes.  Remark~\ref{prop:eccentricity} then shows
that the Ritz model, which integrates strain energy over the actual spheroidal
surface, introduces mode-dependent corrections at order $\varepsilon^2$.

The mechanism is \emph{mode-dependent curvature sampling}.  Different Legendre
modes $P_n(\eta)$ weight the meridional curvature gradient differently: low-order
modes sample both poles and equator broadly, while higher-order modes
concentrate energy near the equator where the curvature gradient is steepest.
This differential sampling breaks the proportionality of the $a$- and $c$-columns
and restores full rank.  The smallest singular value is well described by the polynomial
fit~\eqref{eq:sigma_min_expansion}, whose regression intercept
$\sigma_0 > 0$ reflects the presence of a finite floor.  Because the
crossover eccentricity $\varepsilon_c = 1.48$ lies outside the physical domain,
the intercept dominates at all realisable eccentricities, and the
condition number approaches $\kappa_\mathrm{floor}\approx 269$ (directly
computed at $\varepsilon = 0.01$) rather than diverging.

Figure~\ref{fig:oblate_prolate_comparison} sharpens that picture by contrasting
oblate and prolate sweeps computed with the same five-mode set
$\mathcal{N}=\{2,3,4,5,6\}$.  The oblate branch exhibits bounded but genuine
identifiability restoration: $\kappa$ falls from the near-spherical floor to
$\kappa=69.4$ at the canonical operating point $\varepsilon=0.745$, while
$\sigma_{\min}$ grows above the regression intercept
$\sigma_0\approx0.011$.  The prolate branch does not show the same lifting.
Over its informative mid-range, $\kappa$ remains in the range
$5.6\times10^2$--$7.3\times10^2$ and $\sigma_{\min}$ stays close to zero.  The
lesson is therefore not that \emph{any} departure from spherical symmetry is
helpful, but that oblate curvature redistribution creates a usable shape
channel whereas prolate distortion, at least in the present model class, does
not.

This interpretation is straightforward and does not invoke group-theoretic
symmetry breaking.  The identifiability transition is not a consequence of
reducing the symmetry group from $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ to $\mathrm{SO}(2)$; it is a
consequence of mode-dependent curvature weighting in the variational
integrals.  The distinction matters because the curvature-sampling explanation
is constructive --- it identifies the specific integrals responsible --- whereas
a symmetry-based framing would be descriptive at best and misleading at worst.

\subsection{Implications for vibroacoustic elastography}

The results are encouraging for vibroacoustic elastography and related spectral
methods.  A small number of low-order flexural resonances may contain enough
information to estimate both geometry and stiffness, provided that the operating
point is sufficiently aspherical and provided that enough modes are measured.
This is attractive because resonant frequencies are often easier to estimate
robustly than full-field mode shapes or transient wavefields.

The Cram\'er--Rao bounds give the result practical content.  At $1\%$ frequency
noise, the uncertainty in $c$ is modest, while $a$ and $E$ remain more weakly
resolved but still finite.  That anisotropy matters for experimental design:
one should not expect the same confidence in all parameters, even when the
Jacobian has full rank.

\subsection{Why model choice matters}

The most important modelling lesson is that forward-model convenience and
inverse-model usefulness are not the same thing.  A spherical reduction may be
acceptable for a rough forward estimate of resonance frequency, but it is a poor
foundation for parameter recovery.  Spherical simplifications are serviceable on
the blackboard; in inverse problems they become noticeably less cooperative.

The present study uses the oblate Ritz model for all identifiability results.
The equivalent-sphere model is retained only to illustrate the algebraic rank
deficiency of Proposition~\ref{prop:chain_rule}.  The Ritz model is itself an
approximation --- it truncates the trial function space and incurs $\sim\!8\%$
error relative to the analytical sphere formula --- but the rank restoration
follows from the $n$-dependence of the curvature integrals rather than from
the approximation error itself.  A full finite-element oblate shell
model would provide the definitive answer, but the Ritz results already
demonstrate the essential geometric mechanism.

\subsection{Relation to existing inverse methods}

Inverse vibration problems have long been studied in structural
dynamics.  Mottershead and Friswell~\cite{MottersheadFriswell1993} surveyed
finite element model updating techniques, in which measured natural
frequencies and mode shapes are used to correct uncertain structural
parameters --- a problem structurally similar to the present one, though
typically applied to metal structures with well-separated modes rather than
soft-tissue shells in the infrasonic band.  Their
textbook~\cite{FriswellMottershead1995} developed the systematic framework
for sensitivity-based updating, of which the Jacobian analysis presented
here is a natural extension.  Gladwell~\cite{Gladwell2004,Gladwell2005}
established the mathematical foundations of inverse vibration, and
model-based elastography~\cite{Doyley2012} has extended similar ideas to
soft-tissue characterisation.  In acoustics, Aernouts et
al.~\cite{Aernouts2012} demonstrated inverse estimation of tympanic membrane
material properties by fitting a dynamic response model to spectral data ---
a problem structurally analogous to the present one, though in a different
organ system.

The present contribution focuses on a preliminary but
useful step: before attempting a sophisticated inversion, we ask whether the
chosen measurements can, in principle, resolve the chosen parameters with
acceptable conditioning.  That is a question of local practical
identifiability rather than of algorithmic inversion.

That perspective is valuable because poor inverse performance is often blamed on
optimisers, priors, or noise models when the underlying issue is simpler: the
Jacobian is nearly singular.  A condition-number and Fisher-information analysis
cannot solve the inverse problem by itself, but it can prevent one from asking
the data for information they do not contain.

\subsection{Local versus global identifiability}

The round-trip tests demonstrate very accurate local recovery, but global
identifiability is a separate matter.  The three-mode system is especially
vulnerable because it is exactly determined.  Local minima can occur, and a
small residual does not necessarily imply the correct parameter set when the
search is started far away.  This is why the five-mode result matters more for
practice than the machine-precision local recovery.

Whether the spectral map is globally injective --- that is, whether the oblate
shell inverse problem admits a unique solution over the full parameter domain
--- remains an open question connecting to the spectral geometry programme
initiated by Kac~\cite{Kac1966} and the perturbation theory of
Kato~\cite{Kato1966}.  The eigenvalue perturbation results of
Hald~\cite{Hald1978,Hald1984CPAM} and the shape optimisation framework of
Henrot~\cite{Henrot2006} provide relevant tools, but a proof of global
uniqueness for the present coupled fluid--shell system would require substantial
additional analysis.

Our recommendation is therefore not based on elegance but on behaviour:
\begin{itemize}
  \item use three modes only for local studies or controlled round-trip tests;
  \item use at least five flexural modes for robust inversion in realistic
  settings.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations remain.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The Ritz forward model is a variational approximation, not a full
  spheroidal shell solution.  It captures the leading eccentricity dependence
  but incurs $\sim\!8\%$ error relative to the analytical sphere formula.  Whether the
  rank restoration persists in a converged finite-element model remains to be
  confirmed.
  \item The analysis is local.  We do not prove global injectivity of the map
  $(a,c,E)\mapsto \{f_n\}$.
  \item Synthetic inversion uses the same model for data generation and
  inversion.  This isolates identifiability from model-form error, but it
  means that the round-trip recovery (Section~\ref{sec:results}) is a
  verification of internal consistency, not a demonstration of real-world
  inversion capability.
  \item The mode numbers are assumed known.  In experiment, mode assignment may
  itself be uncertain.
  \item Only three parameters are estimated.  Allowing $h$, $\nu$, or fluid
  properties to vary would enlarge the parameter space and may degrade
  conditioning.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future directions}

The natural next step is a full finite-element oblate shell model, which would
reduce the Ritz truncation error observed in comparison with the analytical
sphere formula and provide convergence towards the exact spheroidal solution.  Shape-calculus tools~\cite{DelfourZolesio2011} would allow
Hadamard-type Fr\'echet derivatives of the eigenvalues with respect to the
domain boundary, enabling a geometry-aware sensitivity analysis that goes beyond
the parametric approach taken here.  That would show whether the encouraging
results reported here strengthen further when the geometry is represented
exactly.  The second
priority is experimental validation, ideally first on phantoms of known
geometry and stiffness and then on biological specimens.  A Bayesian treatment,
with priors on tissue properties and uncertainty in mode identification, would
also be a useful extension.

The results suggest that geometric asymmetry plays a fundamental role in
practical identifiability of coupled fluid--shell systems, a connection that
merits further investigation using the tools of shape calculus and spectral
perturbation theory~\cite{Kato1966,Henrot2006}.

\subsection{Classification of shell inverse problems}\label{sec:classification}

The two-channel expansion~\eqref{eq:sigma_min_expansion} and the
oblate--prolate comparison together suggest three regimes of identifiability
behaviour for spectral inverse problems on fluid-filled shells.  We present
these as observed patterns in the numerics rather than as a definitive
taxonomy; the classification may evolve as more shell geometries and model
classes are examined.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Singular} (Jacobian rank-deficient).  The equivalent-sphere
  model, which depends on $(a,c)$ only through $R_\mathrm{eq}$, has a
  formally infinite condition number by Proposition~\ref{prop:chain_rule}
  (evaluated numerically as $\kappa \approx 1.37 \times 10^{10}$ with
  finite-difference step $\delta = 10^{-6}|\theta_j|$).  This is the
  degenerate case: all frequency
  sensitivities to $a$ and $c$ are proportional, leaving one parameter
  combination unobservable.

  \item \textbf{Regular bounded} ($\sigma_0 > 0$, $\kappa$ finite and
  generally favourable).  The oblate Ritz model has a positive regression
  intercept $\sigma_0 > 0$ in the polynomial fit~\eqref{eq:sigma_min_expansion},
  with direct evaluation at $\varepsilon = 0.01$ giving
  $\sigma_{\min} = 0.00705$ and
  $\kappa_\mathrm{floor} \approx 269$ (five modes).  At the canonical oblate operating point
  the condition number generally improves to $\kappa = 69.4$
  --- a factor of $3.9$.  The improvement is generally favourable but not
  uniform: local variability attributable to mode crossings in the Ritz eigenvalue
  branches produces isolated spikes in $\kappa$ at intermediate eccentricities.
  Because $\varepsilon_c = 1.48 > 1$, the intercept
  dominates everywhere.  The inverse problem is well posed at all
  physically realisable eccentricities, though the practitioner should be aware
  that the condition number may vary non-smoothly along the oblate branch.

  \item \textbf{Non-lifting} ($\kappa$ high and essentially flat).  For
  prolate geometries ($c > a$), the condition number remains in the range
  $580$--$730$ with no systematic improvement as eccentricity increases.
  The inverse problem is not rank-deficient, but it is poorly conditioned
  and does not appear to benefit from departure from the sphere within the
  present model and parameter range.
\end{enumerate}

The distinction between regimes~2 and~3 is the central new observation.
It replaces an earlier conjecture~\cite[Conjecture~1, first
draft]{BrowntoneCompanion} that proposed a power-law divergence
$\kappa \sim C\varepsilon^{-\alpha}$ as $\varepsilon \to 0$.  That
conjecture was abandoned because the Ritz model has $\sigma_0 > 0$ at the
sphere: the condition number approaches a finite floor, not infinity.  The
two-channel expansion provides a richer and more accurate description.

Whether these three regimes persist in more general shell theories (e.g.\
higher-order Ritz bases, full finite-element models, or non-axisymmetric
geometries) remains to be established.  We therefore present them as
empirical observations from the present model class, not as universal
categories.

\subsection{Oblate--prolate asymmetry}\label{sec:oblate_prolate}

The three-class taxonomy raises a natural question: \emph{why} does oblate
geometry improve identifiability while prolate geometry does not?

The answer lies in the spatial relationship between curvature concentration
and modal displacement peaks --- a mechanism we term
\emph{curvature--mode anti-correlation}.

For an oblate spheroid, the Gaussian curvature is maximal at the equator and
minimal at the poles.  The low-order Legendre modes $P_n(\cos\theta)$, by
contrast, have their largest amplitudes near the poles (where
$\cos\theta \to 1$).  Different modes therefore sample distinct curvature
environments: low-order modes weight the low-curvature polar cap, while
higher-order modes concentrate energy in the high-curvature equatorial belt.
This spatial diversity means that each mode's sensitivity to $a$ and $c$
differs, breaking the proportionality of the Jacobian columns and reducing
$\kappa$.

For a prolate spheroid, the geometry reverses.  Gaussian curvature is now
maximal at the poles --- exactly where all Legendre modes peak.  Every mode
therefore samples essentially the same high-curvature region with similar
weighting.  The Jacobian columns remain nearly proportional regardless of
eccentricity, and $\kappa$ stays high and flat.

The mechanism is constructive rather than symmetry-theoretic: it identifies
the specific integrals
$\int_0^1 P_n^2(\eta)\,G^{-k}(\eta)\,\mathrm{d}\eta$ whose $n$-dependence
does or does not vary with eccentricity.  In the oblate case, the factor
$(\eta^2 - \tfrac{2}{3})$ in the perturbation
integral changes sign on $[0,1]$, and different
$P_n^2$ sample the positive and negative regions differently.  In the
prolate case, the analogous factor does not produce sufficient
$n$-dependent variation, because the modal peaks and curvature peaks are
co-located.

\subsection{Open questions}\label{sec:open_questions}

The two-channel expansion and the three identifiability regimes organise the
numerical findings, but several questions remain open.  We record the
outstanding mathematical programme explicitly.

Let $\mathcal{N}=\{n_1,\ldots,n_N\}$ denote the measured flexural mode set,
let $\varepsilon=\sqrt{1-c^2/a^2}$ be the eccentricity, and let
$\mathbf{J}_s(\varepsilon;\mathcal{N})$ denote the corresponding scaled
Jacobian of the frequency--parameter map.

\paragraph{Open question 1: closed-form expansion coefficients.}
\label{conj:expansion}
For the Ritz model with fixed mode set $\mathcal{N}$, can the expansion
coefficients $\sigma_0(\mathcal{N})$, $\lambda_1(\mathcal{N})$, and
$\lambda_2(\mathcal{N})$ in~\eqref{eq:sigma_min_expansion} be expressed
in closed form as functions of the curvature-weighted Legendre integrals
$\int_0^1 P_n^2(\eta)\,(\eta^2 - \tfrac{2}{3})\,\mathrm{d}\eta$ and the
canonical parameter set?  A derivation would turn the numerically determined
coefficients~\eqref{eq:expansion_coeffs} into design formulae, giving the
floor, slope, and crossover eccentricity as explicit functions of the mode
set.

\paragraph{Open question 2: minimum modes for identifiability.}
\label{conj:min_modes}
Consider the four-parameter material map with parameter vector
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\vartheta} = (E,h,\rho_w,\rho_f)^\top,
\end{equation}
at fixed geometry and fixed $(\nu,K_f,P_\mathrm{iap})$.  The Ritz forward
model introduces three independent dimensionless groups that govern the
frequency spectrum (beyond $\varepsilon$ itself): a stiffness ratio
$Eh/K_f R$, a density ratio $\rho_w h/\rho_f R$, and a prestress ratio
$P_\mathrm{iap}/E$.  If these three groups exhaust the independent parameter
combinations (as the structure of
equations~\eqref{eq:flexural_forward}--\eqref{eq:req} suggests but we do not
formally prove), then three measured flexural modes suffice for local
identifiability if and only if $\varepsilon>0$.  At $\varepsilon=0$, the
additional proportionality constraint of Proposition~\ref{prop:chain_rule}
removes one degree of freedom, and no finite mode set yields full column rank.
The completeness of the three-group reduction has not been established
rigorously; a formal proof would require verifying that no hidden similarity
scaling further reduces the effective parameter dimension.

\begin{remark}[Improvement with mode count]
\label{rem:monotone}
The numerical evidence of Table~\ref{tab:mode_count} shows that the
Cram\'er--Rao bounds generally improve as the mode count $\mathcal{N}$
increases from $3$ to $9$, with diminishing marginal returns beyond
$\mathcal{N}=5$.  This is an expected consequence of overdetermination:
each additional mode adds an independent row to the Jacobian, reducing the
covariance bound.  However, we observe no clean power-law decay of $\kappa$
with $\mathcal{N}$ at fixed $\varepsilon$ --- the condition number is
non-monotone (Table~\ref{tab:mode_count}), ranging from $69.4$ at
$\mathcal{N}=5$ to $93.6$ at $\mathcal{N}=9$.  We therefore record the
improvement as an empirical observation rather than elevating it to a formal
conjecture.  The practical implication is that five modes capture the bulk
of the available information for the present three-parameter problem.
\end{remark}

Open Question~1 is the most tantalising of these.  The numerics already
determine the expansion coefficients
to several significant figures~\eqref{eq:expansion_coeffs}, but a
closed-form derivation would give the floor, slope, and crossover as
explicit functions of the mode set --- turning a useful fit into an
asymptotic theorem.  That would be the crown jewel of the present
identifiability programme; at the time of writing, it remains work in
progress.
