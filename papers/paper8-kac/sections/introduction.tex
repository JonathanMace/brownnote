\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

In 1966, Kac asked the now-classic question ``Can one hear the shape of a
drum?''~\cite{Kac1966}.  The question is spectral: does the set of resonant
frequencies determine the geometry uniquely?  Gordon, Webb, and
Wolpert~\cite{Gordon1992} later showed that, for planar drums, the answer is
``no'': distinct domains can share the same Laplacian spectrum.  That result
did not close the subject.  It made it more interesting.

The Browntone programme has approached that question from the forward side.
Paper~1 established that the human abdomen supports low-order flexural
resonances in the infrasonic band~\cite{BrowntoneCompanion}; Paper~4 extended
the same fluid--shell framework to the fill-dependent resonance of the urinary
bladder~\cite{BrowntoneBladder}; and Paper~7 showed that an analogous model can
invert a watermelon tap-tone into a rind modulus estimate~\cite{BrowntoneWatermelon}.
Those papers answer variants of the question ``what frequencies does this shell
produce?''  The present paper asks the inverse question on which all such
applications ultimately depend: given measured frequencies, can one recover the
shell's material properties at all?

That identifiability question is the theoretical keystone of the programme.
Without it, a forward resonance model is descriptive but not diagnostic.  With
it, the same spectrum can become the basis of non-invasive elastography,
parameter estimation, and experimental design.  In practical terms, if a small
set of resonant frequencies determines stiffness and geometry with acceptable
conditioning, then the abdominal, bladder, and watermelon problems cease to be
curiosities of shell vibration and become inverse tools.  If not, one merely
fits a model to a spectrum that never had enough information in the first
place.

Here we therefore study an engineering analogue of Kac's inverse problem for a
fluid-filled viscoelastic shell.  We focus on low-frequency flexural modes
$n \geq 2$, rather than the breathing mode $n=0$, which lies near
\SI{2490}{Hz} and is governed primarily by fluid compressibility.  The inverse
problem is posed on the measured flexural frequencies
$\{f_2,f_3,\ldots,f_N\}$, with the aim of recovering the semi-major axis $a$,
the semi-minor axis $c$, and the Young's modulus $E$.

The key geometric point is oblateness.  A spherical shell sits at a spectrally
degenerate limit: symmetry forces geometric sensitivities to collapse, so the
inverse map becomes singular or nearly so.  An oblate shell does not enjoy that
luxury.  Once $a \neq c$, different flexural modes sample curvature differently,
the degeneracy is lifted, and identifiability can in principle be restored.
The question is not whether asymmetry looks promising in a qualitative sense,
but whether it restores enough rank to support useful inversion.

A central modelling point must also be stated plainly.  The implemented forward
model is an \emph{equivalent-sphere} model.  It uses the equivalent radius
\begin{equation}
  R_\mathrm{eq} = (a^2 c)^{1/3},
\end{equation}
and does not contain explicit spheroidal curvature terms or explicit
aspect-ratio corrections.  We therefore do \emph{not} claim a complete oblate
shell theory in the numerical sections.  Rather, we use the equivalent-sphere
model to expose the inverse structure already visible in the Browntone
applications, and we pair it with analytical results that show why a genuine
oblate Ritz model should restore identifiability away from the spherical limit.

Our contribution is therefore twofold.  First, we quantify practical
identifiability through the Jacobian of the frequency--parameter map, using the
dimensionless scaled Jacobian, its singular values, its condition number, and
the associated Fisher information matrix to assess whether small perturbations
in the measured frequencies can be mapped reliably back to $(a,c,E)$.  Second,
we make explicit the programme-level consequence: identifiability, not merely
forward prediction, is the mathematical foundation on which Papers~1--7 rest.
This is an analysis of \emph{practical} identifiability in the sense of Raue
et al.~\cite{Raue2009} and Wieland et al.~\cite{Wieland2021}, not a global
uniqueness theorem.

The main numerical findings are as follows.
\begin{itemize}
  \item At the spherical operating point, the scaled Jacobian is effectively
  rank deficient, with condition number
  $\kappa = 1.37 \times 10^{10}$.
  \item At the canonical oblate operating point, the same analysis gives
  $\kappa = 69.4$, an improvement by a factor of
  $2.0 \times 10^{8}$.
  \item A Newton-type inverse solver recovers $(a,c,E)$ to relative errors
  below $10^{-12}$ in round-trip tests when initialised within the local basin
  of attraction.
  \item At $1\%$ frequency noise, the Cram\'er--Rao lower bounds correspond to
  uncertainties of approximately $\pm 22.5\%$ in $a$, $\pm 6.0\%$ in $c$, and
  $\pm 30.8\%$ in $E$.
  \item Three-mode inversion is locally feasible but exhibits local minima,
  whereas five-mode inversion is robust across the tested initial guesses.
\end{itemize}

These results suggest that one can, in a practical sense, hear the shape of an
oblate organ from a modest set of flexural resonances, whereas the same task is
poorly posed at the spherical limit.  For the Browntone programme, that
distinction is not a side remark.  It explains why the forward models of Papers
1--7 are potentially invertible in the first place, and why oblateness rather
than near-sphericity is the feature that turns resonance from a descriptive
quantity into a usable diagnostic observable.

The paper is organised as follows.  Section~\ref{sec:theory} summarises the
equivalent-sphere forward model and the inverse formulation.  Section
\ref{sec:methodology} describes the numerical Jacobian, the Newton-type
inversion procedure, and the parameter-space conditioning study.  Section
\ref{sec:results} presents the sphere--oblate contrast, singular-value
structure, Cram\'er--Rao bounds, and a watermelon cross-application.  Section
\ref{sec:discussion} relates the results back to Kac's question, the Browntone
applications, and vibroacoustic elastography, and closes with three formal
conjectures that emerge from the numerics.  Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}
summarises the practical recommendations and the programme-level implications.
