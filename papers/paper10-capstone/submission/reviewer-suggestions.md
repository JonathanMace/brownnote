# Suggested Reviewers

**Paper**: Geometry Mediates Excitation, Resonance, and Identifiability in Fluid-Filled Viscoelastic Shells  
**Target**: Proceedings of the Royal Society A

---

## 1. Professor John E. Mottershead

**Affiliation**: Department of Mechanical, Materials and Aerospace Engineering, University of Liverpool, UK

**Justification**: Professor Mottershead is a leading authority on inverse methods in structural dynamics, finite element model updating, and structural identification. His foundational work on sensitivity-based updating and parameter identifiability is directly relevant to our rank-collapse theorem and identifiability-lifting results.

---

## 2. Professor Marco Amabili

**Affiliation**: Department of Mechanical Engineering, McGill University, Canada

**Justification**: Professor Amabili is internationally recognised for his research on nonlinear vibrations of shells and fluid–structure interaction. His extensive work on the dynamics of fluid-filled shells, including biomedical applications, makes him ideally placed to evaluate the forward-model aspects of our framework.

---

## 3. Professor Maarten V. de Hoop

**Affiliation**: Department of Computational and Applied Mathematics, Rice University, USA

**Justification**: Professor de Hoop's research programme in inverse problems, spectral geometry, and microlocal analysis provides the mathematical perspective needed to evaluate our identifiability results and the near-spherical asymptotic analysis. His work on geometric inverse problems bridges the gap between pure mathematics and applied mechanics.

---

## 4. Professor Eleanor Stride

**Affiliation**: Institute of Biomedical Engineering, University of Oxford, UK

**Justification**: Professor Stride's expertise in biomedical acoustics, ultrasound-mediated therapy, and bubble dynamics is directly relevant to the biomedical applications discussed in the paper (abdominal resonance, bladder fill-tracking, gas-pocket transduction). She can assess whether the cross-application claims are well grounded in physiological acoustics.

---

## 5. Professor Wim Desmet

**Affiliation**: Department of Mechanical Engineering, KU Leuven, Belgium

**Justification**: Professor Desmet is a recognised expert in vibroacoustics, structural dynamics, and computational methods for noise and vibration problems. His experience with transfer-path analysis and indirect force identification is relevant to the excitation-filtering and coupling-disparity aspects of the paper.

---

## 6. Professor Assad Oberai

**Affiliation**: Department of Aerospace and Mechanical Engineering, University of Southern California, USA

**Justification**: Professor Oberai has made significant contributions to inverse problems in elastography, including the recovery of material properties from measured displacement fields. His work on identifiability and uniqueness in soft-tissue parameter estimation directly relates to our formal results on the forward≠inverse adequacy distinction.

---

## 7. Professor Nicole Kessissoglou

**Affiliation**: School of Mechanical and Manufacturing Engineering, UNSW Sydney, Australia

**Justification**: Professor Kessissoglou's research on structural acoustics, shell vibration, and underwater sound radiation provides relevant expertise for evaluating the shell-theory foundations and fluid-loading models. Her experience with both analytical and computational approaches to coupled shell–fluid problems is well matched to the scope of the paper.

---

## 8. Professor Gennadiy Burlak

**Affiliation**: Centro de Investigación en Ingeniería y Ciencias Aplicadas, Universidad Autónoma del Estado de Morelos, Mexico

**Justification**: Professor Burlak's work on spectral properties of operators on shells and inverse spectral problems provides the mathematical background to assess our Kac-inspired identifiability analysis. His expertise in eigenvalue perturbation theory is directly relevant to the near-spherical asymptotics proposition.
