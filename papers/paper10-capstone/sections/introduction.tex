\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Spectral inverse problems ask what physical parameters can be recovered from an
observed resonance spectrum.  Versions of that question arise in structural
dynamics, elastographic parameter estimation, and non-destructive testing,
where one compares measured modal data with a forward model and seeks the
parameter set that best explains the observations
~\cite{Gladwell2004,Gladwell2005,MottersheadFriswell1993,FriswellMottershead1995,Doyley2012}.
The central issue is not simply whether a model reproduces the spectrum in the
forward direction.  It is whether the spectrum contains enough independent
information to distinguish the geometric and material parameters that generated
it.

The present paper treats that question within the lineage of structural and
practical identifiability.  In the classical system-identification literature,
Bellman and {\AA}str{\"o}m formalised structural identifiability as a question
of whether distinct parameter values can in principle be separated by the model
output~\cite{BellmanAstrom1970}, while Rothenberg's rank conditions gave a local
identification criterion in terms of the information carried by the observable
map~\cite{Rothenberg1971}.  In contemporary sensitivity-matrix approaches,
including the biomedical inverse-problem literature, the same distinction is
often phrased operationally: structural identifiability concerns Jacobian rank,
whereas practical identifiability concerns conditioning and the amplification of
measurement error through the inverse map~\cite{Raue2009,Doyley2012}.  Here
structural identifiability refers to local parameter distinguishability encoded
by Jacobian rank, whereas practical identifiability refers to the conditioning
of that recovery problem under finite perturbations and finite-precision data.
The scaled Jacobian framework used in Section~\ref{sec:theory} belongs directly
to that tradition.

The analogy with classical spectral geometry is limited but useful.  Kac's 1966
formulation remains a canonical statement of the inverse spectral ambition
~\cite{Kac1966}.  The present problem differs in that the operator is a coupled
fluid--structure system rather than a scalar Laplacian, but the identifiability
question --- which geometric features are spectrally distinguishable --- is
analogous.  Unlike Kac's global spectral-geometry problem, however, the present
paper concerns local identifiability of a finite-dimensional coupled
fluid--structure model from modal data.  The Gordon--Webb--Wolpert
counterexample remains relevant as a reminder that symmetry can hide geometric
information even when spectral data are exact~\cite{Gordon1992}.

We study these issues for fluid-filled viscoelastic shells.  Such shells arise
in biomechanics, soft-structure acoustics, postharvest sensing, and related
inverse problems, yet their spectral interpretation is often application-led.
Our claim is instead mechanistic and general.  In these systems geometry enters
in three distinct roles.  It filters how external forcing projects onto
admissible deformation fields; it organises the modal spectrum through
curvature, symmetry, and fluid loading; and it determines whether that spectrum
supports identifiable parameter recovery.  The first role is formalised in the
Breit--Wigner excitation proposition; the second organises the shell spectra
seen across the companion studies; the third is the main inverse-problem focus
of the present paper.

The excitation role matters because resonance cannot be interpreted apart from
how a forcing field couples to the relevant modes.  In the long-wavelength
limit, the Breit--Wigner absorption law shows that even an existing flexural
mode may be weakly excited if the radiative channel is geometrically
suppressed.  The spectral-organisation role matters because curvature and fluid
loading separate low-frequency flexural families from other motions and alter
how different modes respond to geometric perturbation
~\cite{Leissa1973,Soedel2004,Junger1986}.  The inverse role matters because a
model may preserve the dominant frequency scale of the shell while erasing the
very curvature information required for multi-parameter recovery.

The applications considered here provide evidence for that broader claim.  They
include human abdominal resonance, beginning from an infrasound-coupling
question in abdominal biomechanics~\cite{BrowntoneP1,BrowntoneP6}; watermelon
ripeness assessment, where a reduced forward surrogate can remain useful when
the inferential target is deliberately narrow~\cite{BrowntoneP7}; and
organ-shape identifiability, where geometry and material properties must be
recovered jointly and the inverse problem becomes central~\cite{BrowntoneP8}.
The formal results are new to this paper; the applications supply cross-domain
evidence from companion studies, but are not re-derived here.

Against that background, the paper isolates a specific geometric mechanism.
Symmetry-reduced descriptions, especially equivalent-radius reductions, can be
entirely serviceable as forward surrogates because they preserve the leading
frequency scale of a non-spherical shell.  Their inverse behaviour is more
restrictive.  Once two geometric degrees of freedom are compressed into a
single scalar descriptor, the spectrum may lose an independent curvature
channel and the scaled Jacobian may become rank deficient.  Structural
identifiability is then lost.  Even when the rank is restored, the smallest
singular value may remain small enough that the inverse problem is practically
fragile.  In this sense the gap between rank and conditioning is not a technical
afterthought; it is part of the geometry of the inverse problem itself.

The geometric remedy is precise rather than universal.  Theorem~\ref{thm:p10_lifting}
does not assert that arbitrary symmetry breaking restores identifiability.
It shows, more modestly and more usefully, that oblate asphericity can lift the
rank deficiency on a non-empty open set because different flexural modes sample
meridional and circumferential curvature differently.  Proposition~\ref{prop:p10_asymptotics}
then gives the corrected near-spherical law
$\sigma_3(\varepsilon)=\lambda_1\varepsilon^2+O(\varepsilon^4)$, so the exact
sphere is singular and the recovered identifiability grows regularly as the
shell departs from spherical symmetry.

The formal development therefore contains four principal formal results and one
supporting proposition, matching the hierarchy stated in the abstract.
Proposition~\ref{prop:p10_breit_wigner} recasts the Breit--Wigner excitation
filter as a geometric statement about modal coupling.  Theorem
~\ref{thm:p10_scalar_reduction} proves rank collapse under scalar geometric
reduction and therefore the loss of structural identifiability.  Theorem
~\ref{thm:p10_lifting} shows that oblate asphericity can restore full rank on a
non-empty open set.  Proposition~\ref{prop:p10_asymptotics} gives the correct
near-spherical asymptotics for the smallest singular value of the scaled
Jacobian.  Lemma~\ref{lem:p10_forward_inverse} then demonstrates that a small
forward spectral error does not by itself imply an inverse model suitable for
parameter recovery.

This scope is deliberately local and linearised.  We study practical
identifiability through the singular values and condition number of a scaled
Jacobian built from modal frequencies, and structural identifiability through
its rank.  We do not claim global uniqueness for nonlinear inverse problems,
and we do not suggest that spectral data alone resolve every ambiguity in real
shells.  The analysis is restricted to linearised shell dynamics with
homogeneous viscoelastic walls, axisymmetric reference geometries, and a single
internal fluid phase.  These assumptions are strong enough to expose the
mechanism of interest without pretending to settle the full experimental
problem.

Nor does the present paper provide new experiments.  Its contribution is
analytical and synthetic: to formulate the general inverse-problem principle,
prove the formal results, and show how prior work across several shell classes
supports that principle.  Experimental routes are discussed later, particularly
for modal measurements on shells with controlled asphericity and independently
known stiffness, but they are not part of the present contribution.

Section~\ref{sec:background} recalls the companion evidence and the common
geometric structure required for the present argument.  Section~\ref{sec:theory}
proves the supporting proposition and the four principal formal results in a
unified local-identifiability framework.  Section~\ref{sec:results} tests those
results against the cross-domain record, Section~\ref{sec:discussion}
interprets the implications for spectral inference on shells, and
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} closes with the general lesson that geometry
governs not only what shell spectra look like, but also what they can reliably
reveal.
