\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Spectral inverse problems ask what physical parameters can be recovered from an
observed resonance spectrum.  Versions of that question arise in structural
dynamics, elastographic parameter estimation, and non-destructive testing,
where one compares measured modal data with a forward model and seeks the
parameter set that best explains the observations
~\cite{Gladwell2004,Gladwell2005,MottersheadFriswell1993,FriswellMottershead1995,Doyley2012}.
The central issue is not simply whether a model reproduces the spectrum in the
forward direction.  It is whether the spectrum contains enough independent
information to distinguish the geometric and material parameters that generated
it.  Following the classical identifiability framework of Bellman and
{\AA}str{\"o}m~\cite{BellmanAstrom1970}, Rothenberg~\cite{Rothenberg1971}, and
its computational operationalisation by Raue \emph{et al.}~\cite{Raue2009}, the
question is one of both rank and conditioning.

For fluid-filled viscoelastic shells, geometry enters that question in three
distinct roles.  It filters how external forcing projects onto admissible
deformation fields; it organises the modal spectrum through curvature,
symmetry, and fluid loading; and it determines whether that spectrum supports
identifiable parameter recovery.  The first role explains why a resonance can
exist yet remain weakly excitable.  The second explains why different modal
families respond differently to shape.  The third is the main concern of the
present paper, because it determines whether spectral data can separate
geometric and material effects at all.  Figure~\ref{fig:three_jobs} summarises
these three geometric roles and their relation to the present shell models.

The analogy with Kac's question is instructive but limited.  Kac's 1966
formulation remains a canonical statement of the inverse spectral ambition
~\cite{Kac1966}.  The present problem concerns local parametric identifiability
of a finite-dimensional model, not global spectral uniqueness of a differential
operator.  The operator is a coupled fluid--structure system rather than a
scalar Laplacian, but the analogous issue remains which geometric features are
spectrally distinguishable.  The Gordon--Webb--Wolpert counterexample remains
relevant as a reminder that symmetry can hide geometric information even when
spectral data are exact~\cite{Gordon1992}.

We study these issues for fluid-filled viscoelastic shells.  Such shells arise
in biomechanics, soft-structure acoustics, postharvest sensing, and related
inverse problems, yet their spectral interpretation is often application-led.
Here structural identifiability refers to local parameter distinguishability
encoded by Jacobian rank, whereas practical identifiability refers to the
conditioning of that recovery problem under finite perturbations and
finite-precision data~\cite{Raue2009,Doyley2012}.  The scaled Jacobian
framework used in Section~\ref{sec:theory} belongs directly to that tradition.

Within that framework, the most restrictive mechanism is geometric reduction.
Symmetry-reduced descriptions, especially equivalent-radius reductions, can be
entirely serviceable as forward surrogates because they preserve the leading
frequency scale of a non-spherical shell.  Their inverse behaviour is less
forgiving.  Once two geometric degrees of freedom are compressed into a single
scalar descriptor, the spectrum may lose an independent curvature channel and
the scaled Jacobian may become rank deficient.  Structural identifiability is
then lost.  Even when rank is formally restored, the smallest singular value
may remain small enough that the inverse problem is severely ill-conditioned.

Oblate asphericity can restore local rank in the present setting, but only
in the precise local sense established in Section~\ref{sec:theory}.  Theorem
~\ref{thm:p10_lifting} does not assert that arbitrary symmetry breaking is
sufficient.  It shows, more modestly and more usefully, that oblate
asphericity can lift the rank deficiency on a non-empty open set because
different flexural modes sample meridional and circumferential curvature
differently.  Proposition~\ref{prop:p10_asymptotics} then gives the corrected
near-spherical law $\sigma_3(\varepsilon)=\lambda_1\varepsilon^2+O(\varepsilon^4)$,
so the exact sphere is singular and local distinguishability grows regularly as
the shell departs from spherical symmetry.  At the canonical oblate operating
point, the inverse is not merely full-rank but moderately conditioned, with
$\kappa=69.4$ for the five-mode study reported in~\cite{BrowntoneP8}.

The applications considered here provide evidence for that broader claim.  They
include human abdominal resonance, beginning from an infrasound-coupling
question in abdominal biomechanics (including the phenomenon sometimes
discussed under the ``brown note'' label)~\cite{BrowntoneP1,BrowntoneP6};
watermelon ripeness assessment, where a reduced forward surrogate can remain
useful when the inferential target is deliberately narrow~\cite{BrowntoneP7};
and urinary-bladder resonance, where fill state changes both size and effective
stiffness and therefore provides a contrasting geometry-sensitive shell
application~\cite{BrowntoneP4}.  The formal results stated below are new; the
applications are drawn from companion studies and serve as the evidence base.

The formal development contains four principal formal results and one
supporting excitation proposition.  The supporting proposition,
Proposition~\ref{prop:p10_breit_wigner}, recasts the Breit--Wigner excitation
filter as a geometric statement about modal coupling.  The four principal
results are these.  Theorem~\ref{thm:p10_scalar_reduction} proves rank collapse
under scalar geometric reduction and therefore the loss of structural
identifiability.  Theorem~\ref{thm:p10_lifting} shows that oblate asphericity
can restore full rank on a non-empty open set.  Proposition
~\ref{prop:p10_asymptotics} gives the correct near-spherical asymptotics for
the smallest singular value of the scaled Jacobian.  Proposition
~\ref{prop:p10_forward_inverse} then demonstrates that small forward spectral
error does not by itself imply an inverse model suitable for parameter
recovery.

The broader significance is not confined to the present shell class.  Whenever
resonance data are used to infer geometry, stiffness, or both, there is an
unwarranted inference to avoid: success in the forward fit does not by itself
establish that the inverse map is informative.  The identifiability lesson is
that symmetry can preserve the dominant spectral scale while removing the
independent geometric channels required for parameter recovery.

Our scope is deliberately local and linearised.  We study structural
identifiability through Jacobian rank and practical identifiability through the
singular values and condition number of a scaled Jacobian built from modal
frequencies.  We do not claim global uniqueness for nonlinear inverse problems,
and we do not suggest that spectral data alone resolve every ambiguity in real
shells.  The analysis is restricted to linearised shell dynamics with
homogeneous viscoelastic walls, axisymmetric reference geometries, and a single
internal fluid phase; experimental routes are discussed later, but no new
experiments are presented here.
