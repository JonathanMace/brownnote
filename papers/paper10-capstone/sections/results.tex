\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We now test the four formal results against the cross-application record assembled
in~\cite{BrowntoneP1,BrowntoneP4,BrowntoneP5,BrowntoneP7,BrowntoneP8}.  The point of the comparison is not that every
application solves the same inverse problem.  Instead, some cases
are dominated by excitation filtering, some by spectral organisation, and some
by parameter recovery.  What persists across them is that geometry decides which
of those questions can be asked sensibly in the first place.
Table~\ref{tab:cross_application_evidence} collects the principal
application-level evidence used below.

Table~\ref{tab:cross_application_evidence} already hints at the main result.
The abdominal, bladder, watermelon, and borborygmi studies occupy different
frequency bands and involve different observables, yet they all distinguish
between a forward spectral pattern and the stronger claim that parameters can be
recovered independently from that pattern.  That distinction becomes explicit
once the four formal results are read as a sequence rather than as isolated
claims.

\subsection{Rank collapse under scalar geometric reduction}

The first theorem is the most stringent mathematically.  If the flexural spectrum depends on the geometry only through
the scalar reduction
\begin{equation}
  R_\mathrm{eq} = (a^2 c)^{1/3},
\end{equation}
then the two geometric sensitivity directions cannot remain independent.  By
the chain rule, every modal derivative with respect to the equatorial semi-axis
$a$ is proportional to the corresponding derivative with respect to the polar
semi-axis $c$.  In the scaled Jacobian $\widetilde{J}$, the two geometry columns are
therefore collinear.  At the sphere this becomes physically transparent: a
small perturbation in $a$ and a small perturbation in $c$ both act only by
changing the same radius, so the geometry columns become proportional and one
geometric direction disappears from the inverse map.

The identifiability analysis in~\cite{BrowntoneP8} made that loss concrete for the canonical abdominal parameter set.  The
reported condition number for the equivalent-sphere inverse problem is
\begin{equation}
  \kappa_\mathrm{sphere} \approx 1.37 \times 10^{10},
\end{equation}
numerically finite only because the derivatives were estimated with a finite
difference rather than evaluated symbolically~\cite{BrowntoneP8}.  The true
statement is stronger than ``poorly conditioned'': the model architecture has
already removed one independent geometric degree of freedom before any data are
fitted.

The cross-application evidence supports this interpretation rather than weakens
it.  In the watermelon study of~\cite{BrowntoneP7}, the equivalent-sphere model works because the inverse
task is deliberately narrow: the fruit geometry is measured separately, and the
frequency is used to infer one effective stiffness scale
$E_\mathrm{rind}$~\cite{BrowntoneP7}.  In the constrained-bubble analysis
of~\cite{BrowntoneP5}, the
borborygmi model is similarly successful only because the dominant unknown is
effectively the gas-pocket size or mode family, not the simultaneous recovery
of two shape axes and a wall modulus~\cite{BrowntoneP5}.  These are not
counterexamples to rank deficiency.  They are examples of problems that avoid
it by reducing the inverse target.  Once one asks for joint recovery of
$(a,c,E)$ from spectra alone, the equivalent-radius reduction has already
discarded too much of the available information.

\subsection{Identifiability lifting by oblate asphericity}

The second theorem asks whether this degeneracy is merely diagnosed or
actually repairable.  The evidence says that it is repairable, but only when
the model retains genuine curvature information.  In the oblate Ritz family
analysed in~\cite{BrowntoneP8}, we may sweep continuously from the spherical limit toward the canonical
abdominal geometry by decreasing the aspect ratio $\zeta=c/a$ from near unity
to $\zeta=0.667$, equivalently increasing the eccentricity
$\varepsilon=\sqrt{1-c^2/a^2}$ from almost zero to the canonical oblate value
$\varepsilon=0.745$~\cite{BrowntoneP8}.

Two comparisons matter.  The first is the broad conceptual one: the condition
number drops from the \num{1e10}-scale equivalent-sphere pathology to
\begin{equation}
  \kappa_\mathrm{oblate} = 69.4
\end{equation}
once the geometry is treated as oblate rather than spherical.  The second is
the stricter like-for-like comparison within the same oblate Ritz model: the
conditioning improves from the near-spherical floor to the canonical oblate
point without changing the inverse variables or the measured mode set.  In both
comparisons the message is the same.  Asphericity is not merely a geometric detail; it
reintroduces a second geometric sensitivity channel.

The physical reason is mode-dependent curvature sampling.  The lowest abdominal
flexural mode already sits at
\begin{equation}
  f_2 = \SI{3.95}{\hertz},
\end{equation}
so the improvement in identifiability does not arise because the resonance is
dragged into a more convenient frequency band.  It arises because the higher
flexural modes do \emph{not} sample the oblate shell in the same way.  Meridional
and circumferential curvature changes are weighted differently by different
mode shapes, so the $a$- and $c$-columns of $\widetilde{J}$ cease to be parallel.  The
bladder study provides a useful supporting contrast here.  Its fill-dependent
U-shaped curve shows that geometry and stiffness can compete strongly in the
forward spectrum~\cite{BrowntoneP4}; the identifiability analysis in~\cite{BrowntoneP8}
shows what is additionally needed
for inversion, namely that different modes must separate those effects rather
than merely combine them.

This is also where the abdominal coupling result matters conceptually.  In the
abdominal model of~\cite{BrowntoneP1}, the same oblate geometry that yields the canonical
$f_2=\SI{3.95}{\hertz}$ also produces a severe excitation asymmetry, with a
reported mechanical-to-airborne displacement ratio
$R \approx 3.3\times 10^4$ in the canonical comparison~\cite{BrowntoneP1}.  The
same geometry therefore controls both whether a mode is efficiently excited and
whether it is diagnostically informative.  This encapsulates the capstone
thesis: geometry controls both the efficiency of excitation and the information
content of the measured response.

\subsection{Regular near-spherical asymptotics}

The third formal result concerns the approach to the sphere.  A tempting but
incorrect intuition is that the near-spherical oblate inverse problem should
retain a finite condition-number floor because early low-order Ritz fits
appeared to give a positive intercept $\sigma_0$.  The corrected analysis is
stricter.  At the exact sphere, restored symmetry forces rank collapse, so
$\sigma_3(0)=0$.  What remains is the regular even expansion
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_3(\varepsilon) = \lambda_1 \varepsilon^2 + O(\varepsilon^4),
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_1>0$ for the canonical oblate family.  The previously reported
finite floor was therefore a discretisation artefact of the low-order Ritz
spherical limit, not a property of the continuum inverse problem.

The resulting classification has three numerically distinct classes.  The first class is the
\emph{singular sphere}: the equivalent-sphere reduction has
$\kappa_\mathrm{sphere}\approx 1.37\times 10^{10}$ because the inverse map is
rank deficient.  The second is the \emph{regular oblate} class: the
curvature-resolving oblate family is full rank on the tested oblate branch and,
in particular, on a non-empty open set around the canonical oblate operating
point, with conditioning that deteriorates like $\varepsilon^{-2}$ as the
sphere is approached and improves to $69.4$ at the canonical oblate point.  The third is
the \emph{non-lifting prolate} class: breaking
spherical symmetry in the wrong geometric direction does not help much, and the
condition number stays in the range
\begin{equation}
  \kappa_\mathrm{prolate} \approx 561\text{--}729
\end{equation}
across the tested prolate branch~\cite{BrowntoneP8,BrowntoneP9}.

This three-class picture matters because it replaces a vague slogan ---
``non-spherical is better'' --- with a more precise statement.  Some departures
from the sphere restore useful inverse information; some do not; and some model
reductions remain singular regardless of numerical precision.  The borborygmi
and bladder papers again provide supporting context.  Both show that spectra in
soft, fluid-loaded biological systems can change strongly with geometry, fill
state, or confinement~\cite{BrowntoneP4,BrowntoneP5}.  The near-spherical
analysis tells us when such spectral variation should also be expected to carry
independent inverse information.  Those are related questions, but they are not
the same question.

\subsection{Forward adequacy does not imply inverse adequacy}

The fourth formal result is the most practical, and the watermelon analysis
in~\cite{BrowntoneP7} supplies the most useful cautionary example.  The
equivalent-sphere watermelon model predicts
tap-tone frequencies with useful accuracy over the relevant stiffness range:
$f_2$ spans \SIrange{46.4}{203.2}{\hertz}, and at the canonical reference point
the model gives $f_2=\SI{89.9}{\hertz}$ with a monotonic relation between
$f_2^2$ and the effective rind modulus $E_\mathrm{rind}$~\cite{BrowntoneP7}.
For that task the reduced model is appropriate.  Geometry is measured
independently; the inverse target is essentially one-dimensional; and the model
therefore serves as a practical forward predictor and calibration law.

The difficulty arises when that forward success is promoted into a general
inversion claim.  If the same equivalent-sphere architecture is asked to recover
$(a,c,E)$ jointly from modal data, the condition number returns to the
\num{1e10} scale, because the geometry has again been collapsed into a single
radius.  The curvature-resolving oblate model, by contrast, yields broadly
similar low-order frequencies while restoring a usable inverse map with
\begin{equation}
  \kappa = 69.4.
\end{equation}
Small forward spectral error and good inverse conditioning are related only
loosely, and sometimes not at all.

The other applications reinforce the same lesson from different angles.  The
bladder model predicts a clear forward phenomenon --- a minimum
$f_2=\SI{13.5}{\hertz}$ at \SI{222}{\milli\litre} --- and a clear coupling
phenomenon --- $\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{bladder}\approx 6{,}400$ in favour of
mechanical excitation~\cite{BrowntoneP4}.  Neither result, by itself, certifies
that a joint inverse problem for geometry and material parameters would be well
conditioned.  The borborygmi model similarly supports narrow inferences, such as
mapping a peak near \SI{340}{\hertz} to a pocket volume of order
\SI{3}{\milli\litre}, without claiming that bowel geometry and wall elasticity
can be reconstructed uniquely from the sound alone~\cite{BrowntoneP5}.  Forward
adequacy is often enough for diagnosis, classification, or design.  It does not
obviate the need to assess identifiability.

Taken together, the four formal results survive the cross-application
synthesis.  Equivalent-radius reductions are structurally rank deficient;
oblate curvature can lift that defect; the lifting vanishes regularly at the
exact sphere; and forward predictive success does not guarantee inverse
usefulness.
That is why the same research programme can contain, without contradiction, a
successful equivalent-sphere watermelon model and a strong warning against
equivalent-sphere inversion.  The models answer different questions.  Geometry
determines which inferences are defensible.
