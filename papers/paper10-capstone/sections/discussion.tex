\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The capstone claim of this paper is not simply that geometry matters.  That
would be a qualitative observation rather than a mechanistic one.  The stronger conclusion is
that geometry enters the problem of fluid-filled viscoelastic shells in three
logically distinct ways, and that confusing those roles obscures both the
forward physics and the inverse problem.  Geometry filters how forcing is
projected onto admissible deformation fields; geometry organises the modal
spectrum through curvature, symmetry, and fluid loading; and geometry
determines whether that spectrum contains enough independent information to
recover the parameters that produced it.  The four formal results of
Section~\ref{sec:theory} are best read as consequences of this triple role,
rather than as disconnected observations assembled from a sequence of
applications.

\subsection{Forward adequacy is not inverse adequacy}

Of the four formal results, the deepest is the fourth: forward adequacy does not
imply inverse adequacy.  A reduced model may reproduce the dominant resonance
frequencies of a shell to within a tolerance that is entirely acceptable for
classification, screening, or preliminary design, yet remain unusable for
parameter recovery because the inverse map is ill-conditioned or effectively
singular.  The present programme suggests that this decoupling is not an
incidental numerical inconvenience.  It is geometry-mediated.

Equivalent-radius reductions explain the practical appeal.  If the observable of
interest is a small set of low-order frequencies, a scalar geometric surrogate
can often preserve the leading scale of the answer.  Such models compress the
problem without obviously destroying the forward spectrum, and that economy is
precisely why they remain attractive in practice.  What they do not preserve is
\emph{separability}.  Once independent curvature information has been collapsed
into a single scalar descriptor, distinct combinations of geometry and material
stiffness can generate nearly indistinguishable spectra.  The forward error may
remain small while the inverse uncertainty becomes enormous.  A model may
therefore look persuasive in prediction and still be defective in inference.

This distinction is the most important conceptual result to emerge from the
Browntone sequence.  The existence of a resonance is not enough to establish an
effective excitation pathway~\cite{BrowntoneP1,BrowntoneP6}.  Nor is the
existence of a good spectral fit enough to establish a recoverable parameter
map~\cite{BrowntoneP7,BrowntoneP8}.
These are parallel lessons.  In both cases, geometry selects what can be seen.
On the excitation side, it selects which forcing fields project efficiently onto
the mode family of interest.  On the inverse side, it selects which parameter
directions remain distinguishable once those modes are measured.  A reduced
model that is useful in forward prediction may therefore be unstable for inversion for reasons that
are not accidental but structural.

\subsection{Why geometry does three jobs}

The first job of geometry is filtering.  The original brown-note problem made
this clear.  A fluid-filled oblate shell can support a genuine low-frequency
flexural mode near \SI{4}{\hertz}, yet airborne infrasound couples to that mode
vanishingly weakly because the long-wavelength forcing field is itself filtered
by the geometry of the enclosure~\cite{BrowntoneP1}.  The same mode is far more
accessible to mechanical excitation because whole-body vibration is not subject
to the same spatial-harmonic mismatch~\cite{BrowntoneP6}.  Geometry therefore
acts as an impedance and projection filter between source and shell response.  A
resonance may exist without constituting a practical pathway.

The second job is organisation.  Fluid-filled shells do not present arbitrary
collections of frequencies.  Curvature, aspect ratio, internal loading, and wall
compliance sort the admissible motions into structured spectral families.  In
the abdominal problem, this distinguishes the low-frequency flexural modes from
the much higher breathing mode dominated by fluid compressibility
~\cite{BrowntoneP1}.  In the bladder, changing fill state alters both size and
effective stiffness, producing a resonance trajectory that is not captured by a
single monotonic size law~\cite{BrowntoneP4}.  In the gas-pocket and
borborygmi papers, confinement and cavity geometry organise a different but
related band of observable oscillations~\cite{BrowntoneP2,BrowntoneP5}.  Across
applications, geometry is the principal organiser of what the spectrum is allowed to
look like before any inversion begins.

Of the three geometric roles, excitation filtering and inverse identifiability
are formalised as propositions and theorems; spectral organisation is supported
by cross-application synthesis rather than by a comparably general formal
result.

The third job is inversion, and this is the capstone contribution.  Geometry
governs which parameter directions are distinguishable in spectral space.  When
symmetry is high, mode sensitivities tend to align and the scaled Jacobian loses
rank or becomes badly conditioned.  When symmetry is broken in a way that
creates genuinely different curvature sampling across modes, the sensitivity
columns separate and inversion becomes possible.  The rank-deficiency theorem,
the identifiability-lifting theorem, and the near-spherical asymptotic
proposition are all different ways of stating that fact.  Geometry is not
merely an input to the
forward problem.  It is the source of the information one hopes to recover from
the spectrum.

\subsection{Oblate--prolate asymmetry is a selectivity result}

It would be tempting to interpret the inverse results as evidence that any
departure from spherical symmetry is beneficial.  The present evidence does not
support so universal a reading.  The oblate--prolate comparison instead suggests
a more selective conclusion: some symmetry breakings produce useful modal
differentiation, whereas others do not.  Oblate geometries appear especially
effective because they induce mode-dependent curvature sampling that decorrelates
the parameter sensitivities.  Prolate geometries, at least in the regimes so far
examined, need not do so.

This matters because it changes how reduced models should be judged.  If the aim
is only to capture the leading frequency scale, an equivalent sphere may remain
an entirely sensible first approximation, and oblate and prolate corrections may
appear equally modest at the level of forward error.  If the aim is parameter
recovery, however, the choice of reduced geometry becomes part of the inverse
design problem.  A reduction that preserves mean size while erasing curvature
contrast may destroy identifiability; a reduction that retains the wrong kind of
asphericity may improve very little.  The asymmetry is therefore a selectivity
result, not a universality result.  Not all symmetry breaking helps, and reduced
models should be assessed by the sensitivity structure they preserve, not by
forward residuals alone.

That conclusion has broader methodological force.  In many practical inverse
problems, model reduction is justified \emph{a posteriori} by a small mismatch in
the predicted observables.  The present results suggest a stricter criterion.  A
reduction is acceptable only if it preserves the geometry-induced information
structure required by the inference task.  That is a harder requirement, but it
is the more appropriate one.

\subsection{What the cross-application evidence establishes}

Taken individually, the Browntone studies addressed local questions.  Could a
human abdominal cavity possess a low-frequency flexural resonance?  Could a
tissue-constrained gas pocket radiate in the audible band?  How does bladder
fill state alter resonance?  Can tap-tone data support inversion in a
watermelon?  Does asphericity lift inverse degeneracy?  The capstone claim is
that these are not merely isolated case studies.  They instantiate a common
mechanism in which geometry governs forcing pathways, spectral structure, and
identifiability in tandem.

The evidence, however, is not uniform, and it should not be presented as though
it were.  The filtering evidence is clearest in the abdominal, gas-pocket, and
sub-bass studies: excitation is geometry selective, and the same resonance can
be practically invisible to one forcing pathway yet accessible to another
~\cite{BrowntoneP1,BrowntoneP2,BrowntoneP6}.  The organisational evidence is
clearest in the scaling, bladder, and borborygmi studies: geometric scaling,
fill state, and confinement sort the spectrum into coherent families
~\cite{BrowntoneP3,BrowntoneP4,BrowntoneP5}.  The inverse evidence is clearest
in the watermelon and Kac-style studies, because they expose the tension between
a satisfactory forward fit and a recoverable parameter map
~\cite{BrowntoneP7,BrowntoneP8}.  What the
programme establishes, then, is not merely a collection of isolated case
studies but a coherent synthesis: apparently disparate vibroacoustic phenomena
in fluid-filled shells can be read as manifestations of one
geometry-mediated structure.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be stated plainly.  First, the present theory relies
on a Ritz approximation.  That is a reasonable and economical way to capture the
low-order modal structure of smooth axisymmetric shells, but it remains an
approximation.  A richer basis, higher-order shell theory, or full finite-element
verification would be desirable, especially in parameter regimes where modal
veering or stronger fluid--structure interaction becomes important.

Secondly, the kinematics are intentionally reduced.  The low-dimensional shell
description isolates the dominant meridional and radial content required for the
present formal results, but it cannot represent the full deformation space of a
real viscoelastic enclosure.  This simplification is analytically productive and
predictively limiting in equal measure.

Thirdly, the capstone is theoretical rather than experimental.  No dedicated
phantom measurements or in vivo data have yet been used to test the inverse
claims directly.  The present conclusions should therefore be read as
analytically supported and cross-application consistent, rather than
experimentally closed.

Fourthly, the model class is intentionally narrow: thin elastic shells,
single-fluid filling, small deformations, and linear response.  Real biological
and engineering enclosures may be layered, anisotropic, prestressed, spatially
heterogeneous, or only approximately shell-like.  Likewise, the surrounding
supports and boundary conditions in vivo are more complicated than those
represented here.  Finally, the analysis remains confined to the linear regime
and therefore excludes contact, finite strain, geometric stiffening, and other
nonlinear effects that may become important in some applications.

These limitations do not negate the present argument, but they do mark its
proper scope.  The four formal results concern the geometric structure of
spectral inference in a deliberately clean model class.  Whether they survive, and in
what modified form, under greater constitutive and geometric realism is precisely
the next question.

\subsection{Future directions}

The most immediate next step is experimental validation.  Phantom studies with
controlled oblate and prolate shells would allow direct measurement of modal
frequencies, damping, and inverse conditioning under known geometry and material
properties.  Such experiments matter because the capstone claims concern
\emph{parameter recovery}, not merely forward resonance.  It is not enough to
observe the correct frequency shift.  One must test whether the recovered
parameters are stable, repeatable, and geometry dependent in the predicted way.
If that programme succeeds, in vivo measurements would provide the more difficult
but more consequential test of whether the same structure persists in biological
variability.

A second direction is theoretical extension beyond the linear thin-shell
setting.  Layered walls, anisotropy, prestress, nonlinear kinematics, and more
realistic fluid--structure coupling may all alter the conditioning landscape.  A
particularly important question is which features of the present picture are
robust consequences of symmetry and curvature, and which depend more delicately
on the present reduced shell model.

A third direction is application.  The same spectral-mechanics template may be
useful for other compliant enclosures in biomechanics, soft robotics,
membrane-lined vessels, industrial casings, and postharvest systems.  In noise,
vibration, and harshness, non-destructive testing, and biomedical sensing, one
often seeks to infer internal state from sparse spectral observables.  The
present work suggests that success in such tasks may depend less on constitutive
ornament and more on understanding which geometric features create
distinguishable modal signatures.

Finally, the formal results deserve extension to richer model classes.  The
rank-deficiency and identifiability-lifting claims should be carried to higher
order shell theories, the near-spherical asymptotics should be re-derived for
layered and non-axisymmetric geometries, and the oblate--prolate selectivity
should be characterised beyond the regimes so far examined.  If the programme
began with an unconventional motivating question, it now defines a tractable set
of mathematical problems about
symmetry, curvature, and information in shell spectra.
