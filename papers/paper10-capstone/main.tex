\begin{abstract}
The resonant behaviour of fluid-filled viscoelastic shells arises
repeatedly in biomechanics, postharvest sensing, and non-destructive
evaluation, yet each application is typically treated in isolation.
This paper advances a unifying thesis: \emph{shell geometry simultaneously
filters the forcing field, organises the modal spectrum, and determines
the invertibility of the corresponding parameter-estimation problem.}

We formalise this thesis through four formal results and a supporting
excitation proposition.
First, we prove that equivalent-radius (spherical) reductions of
non-spherical shells are \emph{rank-deficient} in the sense of
structural identifiability: geometry and material parameters become
confounded, rendering independent recovery impossible.
Second, we prove that oblate asphericity lifts this rank deficiency through
curvature-dependent mode splitting, providing the additional spectral
information needed for locally identifiable inversion.
Third, we establish the corrected near-spherical asymptotics,
\(\sigma_3(\varepsilon)=\lambda_1\varepsilon^2+O(\varepsilon^4)\), so the exact
sphere is singular and the previously reported finite floor is recognised as a
discretisation artefact.
Fourth, we prove a cautionary lemma showing that forward-model adequacy does not imply
inverse-model adequacy: an equivalent-sphere model may predict
resonant frequencies within engineering tolerance yet fail
catastrophically when used for parameter estimation.

These formal results are supported by cross-application evidence drawn
from the human abdomen (infrasound resonance), the watermelon
(acoustic ripeness assessment), and the urinary bladder (fill-dependent
resonance), demonstrating that geometry is the hidden variable
unifying apparently disparate vibroacoustic phenomena.
\end{abstract}
