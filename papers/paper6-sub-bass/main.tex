\begin{highlights}
\item Airborne acoustic coupling to the abdomen increases as $(ka)^n$ with frequency
\item At \SIrange{20}{80}{\hertz}, $ka$ transitions from negligible to $\sim$0.2
\item Energy-consistent displacement at concert levels is $\sim$\num{2.5} orders below perception thresholds
\item Structure-borne (floor/seat) pathway dominates by $\sim$\num{2600}$\times$ over airborne at \SI{40}{\hertz}
\item The ``feel it in your gut'' sensation requires structural rather than airborne transmission
\end{highlights}

\begin{abstract}
Concertgoers commonly report ``feeling the bass in their gut'' at high
sound pressure levels, particularly during electronic dance music and
pipe-organ performances.  While whole-body vibration at
\SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} is known to excite abdominal resonances, the
relative contributions of airborne sub-bass sound
(\SIrange{20}{80}{\hertz}) and structure-borne vibration (through floors
and seating) to this visceral sensation have not been quantitatively
partitioned.

We extend the fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shell model
developed in our companion paper to the \SIrange{20}{80}{\hertz} band,
where the Helmholtz number $ka$ transitions from negligible
(${\sim}\num{0.01}$ at \SI{4}{\hertz}) to appreciable (${\sim}\num{0.23}$
at \SI{80}{\hertz}).  Using literature-derived concert sound pressure
level spectra for rock, electronic dance music, and pipe-organ venues,
we compute energy-consistent tissue displacements via the Junger \& Feit
reciprocity formulation and compare them with whole-body vibration
perception thresholds derived from ISO~2631-1.

Despite the improved acoustic coupling at higher frequencies, the
off-resonance penalty (the abdomen's flexural resonance lies at
${\sim}\SI{4}{\hertz}$, not \SIrange{20}{80}{\hertz}) reduces
displacements by approximately \num{2.5}~orders of magnitude below
perception thresholds.  At peak electronic dance music levels
(\SI{115}{\decibel} at \SI{40}{\hertz}), airborne-induced displacement
reaches \SI{0.35}{\percent} of the perception threshold:
insufficient alone, but not negligible.  By contrast, structure-borne
vibration transmitted through the floor and seating---modelled via a
damped-plate power balance---produces displacements
${\sim}\num{2600}\times$ larger (${\sim}9\times$ the perception
threshold; 90\% CI: $5.8$--$12.7\times$), confirming that floor
vibration is readily perceptible.

These results partition the ``feel it in your gut'' experience into its
constituent pathways: the airborne acoustic contribution is a near-miss
(not the four-orders-of-magnitude gap previously estimated using
pressure-based methods), while the structure-borne pathway provides
the dominant and perceptible route to visceral sensation.  Near-field
pressure enhancement (\SIrange{6}{10}{\decibel} within
\SI{1.5}{\metre} of a subwoofer stack) and mechanical coupling through
resonant church pews may further narrow the airborne gap in specific
scenarios.
\end{abstract}
