\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The central result of this study is an upper-bound coupling disparity of
approximately $6.6 \times 10^4$ between mechanical whole-body vibration and
airborne acoustic excitation of the human abdominal cavity for
\SI{120}{\decibel} SPL airborne loading and
$a_\mathrm{rms} = \SI{0.1}{\metre\per\second\squared}$ RMS whole-body vibration
under the SDOF idealisation ($\Gamma_2 = 1$).  This single quantity,
derived from first-principles shell theory and energy-consistent reciprocity
analysis, resolves a long-standing empirical asymmetry: occupational
whole-body vibration at \SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} causes well-documented
gastrointestinal effects, while airborne infrasound at comparable frequencies
and extreme intensities does not.

The disparity has a clean physical origin.  The abdominal cavity possesses
two distinct mode families: a breathing mode ($n = 0$) near
\SI{2500}{\hertz}, governed by the fluid bulk modulus and therefore irrelevant
at infrasonic frequencies; and flexural modes ($n \geq 2$) in the
\SIrange{4}{10}{\hertz} range that match ISO~2631 abdominal transmissibility
data.  Airborne sound at these frequencies has a wavelength exceeding
\SI{85}{\metre}, placing the abdomen deep in the Rayleigh regime
($ka \approx 0.01$).  The $(ka)^n$ geometric penalty, compounded by the
air--tissue impedance mismatch, reduces the effective driving pressure by
four orders of magnitude.  An energy-consistent analysis confirms an
absorption efficiency of $\sim 10^{-14}$: the shell is, for practical
purposes, acoustically transparent.  Reaching the cellular mechanotransduction
benchmark via airborne infrasound alone would require approximately
\SI{158}{\decibel} on the energy-consistent basis
(\SI{135}{\decibel} using the less physical pressure-based
estimate)---a level at which structural damage to the middle ear and other
severe acoustic hazards would present more immediate concerns than any
intestinal indignity.

Mechanical vibration, transmitted through the skeleton and abdominal wall,
couples directly and, for the headline comparison
(\SI{120}{\decibel} SPL airborne versus
$a_\mathrm{rms} = \SI{0.1}{\metre\per\second\squared}$ RMS WBV with
$\Gamma_2 = 1$), produces displacements approximately
$6.6 \times 10^4$ times larger; including the participation correction leaves
$\sim 3 \times 10^4$.  This disparity is not a subtle biomechanical effect
but a straightforward consequence of long-wavelength acoustics and impedance
physics.  Monte Carlo uncertainty quantification using the pressure-based
airborne upper bound yields a ratio spanning roughly one and a half orders of
magnitude, with a 90\% credible interval of
$[2 \times 10^3,\; 3 \times 10^4]$; elastic modulus accounts for 86\% of total
variance.

The brown note, then, is a half-truth.  The resonance it invokes is genuine;
the mechanism it implies is not.  What popular culture attributes to airborne
sound is, in reality, the province of mechanical vibration---a distinction
that matters considerably for occupational health standards.  Airborne
infrasound cannot excite the global cavity resonance to physiologically
relevant amplitudes; whether localised compressible inclusions
(intestinal gas pockets) provide a secondary transduction pathway at extreme
intensities remains an open question, as they operate by quasi-static compliance
rather than cavity resonance and are explored separately.

Future work should verify the boundary condition assumptions through finite
element analysis of anatomically realistic geometries, investigate the role of
bowel gas pockets as localised acoustic transducers, and extend the model into
the nonlinear regime relevant to high-amplitude mechanical excitation.
