\begin{highlights}
\item Transfer-function contrast, not a single scenario ratio, explains vibration--sound asymmetry
\item Oblate spheroidal shell model predicts abdominal resonance at 4--10~Hz
\item Breathing mode at $\sim$2500~Hz; flexural modes at infrasonic frequencies
\item Airborne coupling penalised by $(ka)^n$; negligible at 120~dB SPL
\item Monte Carlo UQ shows the transfer-function contrast remains decisive; Sobol $S_T = 0.86$ for modulus
\end{highlights}

\begin{abstract}
Whole-body vibration at \SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} causes well-documented
gastrointestinal disturbances, yet airborne sound at the same
frequencies does not---even at extreme intensities.  This asymmetry
underlies the persistent ``brown note'' hypothesis: the conjecture that
infrasonic sound can induce involuntary defecation.  We resolve the
asymmetry by modelling the human abdomen as a fluid-filled viscoelastic
oblate spheroidal shell and quantifying both excitation pathways within
a common energy-budget framework.

The model predicts flexural modes ($n \geq 2$) at \SIrange{4}{10}{\hertz},
consistent with ISO~2631 abdominal resonance data.  At these frequencies the
abdomen lies deep in the Rayleigh regime ($ka \approx 0.01$); the resulting
$(ka)^n$ spatial-filtering penalty suppresses modal-pressure content by
four orders of magnitude, while the air--tissue impedance mismatch limits
energy transfer to the shell.  The corresponding airborne and mechanical
transfer functions at the $n=2$ resonance are
$H_\mathrm{air} \approx 1.4 \times 10^{-9}\,\si{\metre\per\pascal}$ and
$H_\mathrm{mech} \approx 9.17 \times 10^{-3}\,\si{\second\squared}$
(SDOF upper bound, before modal-participation correction), giving a canonical
transfer-function ratio
$H_\mathrm{mech}/H_\mathrm{air} \approx 6.5 \times 10^6\,
\si{\pascal\second\squared\per\metre}$.  Energy-consistent analysis yields wall
displacements of \SI{0.028}{\micro\metre} at \SI{120}{\decibel}
SPL---far below cellular mechanotransduction benchmarks.  For the reference
exposure pair \SI{120}{\decibel} SPL and
$a_\mathrm{rms} = \SI{0.1}{\metre\per\second\squared}$, this transfer-function
contrast implies a displacement ratio of approximately
$3.3 \times 10^4$.  Monte Carlo analysis spanning representative anatomical
variability confirms that the transfer-function contrast remains decisive
throughout the parameter space, with the fundamental flexural frequency varying
between 3 and \SI{16}{\hertz} (90\% credible interval, median
\SI{6.4}{\hertz}; $S_T = 0.86$ for elastic modulus).  The global
abdominal-cavity resonance is physically real, but airborne sound at any
plausible intensity cannot excite it to physiologically relevant amplitudes.
\end{abstract}
