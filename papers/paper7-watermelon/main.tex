\begin{abstract}
The `thump test''---tapping a watermelon and listening for a
characteristic tone---is practised worldwide as an informal guide to
eating quality, but its directly observable output is mechanical rather
than biological.  We model the watermelon as a fluid-filled
viscoelastic shell using an equivalent-sphere approximation: the oblate
geometry is captured through the equivalent-volume radius
$R_{\mathrm{eq}} = (a^{2}c)^{1/3}$, and the eigenfrequency is derived
from classical thin-shell theory applied to a sphere of that radius.
Sobol sensitivity analysis ($N_{\mathrm{base}}(D{+}2) = 5{,}632$
evaluations) reveals that the rind elastic modulus
$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ dominates the frequency response (total-order
index $S_T = 0.54 \pm 0.05$).  Because $f_2^2$ is linear in
$E_{\mathrm{rind}}$ when turgor pressure is negligible, the forward
model admits a closed-form inversion: a single tap-tone measurement,
combined with knowledge of fruit geometry, yields an estimate of the
\emph{effective rind stiffness parameter}.  This is a mechanics-based
modulus estimate, not a direct biological quality label; any mapping from
stiffness to maturity, eating quality, or commercial grading requires
cultivar-specific calibration against destructive reference data, which
has not yet been demonstrated experimentally.  The condition number of
the frequency-to-modulus inversion is unity with respect to $f^2$ and
approximately~2 with respect to measured frequency~$f$.  As a
secondary result, a dimensionless geometric invariant
$\Pi_{\mathrm{geom}}$ collapses predictions across cultivar geometries
onto a narrow band ($0.062 \pm 0.002$), supporting cultivar-independent
stiffness calibration within the model assumptions.  Predictions are
benchmarked against published resonance frequency ranges for
watermelons; these comparisons represent consistency checks against
literature data rather than controlled experimental validation.
\end{abstract}
