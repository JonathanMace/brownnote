\begin{abstract}
Whole-body acoustic models predict negligible tissue displacement from
airborne infrasound, yet gastrointestinal (GI) discomfort is anecdotally
reported at high sound pressure levels (SPL).  We develop a first-principles
model to investigate whether \emph{intraluminal gas pockets} could act as
impedance-matching transducers, converting pressure oscillations to local
wall strain more efficiently than whole-cavity resonance.  A
constrained-bubble model---Minnaert dynamics modified for the elastic
intestinal wall and cylindrical lumen geometry---predicts that gas pockets of
\SIrange{5}{100}{\milli\litre} have resonant frequencies from tens to
hundreds of hertz, depending on geometry, but sub-resonantly amplify tissue
displacement as compliant inclusions in an otherwise stiff medium.  Under
idealised sealed-pocket conditions, the model predicts SPL thresholds for
illustrative mechanotransduction activation ranging from
\SI{111}{\decibel} (\SI{100}{\milli\litre} pocket) to \SI{120}{\decibel}
(\SI{5}{\milli\litre}).  However, the analysis identifies acoustic
short-circuiting via the GI air column as a critical limitation: at
\SI{7}{\hertz}, pressure equalisation attenuates the effective driving
pressure by up to 83\%, potentially reducing all displacements below
activation thresholds.  Whether this short circuit is suppressed
\emph{in~vivo}---by mucosal folds, liquid plugs, or luminal
collapse---remains the key unknown.  A sensitivity analysis
($N = 10\,000$ Monte Carlo samples) identifies gas pocket volume and
acoustic sealing as the dominant parameters.  We define the experimental
measurements needed to test this hypothesis, including \emph{in~vivo}
pocket impedance characterisation and tissue-level mechanotransduction
thresholds.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:}
infrasound, bowel gas, constrained bubble, mechanotransduction,
\piezo{} channels, gastrointestinal acoustics, Minnaert resonance,
hypothesis, acoustic short circuit

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The so-called ``brown note''---a hypothetical infrasonic frequency that
induces involuntary bowel movements---has persisted in popular culture and
military speculation for decades, despite a lack of credible experimental
evidence \citep{Jauchem2007,Leventhall2007,SouthPark2000BrownNoise,MythBusters2005BrownNote}.  Early aerospace-medicine
studies documented nausea, abdominal vibration, and vague GI discomfort in
subjects exposed to high-intensity infrasound (\SI{>140}{\decibel}), but
attributed these effects primarily to whole-body vibration rather than
airborne acoustic coupling \citep{Mohr1965,vonGierke1974}.

Our companion study \citep{Mace2026browntone} showed that
airborne infrasound cannot excite abdominal-cavity flexural resonances at
physiologically meaningful amplitudes.  The breathing mode of the
abdomen---often conflated with the brown-note myth in popular accounts---lies at
$\sim$\SI{2490}{\hertz}, far above the infrasound range.  The relevant
flexural modes ($n \ge 2$) do fall at \SIrange{4}{10}{\hertz}, but the
$(ka)^n$ coupling penalty renders airborne excitation negligible: the
required SPL for \SI{1}{\um} tissue displacement exceeds
\SI{180}{\decibel}.

However, the whole-cavity analysis treats the abdomen as a homogeneous
elastic shell filled with incompressible fluid.  This neglects a critical
feature: the human gastrointestinal tract normally contains
\SIrange{100}{200}{\milli\litre} of gas distributed in multiple discrete
pockets \citep{Levitt1971,Suarez1997}, with pathological or post-prandial
values reaching \SIrange{500}{1500}{\milli\litre}
\citep{Bedell1956,Serra2001}.  These gas pockets constitute
\emph{compressible inclusions} within an otherwise nearly incompressible
medium, creating local impedance mismatches of $\sim 3600{:}1$ at each
gas--tissue interface.

We hypothesise that bowel gas pockets could function as \emph{acoustic
transducers}: the spatially uniform infrasonic pressure field would drive
radial oscillation of each gas--tissue interface, converting acoustic
pressure to local wall strain.  This mechanism would be fundamentally
different from---and potentially far more efficient than---whole-cavity
resonance, because:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Gas compressibility provides a compliance that tissue lacks;
  \item The transduction is broadband (not limited to a single resonance);
  \item The displacement is concentrated at the gas--tissue boundary, where
        mechanosensitive ion channels (\piezo1, \piezo2) are expressed in
        enterochromaffin cells and enteric neurons
        \citep{Coste2010,Alcaino2017,Zeitzschel2024}.
\end{enumerate}

In this paper we develop a constrained-bubble model for intraluminal gas
pockets, estimate the conditions under which \piezo{} channel activation
thresholds could potentially be reached, identify the acoustic
short-circuit as the critical limiting factor, and use sensitivity analysis
to determine which parameters most affect the outcome.  The aim is not to
demonstrate that this mechanism operates \emph{in~vivo}, but to establish a
theoretical framework, identify the key unknowns, and define the
experimental measurements that would resolve the question.

\section{Model}

\subsection{Geometry and assumptions}
\label{sec:geometry}

We model a single gas pocket as a compressible inclusion within the
intestinal lumen, surrounded by an elastic wall of thickness $h_w$,
Young's modulus $E_w$, Poisson's ratio $\nu_w$, and density $\rho_w$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}).  The surrounding tissue is modelled as an
incompressible fluid of density $\rho_f$ at infrasound wavelengths
($\lambda \approx \SI{50}{\metre}$ at \SI{7}{\hertz}, $\gg$
body dimension ${\sim}\SI{0.3}{\metre}$).

Two idealized geometries bracket the range of real pocket shapes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Spherical:} radius
        $a = (3V/4\pi)^{1/3}$, appropriate for isolated gas pockets in
        distended segments.
  \item \textbf{Cylindrical:} fixed lumen radius
        $R = \SI{15}{\milli\metre}$ (small intestine), length
        $L = V/(\pi R^2)$, appropriate for elongated gas slugs.
\end{itemize}

Canonical parameter values are listed in Table~\ref{tab:params}.

\subsection{Resonance frequency}
\label{sec:resonance}

\subsubsection{Spherical pocket: modified Minnaert frequency}

The classical Minnaert frequency for a free spherical gas bubble of
radius~$a$ in an infinite liquid is \citep{Minnaert1933}:
\begin{equation}
  f_M = \frac{1}{2\pi a}\sqrt{\frac{3\gamma P_0}{\rho_f}}.
  \label{eq:minnaert}
\end{equation}

For a pocket enclosed by an elastic shell (the intestinal wall), both the
gas stiffness and the wall stiffness contribute, while the inertia includes
both the radiation added mass of the surrounding fluid and the shell mass
\citep{Church1995,Hoff2000}.  Expressing all quantities as stiffness and
inertia per unit radial displacement:
\begin{equation}
  f_0^\text{(sph)} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{
    \frac{k_\text{gas} + k_\text{wall}}{m_\text{fluid} + m_\text{wall}}
  },
  \label{eq:f0_sphere}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{align}
  k_\text{gas}  &= \frac{3\gamma P_0}{a}, &
  k_\text{wall} &= \frac{2\,E_w\,h_w}{a^2\,(1-\nu_w)}, \label{eq:stiffness_sphere}\\
  m_\text{fluid} &= \rho_f\,a, &
  m_\text{wall}  &= \rho_w\,h_w. \label{eq:mass_sphere}
\end{align}
The gas stiffness arises from adiabatic compression of the enclosed gas
(Boyle's law linearised about equilibrium radius~$a$); the wall stiffness
from hoop stress in a thin spherical shell; and the inertia terms from
radiation loading and shell mass per unit area, respectively.
In the limit $h_w \to 0$ (free bubble), $k_\text{wall}$ and
$m_\text{wall}$ vanish and Eq.~\eqref{eq:f0_sphere} reduces exactly to
the Minnaert result, Eq.~\eqref{eq:minnaert}:
\begin{equation*}
  f_0 \to \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{3\gamma P_0 / a}{\rho_f\,a}}
       = \frac{1}{2\pi a}\sqrt{\frac{3\gamma P_0}{\rho_f}}
       = f_M.
\end{equation*}
For the soft intestinal wall ($E_w = \SI{10}{\kilo\pascal}$),
$k_\text{wall}$ contributes only 0.9--2.4\% of the total effective stiffness
$k_\text{gas} + k_\text{wall}$ across the physiological pocket-size range;
gas compressibility dominates.  The wall mass $m_\text{wall} = \rho_w h_w$
adds ${\sim}20\%$ to the fluid inertia, which lowers $f_0$ slightly below
$f_M$.  The ``constrained'' label therefore refers to the geometric
confinement of the gas pocket within the tubular lumen, not to a significant
elastic restraint by the wall.

\subsubsection{Cylindrical pocket: radial and axial modes}

For an elongated gas column in an elastic tube of radius~$R$, the lowest
\emph{radial} (breathing) mode has frequency:
\begin{equation}
  f_0^\text{(cyl,rad)} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{
    \frac{2\gamma P_0 / R + E_w h_w / [R^2(1-\nu_w^2)]}{\rho_f R + \rho_w h_w}
  }.
  \label{eq:f0_cyl_rad}
\end{equation}
The factor of 2 (vs.\ 3 for the sphere) reflects the cylindrical geometry.

The \emph{axial} (piston) mode treats the gas slug as a spring with
stiffness $k = \gamma P_0 \pi R^2 / L$ and radiation mass
$m_\text{end} = \tfrac{8}{3}\rho_f R^3$ at each open end
\citep{Leighton1994}:
\begin{equation}
  f_0^\text{(cyl,ax)} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{
    \frac{\gamma P_0\,\pi R^2}{2L \cdot \tfrac{8}{3}\rho_f R^3}
  }.
  \label{eq:f0_cyl_ax}
\end{equation}

The lowest resonance for a cylindrical pocket is
$f_0 = \min(f_0^\text{(cyl,rad)},\, f_0^\text{(cyl,ax)})$.

For physiological gas pocket volumes (\SIrange{1}{100}{\milli\litre}),
resonance frequencies range from $\sim$\SI{30}{\hertz} (large cylindrical
pockets, axial mode) to $\sim$\SI{440}{\hertz} (small spherical pockets
with elastic wall).  Because wall stiffness adds little and wall mass
lowers $f_0$ below the free-bubble Minnaert value, the constrained
spherical frequencies are roughly 89--96\% of~$f_M$ for clinically
relevant volumes of \SIrange{5}{100}{\milli\litre}
(Table~\ref{tab:freq}).  All resonances remain well above
\SI{20}{\hertz}---i.e.\ above the conventional infrasound boundary.  This
is a critical distinction from earlier analyses that sought resonance
\emph{at} infrasound frequencies.


% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Damping}
\label{sec:damping}

The total damping ratio is
\begin{equation}
  \zeta = \tfrac{1}{2}(\delta_\text{rad} + \delta_\text{th}
          + \delta_\text{vis} + \delta_\text{wall}),
  \label{eq:damping}
\end{equation}
with contributions from:
\begin{align}
  \delta_\text{rad}  &= \frac{\omega a}{c_f}
    && \text{(radiation into surrounding tissue)}, \\
  \delta_\text{th}   &\approx 0.04
    && \text{(thermal conduction in gas)}, \\
  \delta_\text{vis}  &= \frac{4\mu}{\rho_f\,\omega\,a^2}
    && \text{(viscous boundary layer)}, \\
  \delta_\text{wall} &\approx 0.3
    && \text{(structural loss in intestinal wall \citep{Fung1993})}.
\end{align}


% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Forced response: sub-resonant transduction}
\label{sec:forced}

The key mechanism is not \emph{resonance} but \emph{compliance}: gas
pockets respond to the incident pressure because they are compressible,
even far below their resonant frequency.  The radial displacement of the
gas--tissue interface under a spatially uniform incident pressure
$p_\text{inc}$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \xi = \frac{p_\text{inc}}{k_\text{eff}} \cdot H(\omega),
  \label{eq:forced}
\end{equation}
where $k_\text{eff}$ is the effective radial stiffness (gas plus wall
contributions) and $H(\omega)$ is the standard single-degree-of-freedom
frequency response:
\begin{equation}
  H(\omega) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(1 - r^2)^2 + (2\zeta r)^2}}, \qquad
  r = \frac{\omega}{\omega_0} = \frac{f}{f_0}.
  \label{eq:H}
\end{equation}

In the sub-resonant regime ($f \ll f_0$, so $r \ll 1$), $H \to 1$ and the
displacement becomes frequency-independent:
\begin{equation}
  \xi_\text{sub-res} \approx \frac{p_\text{inc}}{k_\text{eff}}.
  \label{eq:subresonant}
\end{equation}

For a spherical pocket with elastic wall:
\begin{equation}
  k_\text{eff}^\text{(sph)} = \frac{3\gamma P_0}{a}
    + \frac{2\,E_w\,h_w}{a^2(1-\nu_w)}.
  \label{eq:keff_sphere}
\end{equation}

For a cylindrical pocket:
\begin{equation}
  k_\text{eff}^\text{(cyl)} = \frac{2\gamma P_0}{R}
    + \frac{E_w\,h_w}{R^2(1-\nu_w^2)}.
  \label{eq:keff_cyl}
\end{equation}

The incident pressure at SPL~$S$ (in \si{\decibel} re \SI{20}{\micro\pascal}) is:
\begin{equation}
  p_\text{inc} = p_\text{ref} \times 10^{S/20},
  \qquad p_\text{ref} = \SI{20}{\micro\pascal}.
  \label{eq:spl}
\end{equation}


% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Volume displacement and tissue coupling}
\label{sec:coupling}

The oscillating gas--tissue interface radiates displacement into the
surrounding tissue as a monopole source.  The volume displacement is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V =
  \begin{cases}
    4\pi a^2\,\xi & \text{(spherical)}, \\
    2\pi R\,L\,\xi & \text{(cylindrical, radial mode)}.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:dV}
\end{equation}

In the near field, the tissue displacement at distance $r$ from the pocket
centre decays as:
\begin{equation}
  u(r) \approx \left(\frac{a}{r}\right)^{\!2} \xi, \qquad r > a.
  \label{eq:monopole}
\end{equation}

This concentrates the acoustic displacement at the gas--tissue boundary,
precisely where \piezo{} channels are located in the intestinal wall
\citep{Alcaino2017,Mazzuoli2015}.


% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Illustrative mechanotransduction comparison}
\label{sec:piezo}

To place the predicted displacements in a biological context, we compare
them against the activation threshold of \piezo2 mechanosensitive ion
channels.  \citet{Coste2010} reported membrane indentation thresholds of
\SIrange{0.5}{2.0}{\um} in patch-clamp experiments on isolated cells;
we adopt $\xi_\text{PIEZO} = \SI{0.5}{\um}$ as an illustrative lower
bound.

We emphasise that this comparison is presented to establish
\emph{order-of-magnitude plausibility}, not quantitative equivalence.
The patch-clamp threshold reflects membrane deformation of individual cells
under controlled conditions; the bulk tissue wall displacement computed by
the constrained-bubble model involves a fundamentally different strain
field.  Resolving whether gas-pocket wall displacement can activate
\piezo{} channels \emph{in~situ} requires tissue-level mechanotransduction
measurements that do not currently exist (see \S\ref{sec:experiments}).

Subject to this caveat, the minimum SPL for which the model predicts
$\xi = \xi_\text{PIEZO}$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{SPL}_\text{thresh} = 20\log_{10}\!\left(
    \frac{\xi_\text{PIEZO}\,k_\text{eff}}{p_\text{ref}\,H(\omega)}
  \right).
  \label{eq:spl_threshold}
\end{equation}


% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Sensitivity analysis: population variability}
\label{sec:montecarlo}

Inter-individual variability in bowel gas content is substantial
\citep{Levitt1971,Bedell1956,Serra2001}.  To identify which parameters
most affect the predicted displacement, we perform a Monte Carlo
sensitivity analysis with $N = 10\,000$ sampled individuals, each
characterised by:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Total bowel gas volume drawn from a log-normal distribution with
        median \SI{200}{\milli\litre} and $\sigma_{\ln} = 0.65$, giving a
        95\% CI of approximately \SIrange{56}{715}{\milli\litre} and a
        heavy right tail extending to $\sim$\SI{1500}{\milli\litre};
  \item Number of discrete pockets $N_p \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda=10)$,
        clipped to $[2,\,40]$;
  \item Per-pocket volumes drawn from a symmetric Dirichlet partition of the
        total volume;
  \item Geometry randomly assigned (70\% cylindrical, 30\% spherical).
\end{itemize}

For each sampled individual, the maximum pocket-wall displacement at
\SI{7}{\hertz} and \SI{120}{\decibel} is computed under the idealised
sealed-pocket assumption.  This analysis identifies the sensitivity of the
outcome to the assumed input distributions; it does not constitute
independent validation of the mechanism (see \S\ref{sec:res_sensitivity}).

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Resonance frequencies are above infrasound}
\label{sec:res_freq}

Table~\ref{tab:freq} summarizes the resonance frequencies for
representative gas pocket sizes.  Spherical pocket resonances range from
approximately \SI{108}{\hertz} at \SI{100}{\milli\litre} to
\SI{438}{\hertz} at \SI{1}{\milli\litre}, and the cylindrical radial
(breathing) mode lies at $\sim$\SI{162}{\hertz}.  However, cylindrical
\emph{axial} (piston) modes fall below \SI{100}{\hertz} for pockets larger
than $\sim$\SI{10}{\milli\litre}, reaching \SI{31}{\hertz} at
\SI{100}{\milli\litre}---near, but still above, the conventional infrasound
boundary (\SI{20}{\hertz}).  None of the pockets resonate \emph{at}
infrasound frequencies; the sub-resonant compliance mechanism described
below operates efficiently across the entire infrasound band.

\subsection{SPL thresholds under idealised conditions}
\label{sec:res_spl}

Figure~\ref{fig:spl_threshold} shows the SPL predicted by the model to
produce \SI{0.5}{\um} wall displacement as a function of gas pocket volume,
\emph{assuming acoustically sealed pockets} (i.e.\ no pressure
equalisation via the luminal air column; see \S\ref{sec:short_circuit}
for the effect of relaxing this assumption).  Under these idealised
conditions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item For \textbf{spherical} pockets, the predicted threshold decreases
        from $\sim$\SI{120}{\decibel} at \SI{5}{\milli\litre} to
        $\sim$\SI{111}{\decibel} at \SI{100}{\milli\litre}.
  \item For \textbf{cylindrical} pockets, the predicted threshold is nearly
        independent of volume ($\sim$\SI{114}{\decibel}), because the
        lumen radius~$R$ is fixed.
  \item The 9-dB range across physiological pocket sizes maps directly
        to the range of inter-individual gas content.
\end{itemize}
These values represent an \emph{upper bound} on the mechanism's
efficiency.  As discussed in \S\ref{sec:short_circuit}, acoustic
short-circuiting can substantially reduce the effective driving pressure,
raising the true thresholds above these sealed-pocket estimates.

\subsection{Sensitivity analysis results}
\label{sec:res_sensitivity}

Figure~\ref{fig:population} presents the Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis
for $N = 10\,000$ sampled individuals at \SI{120}{\decibel}, under the
sealed-pocket assumption.  Under these idealised conditions, the model
predicts that nearly all sampled individuals would exceed the illustrative
\SI{0.5}{\um} displacement threshold.  This high exceedance fraction is
a direct consequence of the input assumptions rather than an independent
prediction: the model assigns every individual at
least \SI{30}{\milli\litre} of bowel gas (a physiologically conservative
lower bound), and even a \SI{3}{\milli\litre} cylindrical pocket produces $\xi >
\SI{0.5}{\um}$ at \SI{120}{\decibel} in the sealed-pocket limit.

The value of this analysis lies not in the exceedance fraction itself---which
is tautological given the assumed distributions---but in identifying the
\emph{sensitivity} of the outcome to individual parameters.  Gas pocket
volume emerges as the dominant factor: the distribution of maximum
pocket-wall displacement spans approximately
\SIrange{1.03}{2.70}{\um}, with a median of $\sim$\SI{1.08}{\um},
tracking the log-normal gas volume distribution closely.
Individuals in the upper tail ($>$\SI{500}{\milli\litre} total gas)
have predicted displacements exceeding \SI{2}{\um}.
Crucially, however, these values assume perfect acoustic sealing.
If the short-circuit attenuation factor of ${\sim}0.17$ (see
\S\ref{sec:short_circuit}) is applied, the median displacement falls
to $\sim$\SI{0.18}{\um}---well below the illustrative \piezo{}
threshold.  The mechanism therefore operates only if gas pockets
are acoustically sealed, making pocket sealing the single most
important parameter in the model.

\subsection{Comparison with whole-cavity resonance}
\label{sec:res_comparison}

Figure~\ref{fig:comparison} compares the gas-pocket transduction pathway
with the whole-cavity flexural mode ($n = 2$) pathway from
\citet{Mace2026browntone}, both evaluated under their respective idealised
assumptions.  Under the sealed-pocket assumption, the gas-pocket mechanism
predicts tissue displacement that is \textbf{35--100$\times$ larger} than
the whole-cavity pathway at the same SPL, across the entire range from
\SI{80}{\decibel} to \SI{150}{\decibel}.

At \SI{120}{\decibel}, the whole-cavity $n = 2$ mode yields
$\xi \approx \SI{0.014}{\um}$ (energy-consistent;
\citealp{Mace2026browntone})---more than an order of magnitude below the
illustrative \piezo{} threshold.  In contrast, a \SI{100}{\milli\litre}
sealed gas pocket yields $\xi \approx \SI{1.3}{\um}$.  If acoustic
short-circuit attenuation is applied (factor ${\sim}0.17$ at
\SI{7}{\hertz}), this drops to $\sim$\SI{0.22}{\um}---below the
illustrative threshold, but still an order of magnitude above the
whole-cavity pathway.  The gas-pocket mechanism thus remains the more
efficient of the two pathways regardless of the sealing assumption, but
whether either pathway reaches biologically relevant amplitudes is an
open question.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Gas pockets as candidate impedance-matching transducers}

The central hypothesis of this paper is that intraluminal gas pockets could
serve as \emph{impedance-matching acoustic transducers} between the airborne
pressure field and the intestinal wall.  The proposed mechanism is not resonance
enhancement---the gas-pocket resonances still lie well above the
\SI{7}{\hertz} driving frequency, at roughly \SIrange{31}{438}{\hertz}
across the cases studied---but rather the \emph{compliance mismatch} between the
compressible gas and the surrounding incompressible tissue.

At infrasound wavelengths ($\lambda \gg$ body size), the incident pressure
is spatially uniform throughout the abdomen.  In incompressible tissue,
this uniform pressure produces negligible strain (bulk modulus
$\sim$\SI{2.2}{\giga\pascal}).  But at a gas--tissue interface, the gas
compresses while the tissue does not, creating a displacement discontinuity
that scales as $\xi \propto p_\text{inc}/k_\text{eff}$.  This is
fundamentally the same mechanism by which the middle ear's ossicular chain
provides impedance matching between air and the cochlear fluid
\citep{Junger1986}, albeit at much lower frequencies and without mechanical
advantage.


% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Acoustic short-circuit: the critical limitation}
\label{sec:short_circuit}

The most important finding of this analysis is the identification of the
\emph{acoustic short-circuit} as the critical factor that determines
whether the proposed mechanism can operate.

The GI tract is an open tube (mouth to anus, total length
$\sim$\SI{8}{\metre}).  At infrasound wavelengths
($\lambda = c_\text{air}/f \approx \SI{49}{\metre}$ at \SI{7}{\hertz}),
$\lambda \gg L_\text{GI}$, so luminal air could in principle deliver the
same incident pressure $p_\text{inc}$ to the \emph{interior} of a gas
pocket as tissue delivers to its \emph{exterior}.  If both paths transmit
$p_\text{inc}$ faithfully, the net pressure difference across the
gas--tissue interface vanishes: $\Delta P \to 0$, and no displacement
results---an \emph{acoustic short circuit}.

We estimate the cross-over frequency by modelling the open GI tract as a
Helmholtz resonator.  The luminal gas volume $V$ communicates with the
exterior through a constricted air column of cross-section $S$ and
effective acoustic length $L_\text{eff}$.  The Helmholtz resonance
frequency is
\begin{equation}
  f_H = \frac{c_\text{air}}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{S}{V\,L_\text{eff}}}.
  \label{eq:helmholtz}
\end{equation}
For representative values ($V \approx \SI{200}{\milli\litre}$,
$S \approx \pi(\SI{5}{\milli\metre})^2$, $L_\text{eff} \approx
\SI{5}{\metre}$), this gives $f_H \approx \SI{15}{\hertz}$.
Below $f_H$, the air column can quasi-statically equalise pressure,
partially short-circuiting the transduction mechanism; above $f_H$,
air-column inertia prevents equalisation and the constrained-bubble model
applies without qualification.

At \SI{7}{\hertz} ($r = f/f_H \approx 0.47$), the equalisation parameter
$E = 1/[1 + (f/f_H)^2] \approx 0.83$, indicating that ${\sim}83\%$ of the
exterior pressure is transmitted to the pocket interior, leaving only
${\sim}17\%$ of $p_\text{inc}$ as the effective driving pressure.  This
attenuation is devastating for the mechanism: the strongest pocket
considered (\SI{100}{\milli\litre}, spherical, $\xi = \SI{1.35}{\um}$
under sealed conditions) drops to $\xi \approx \SI{0.23}{\um}$---below the
illustrative \piezo{} threshold of \SI{0.5}{\um}.

We note that Eq.~\eqref{eq:helmholtz} is used here only to estimate
$f_H$ as a cross-over scale; the implied low-pass attenuation model
$1/[1+(f/f_H)^2]$ is a simplification of the full Helmholtz transfer
function.  A true Helmholtz resonator
exhibits amplification near $f_H$ and a steeper roll-off above it;
moreover, the effective path length and aperture diameter governing
$f_H$ are poorly constrained anatomically.
The true equalisation may therefore be \emph{worse} (${\sim}90\%$) than
the 83\% estimated here, which would strengthen rather than weaken
the short-circuit conclusion.  This warrants future investigation
with anatomically informed impedance models.

\subsubsection{Bounded analysis: sealed vs.\ open pockets}

The results presented in \S\ref{sec:results} assume acoustically sealed
pockets (no interior pressure equalisation).  This represents an
\emph{upper bound} on the mechanism's efficiency.  The short-circuit
analysis above provides an approximate \emph{lower bound} for open
pockets.  The true situation lies between these extremes, and
depends critically on the degree of acoustic sealing \emph{in~vivo}.

We acknowledge a tension in the boundary conditions employed in this paper.
The cylindrical axial (piston) resonance mode
(Eq.~\ref{eq:f0_cyl_ax}) assumes open tube ends (pressure-release
boundaries), which is appropriate for computing resonance frequencies of a
gas slug communicating with adjacent fluid.  However, the defence against
the acoustic short-circuit relies on pockets being sealed by liquid
or fecal plugs.  These are mutually contradictory assumptions applied in
different parts of the analysis to obtain favourable results in each case.
We present both cases transparently as bounds rather than claiming one
is correct:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Sealed pocket (upper bound):} The luminal air path is
        blocked by chyme, mucus, or fecal plugs.  The constrained-bubble
        model applies in full.  Imaging studies confirm that gas is
        typically distributed in discrete, separated pockets rather than a
        single connected volume \citep{Levitt1971,Serra2001}, and healthy
        fasting individuals harbour 5--15 discrete pockets separated by
        liquid or collapsed lumen \citep{Suarez1997}.  Under this
        assumption, the maximum displacement for a \SI{100}{\milli\litre}
        pocket at \SI{120}{\decibel} is $\xi \approx \SI{1.35}{\um}$.

  \item \textbf{Open pocket (lower bound):} The pocket communicates
        freely with the GI air column.  The short-circuit transfer
        function attenuates the effective driving pressure by
        ${\sim}83\%$ at \SI{7}{\hertz}, reducing the displacement to
        $\xi \approx \SI{0.23}{\um}$, below the illustrative \piezo{}
        threshold.
\end{itemize}

The acoustic sealing of gas pockets \emph{in~vivo} is therefore the single
most important parameter in determining whether this mechanism operates.
No published measurements of \emph{in~vivo} pocket impedance or acoustic
transmission through intestinal liquid plugs exist.  Obtaining such
measurements is the highest-priority experimental requirement identified by
this analysis (see \S\ref{sec:experiments}).


% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Clinical implications (conditional)}

If the gas-pocket mechanism operates \emph{in~vivo} (i.e.\ if pockets are
sufficiently sealed), the model predicts that individuals with elevated
bowel gas would be more susceptible to infrasound-induced GI effects.
Several clinical populations have chronically elevated intestinal gas:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS):} Patients with IBS
        exhibit altered gas handling and increased gas retention
        \citep{Serra2001}, as well as visceral hypersensitivity
        that may lower the effective \piezo{} threshold.
  \item \textbf{Post-prandial states:} Gas production increases
        significantly after meals, particularly with fermentable substrates,
        transiently increasing both total gas volume and the number of
        discrete pockets \citep{Harder2003}.
  \item \textbf{Aerophagia:} Excessive air swallowing introduces large
        gas volumes into the upper GI tract.
  \item \textbf{Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO):}
        Excess fermentation produces elevated luminal gas.
\end{itemize}

This framework would naturally explain the sporadic and individually variable
nature of reported infrasound effects: susceptibility would depend on a
physiological parameter (gas content and pocket sealing) that varies by up
to 15$\times$ across the population and fluctuates within individuals on
hourly timescales.  We stress, however, that these clinical implications
remain contingent on the mechanism operating at all, which requires
experimental resolution of the acoustic sealing question.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Comparison with established bubble dynamics}

Our constrained-bubble model builds on the extensive literature on
encapsulated microbubbles used as ultrasound contrast agents
\citep{Church1995,Hoff2000}.  The key differences are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Scale:} Ultrasound contrast agents are
        $\sim$\SIrange{1}{10}{\um} in diameter; bowel gas pockets are
        \SIrange{1}{60}{\milli\metre}.
  \item \textbf{Wall properties:} Contrast agent shells are polymeric
        ($E \sim \SI{1}{\giga\pascal}$); the intestinal wall is a soft
        biological tissue ($E \sim \SI{10}{\kilo\pascal}$).
  \item \textbf{Frequency regime:} Contrast agents are driven near
        resonance (\SIrange{1}{10}{\mega\hertz}); bowel gas pockets
        are driven far below resonance ($f/f_0 \sim 0.01$--$0.05$).
\end{enumerate}

Despite these differences, the underlying physics is identical:
pressure-driven radial oscillation of a compressible inclusion in an
elastic medium.  The sub-resonant regime simply replaces the
resonance-amplified response with a quasi-static compliance-dominated
response.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Limitations and key unknowns}
\label{sec:limitations}

Beyond the acoustic short-circuit discussed in \S\ref{sec:short_circuit},
several additional simplifications warrant discussion:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Linear model:} We assume small-amplitude oscillations.
        At very high SPL ($>\SI{140}{\decibel}$), nonlinear effects
        (rectified diffusion, microstreaming) may become relevant.
  \item \textbf{Idealised geometries:} Real gas pockets have irregular
        shapes.  Our spherical and cylindrical models bracket the
        physiological range but do not capture complex topologies.
        The cylindrical breathing-mode model assumes $L/R \geq 1$;
        for pockets smaller than $\sim$\SI{10}{\milli\litre} in a
        \SI{15}{\milli\metre} lumen, the spherical model is more
        appropriate as the pocket geometry becomes disc-like.
  \item \textbf{Quasi-static tissue response:} The monopole coupling
        model (Eq.~\ref{eq:monopole}) neglects viscoelastic relaxation,
        which would reduce the displacement at longer distances.
  \item \textbf{Threshold category error:} As discussed in
        \S\ref{sec:piezo}, the \piezo{} activation threshold of
        \SI{0.5}{\um} derives from \emph{in~vitro} patch-clamp experiments
        on isolated cells and measures membrane indentation, not bulk tissue
        wall displacement.  These are fundamentally different strain fields,
        and the comparison is presented as illustrative only.  The
        \emph{in~vivo} threshold for \piezo{} activation by gas-pocket
        wall oscillation is unknown.
  \item \textbf{No experimental validation:} The model predictions remain
        entirely untested.  The theoretical framework identifies what
        measurements are needed (\S\ref{sec:experiments}), but until those
        measurements are performed, the mechanism remains a hypothesis.
  \item \textbf{Boundary condition inconsistency:} As noted in
        \S\ref{sec:short_circuit}, the axial resonance mode assumes open
        ends while the short-circuit defence assumes sealed pockets.  We
        present both cases as bounds rather than resolving this tension,
        which requires experimental data.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experimental requirements}
\label{sec:experiments}

The theoretical framework developed above identifies three critical
measurements that would resolve whether the gas-pocket mechanism operates
\emph{in~vivo}.  We present these in order of priority.

\subsection{In-vivo pocket impedance and acoustic sealing}
\label{sec:exp_impedance}

The analysis identifies pocket acoustic sealing as the single most
important determinant of whether the mechanism can operate.  The required
measurement is the acoustic transmission coefficient through the liquid
or fecal plugs that separate gas pockets from the continuous GI air
column.

\textbf{Proposed approach:} Simultaneous manometry (intraluminal pressure)
at two locations flanking a liquid plug, with controlled acoustic
excitation at infrasound frequencies (\SIrange{1}{20}{\hertz}).  The
ratio of transmitted to incident pressure amplitude as a function of
frequency would directly determine the equalisation parameter~$E$ and
hence the effective short-circuit attenuation.

\textbf{Minimum requirement:} If the measured attenuation through a
typical liquid plug exceeds \SI{15}{\decibel} at \SI{7}{\hertz}
(i.e.\ $<18\%$ pressure transmission), the sealed-pocket assumption
is justified and the constrained-bubble model applies in full.  If
the attenuation is less than \SI{5}{\decibel}, the short circuit is
dominant and the mechanism is effectively suppressed.

\subsection{Tissue-level mechanotransduction thresholds}
\label{sec:exp_threshold}

The \SI{0.5}{\um} threshold used in this paper derives from single-cell
patch-clamp experiments and is not directly comparable to bulk tissue
wall displacement.  Resolving this requires \emph{in~vitro} experiments
on intestinal tissue explants (not isolated cells) subjected to
controlled oscillatory strain at infrasound frequencies.

\textbf{Proposed approach:} Excised intestinal wall segments mounted in a
perfusion chamber with piezoelectric actuators delivering controlled
sinusoidal displacement at \SIrange{1}{20}{\hertz}.  Readout via
calcium imaging or electrophysiology of the enteric nervous system
response.  The threshold displacement for detectable neural activation
would provide the tissue-level equivalent of the single-cell
\piezo{} threshold.

\subsection{Pocket geometry under acoustic excitation}
\label{sec:exp_geometry}

Real-time imaging of gas pocket geometry during infrasound exposure would
validate the assumed spherical/cylindrical models and measure actual wall
displacements.

\textbf{Proposed approach:} Dynamic MRI or high-frame-rate ultrasound
imaging of abdominal gas pockets during controlled infrasound exposure at
\SIrange{100}{120}{\decibel}.  The imaging would need temporal resolution
$\leq \SI{50}{\milli\second}$ (at least 3 frames per cycle at
\SI{7}{\hertz}) and spatial resolution $\leq \SI{0.5}{\milli\metre}$ to
detect the predicted sub-micrometre wall displacements---a significant
technical challenge that may require phase-sensitive ultrasound techniques.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This work establishes a theoretical framework for evaluating the hypothesis
that intraluminal gas pockets in the gastrointestinal tract could act as
acoustic transducers, converting airborne infrasonic pressure to local wall
strain via a sub-resonant compliance mechanism.

Under idealised sealed-pocket conditions, the constrained-bubble model
predicts tissue displacements 35--100$\times$ larger than the whole-cavity
flexural resonance pathway, with SPL thresholds for reaching an illustrative
mechanotransduction level ranging from \SI{111}{\decibel} to
\SI{120}{\decibel}.  However, the analysis identifies two critical
limitations that prevent these predictions from being presented as
established findings:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Acoustic short-circuiting} via the GI air column
        attenuates the effective driving pressure by up to 83\% at
        \SI{7}{\hertz} for open pockets, reducing all predicted
        displacements below the illustrative threshold.  The mechanism
        operates only if gas pockets are acoustically sealed by mucosal
        folds, liquid plugs, or luminal collapse---a condition that is
        physiologically plausible but experimentally uncharacterised.
  \item \textbf{The mechanotransduction comparison} between bulk tissue
        wall displacement and single-cell patch-clamp thresholds involves
        fundamentally different strain fields and is presented as
        illustrative only.
\end{enumerate}

The analysis identifies pocket acoustic sealing as the critical determinant
and defines three experimental measurements---\emph{in~vivo} pocket
impedance, tissue-level mechanotransduction thresholds, and pocket geometry
imaging under acoustic excitation---that would resolve whether this
mechanism operates.  Pending these measurements, the gas-pocket
transduction hypothesis remains the most efficient of the candidate
mechanisms analysed here for airborne-infrasound-induced GI effects, but it has not been
validated.

If the mechanism is confirmed experimentally, the model makes several
testable predictions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item SPL thresholds for GI effects should correlate with bowel gas
        content (measurable by abdominal X-ray or CT);
  \item Post-prandial subjects should be more susceptible than fasting
        subjects;
  \item Simethicone (which reduces gas bubble size) should attenuate
        effects;
  \item The effect should be broadband (not frequency-specific), with
        magnitude scaling as $p_\text{inc}/k_\text{eff}$.
\end{enumerate}
