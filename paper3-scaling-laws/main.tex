\item Under assumed allometric constraints, $\Pi_0 \approx 0.07$ is approximately conserved across mammalian scales
\item 1458-point parametric verification confirms algebraic consistency of non-dimensionalisation

\begin{abstract}
Fluid-filled viscoelastic shells arise in contexts ranging from abdominal
biomechanics to underwater acoustics.  Modal frequencies of such shells
depend on ten physical parameters, making systematic comparison across
geometries and species impractical without dimensional reduction.  We apply
Buckingham Pi analysis to the governing equations of a fluid-filled oblate
spheroidal shell, identifying eight dimensionless groups of which five
govern the undamped flexural mode frequencies.  The resulting scaling law,
$f_n = \sqrt{E/\rho_f}\,(1/a)\,\Phi_n(\Pi_1,\Pi_2,\Pi_3,\Pi_5,\Pi_6)$,
where $\Phi_n$ absorbs the $1/(2\pi)$ factor,
admits a closed-form expression from the shell equations.
A 1458-point parametric verification spanning physiological ranges of all five
governing dimensionless groups
collapses onto the analytical curves to within numerical round-off
($7.9 \times 10^{-16}$), confirming the algebraic consistency of the
non-dimensionalisation.  Cross-species predictions for four mammals (rat,
cat, pig, human), based on illustrative allometric parameters, yield
a dimensionless frequency $\Pi_0 \approx 0.07$ that is approximately
conserved under the assumed input constraints.  Because the five input
$\Pi$-groups vary only modestly across species (at most $1.7\times$ for
$P_\mathrm{iap}/E$, less for the others), this near-constancy of
$\Pi_0$ is a direct consequence of the continuity of $\Phi_n$
rather than an independent biological discovery.  It implies that the
scattering coupling ratio
$\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{scat} = 1/(kR_\mathrm{eq})^2 \sim 10^3$--$10^4$ is
approximately size-independent and
that animal models would exhibit similar scaling behaviour to the human abdomen,
provided excitation is delivered mechanically rather than acoustically.
The breathing mode ($n=0$), dominated by the fluid bulk modulus, would
require a body radius of order \SI{20}{\metre} to reach \SI{20}{\hertz}
and is therefore irrelevant to infrasound for any biological organism.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The modal frequencies of fluid-filled viscoelastic shells depend on at
least ten physical parameters: geometric (semi-axes $a$, $c$; wall
thickness $h$), material (elastic modulus $E$, Poisson's ratio $\nu$,
wall density $\rho_w$, loss tangent $\eta$), fluid ($\rho_f$, bulk
modulus $K_f$), and loading (intra-abdominal pressure $P_\mathrm{iap}$)
\cite{Soedel2004,Leissa1973,Junger1986,Junger1952,Lamb1882,Warburton1976}.  This high-dimensional
parameter space makes comparison across species, organs, and loading
conditions impractical without systematic dimensional reduction.

In a companion paper~\cite{Mace2026browntone}, we developed a fluid-filled
oblate spheroidal shell model for the human abdominal cavity, predicting
flexural resonances at \SIrange{4}{10}{\hertz} and a mechanical-to-airborne
full displacement ratio $\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{full} \approx 6.6 \times 10^4$.
That metric combines the Rayleigh scattering penalty with the air--tissue
impedance mismatch, whereas the present paper isolates only the
scattering contribution $\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{scat}$.  Here we show that
if the governing dimensionless groups take similar values across mammalian
species---as allometric constraints suggest---then the dimensionless
frequency $\Pi_0 \approx 0.07$ is approximately conserved, implying that
animal models would exhibit similar scaling behaviour and that the
scattering coupling ratio $\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{scat}$ is
approximately size-independent.  Importantly, the near-constancy of $\Pi_0$
is a consequence of the assumed input constancy, not an independent empirical
finding; the contribution of this paper is the identification of which
dimensionless groups govern abdominal resonance and the demonstration that
allometric input constraints propagate to predictable output scaling.
These results follow from a Buckingham Pi
reduction of the ten-parameter model (plus the output frequency,
giving eleven variables) to five governing dimensionless
groups for flexural modes.

Three questions motivate this analysis.  First, can animal models (rat, cat,
pig) be used to study abdominal resonance phenomena that manifest in humans?
The answer depends on whether the governing dimensionless groups---and hence
$\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{scat}$---are approximately conserved across species.  Second, can the
breathing mode ($n = 0$)---whose frequency is dominated by the fluid bulk
modulus---ever enter the infrasonic range for any biological organism?
Third, which dimensionless groups dominate the flexural resonance, and how
sensitive is $f_2$ to each?

\section{Dimensional analysis}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Governing variables}

The natural frequency $f_n$ of mode $n$ and its ten governing parameters
form a set of eleven variables:
$f_n = f(E, a, c, h, \nu, \rho_w, \rho_f, K_f, P_\mathrm{iap}, \eta)$.
With three fundamental dimensions ($M$, $L$, $T$), the Buckingham Pi
theorem~\cite{Buckingham1914} yields $11 - 3 = 8$ independent
dimensionless groups (Table~\ref{tab:pi_groups}).

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Dimensionless groups from Buckingham Pi theorem.  Groups marked
  $\ast$ drop out of the flexural-mode frequency.}
  \label{tab:pi_groups}
  \begin{tabular}{clll}
    \toprule
    Group & Definition & Name & Role \\
    \midrule
    $\Pi_0$ & $f_n\, a\sqrt{\rho_f/E}$ & dimensionless frequency & output \\
    $\Pi_1$ & $h/a$ & thickness ratio & stiffness \& mass \\
    $\Pi_2$ & $c/a$ & aspect ratio & geometry \\
    $\Pi_3$ & $\rho_w/\rho_f$ & density ratio & added mass \\
    $\Pi_4^\ast$ & $K_f/E$ & compressibility ratio & breathing mode only \\
    $\Pi_5$ & $P_\mathrm{iap}/E$ & prestress ratio & tension stiffening \\
    $\Pi_6$ & $\nu$ & Poisson's ratio & membrane coupling \\
    $\Pi_7^\ast$ & $\eta$ & loss tangent & damping only \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Reduction for flexural modes}

For flexural modes ($n \ge 2$), two simplifications apply.  The fluid bulk
modulus $K_f$ contributes no restoring force (the fluid acts as added mass
only), so $\Pi_4$ drops out; this is justified by the extreme stiffness
ratio $K_f/E \approx 2.2 \times 10^4$ (i.e.\ the fluid is approximately
$10^4$ times stiffer than the shell wall in volumetric compression), so the
fluid behaves as incompressible on the shell's flexural time scale.  The
loss tangent $\eta$ affects the quality
factor but not the undamped frequency, so $\Pi_7$ drops out.  The effective
parameter count reduces to six: a dimensionless frequency output $\Pi_0$
governed by five input groups $(\Pi_1, \Pi_2, \Pi_3, \Pi_5, \Pi_6)$.

The scaling law takes the closed form
\begin{equation}
  f_n = \sqrt{\frac{E}{\rho_f}}\,\frac{1}{a}\;
        \Phi_n\!\left(\frac{h}{a},\;\frac{c}{a},\;
        \frac{\rho_w}{\rho_f},\;\frac{P_\mathrm{iap}}{E},\;\nu\right),
  \label{eq:scaling_law}
\end{equation}
where the $1/(2\pi)$ factor is absorbed into $\Phi_n$, which admits a
closed-form expression from the equivalent-sphere shell equations:
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{
    \frac{K_{\mathrm{bend}} + K_{\mathrm{memb}} + K_{\mathrm{pre}}}
         {m_{\mathrm{eff}}}
  } \cdot a\sqrt{\frac{\rho_f}{E}},
\end{equation}
with
\begin{align}
  K_{\mathrm{bend}} &= n(n-1)(n+2)^2\,\frac{D}{R^4}, \\
  K_{\mathrm{memb}} &= \frac{Eh}{R^2}\,\lambda_n, \quad
  K_{\mathrm{pre}} = \frac{P_\mathrm{iap}}{R}(n-1)(n+2), \\
  m_{\mathrm{eff}} &= \rho_w h + \frac{\rho_f R}{n},
\end{align}
where $D = Eh^3/[12(1-\nu^2)]$ is the flexural rigidity,
$R = (a^2 c)^{1/3}$ is the equivalent-sphere radius, and
the membrane mode-shape factor is
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_n = \frac{n^2 + n - 2 + 2\nu}{n^2 + n + 1 - \nu}.
  \label{eq:lambda_n}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Sensitivity: which $\Pi$-group dominates?}

For the $n=2$ mode at canonical parameters ($h/a = 0.056$, $c/a = 0.67$,
$\nu = 0.45$, $P_\mathrm{iap}/E = 0.01$), the stiffness budget is shared
between the membrane term $K_\mathrm{memb}$ ($\sim$54\%) and the prestress
term $K_\mathrm{pre}$ ($\sim$45\%), with bending contributing less than 2\%.
The transition to bending-dominated behaviour occurs near $h/a \approx
0.42$, well above the physiological range.

Consequently, $f_2$ is most sensitive to the thickness ratio $\Pi_1 = h/a$,
which enters both the membrane stiffness (linearly through $h$) and the
effective mass (linearly through $\rho_w h$).  The prestress ratio $\Pi_5 =
P_\mathrm{iap}/E$ contributes substantially at canonical values but is
approximately constant across mammals ($P/E \approx 0.006$--$0.01$),
so it does not drive inter-species variation.  The density ratio $\Pi_3 =
\rho_w/\rho_f$ has a more modest effect: because soft tissue and
peritoneal fluid have similar densities ($\Pi_3 \approx 1.04$--$1.08$), the
fluid added mass $\rho_f R / n$ dominates the denominator of
$m_\mathrm{eff}$.
This sensitivity structure explains why $\Pi_0$ is approximately conserved
across the species considered: the governing ratios $h/a$, $\rho_w/\rho_f$,
and $P/E$ are constrained by shared developmental
biology~\cite{West1997}, and $\Pi_0$ is a smooth function of these inputs.
If the inputs barely vary, continuity ensures the output is near-constant;
the approximate constancy of $\Pi_0$ is therefore a consequence of the
assumed allometric input constraints rather than an independent biological
discovery.  Furthermore, Poisson's ratio is held constant at $\nu = 0.45$
across all species, effectively eliminating $\Pi_6$ as a governing variable
and reducing the cross-species comparison to four input groups.

\section{Parametric verification}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate~\eqref{eq:scaling_law} over a 1458-point grid spanning
physiological ranges of all five governing dimensionless groups:
$h/a \in [0.025,\, 0.100]$,
$c/a \in [0.50,\, 0.80]$,
$\rho_w/\rho_f \in [0.98,\, 1.18]$,
$P_\mathrm{iap}/E \in [5 \times 10^{-4},\, 0.02]$,
and $\nu \in [0.40,\, 0.49]$.
In dimensional terms these correspond to
$E \in [0.05, 2.0]$~MPa (6~values),
$a \in [0.15, 0.20]$~m (3~values), $c/a \in [0.5, 0.8]$ (3~values),
$h \in [0.005, 0.015]$~m (3~values), $\rho_w/\rho_f \in [0.98, 1.18]$
(3~values), and $\nu \in [0.40, 0.49]$ (3~values), with $K_f$ and
$P_\mathrm{iap}$ held at canonical values.  The loss tangent $\eta$
is excluded because it does not affect the undamped natural frequency.
This exercise is a dimensional-analysis \emph{verification}: both the
numerical model and the analytical $\Phi_n$ evaluate the same governing
equations in differently factored forms, so machine-precision agreement
is algebraically expected.  The purpose is to confirm the correctness
of the Buckingham Pi reduction, not to validate against independent data
(which was established in the companion paper~\cite{Mace2026browntone}).

Figure~\ref{fig:collapse} shows the result.  In raw dimensional
coordinates, the data scatter across the $f$--$h$ plane with no clear
pattern (panel~a).  Non-dimensionalisation via~\eqref{eq:scaling_law}
collapses all 1458 points onto universal curves $\Pi_0(h/a)$ parameterised
by $c/a$ (panel~b).  The parity plot (panel~c) confirms that the
analytical $\Phi_n$ reproduces every numerical point to within numerical
round-off (maximum relative error $7.9 \times 10^{-16}$), confirming
the algebraic consistency of the non-dimensionalisation rather than
providing independent physical validation, which was established in the
companion paper~\cite{Mace2026browntone}.

\section{Cross-species scaling}
\label{sec:species}

The scaling law~\eqref{eq:scaling_law} predicts modal frequencies for any
organism given its body dimensions and tissue properties.
Table~\ref{tab:scaling} lists predictions for four mammals, using
illustrative species-specific parameters assumed from broad allometric trends
and tissue-mechanics ranges rather than direct organ-level measurements of the
abdominal wall in each species~\cite{Fung1993,McMahonBonner1983,West1997}.
The values of $E$, $h$, and $P_\mathrm{iap}$ are round estimates guided
by size-scaling principles; no species-specific abdominal-wall modulus
measurements exist for rat, cat, or pig, and these parameters should be
read as plausible order-of-magnitude assumptions rather than definitive
anatomical data.  The resulting frequencies are directly
testable by mechanically driven shaker-table experiments that identify the
lowest flexural resonance in instrumented animal models.  Four of the five
governing dimensionless groups ($h/a$, $c/a$, $\rho_w/\rho_f$,
$P_\mathrm{iap}/E$) are shown explicitly; Poisson's ratio ($\nu = 0.45$) is
held constant, thereby eliminating $\Pi_6$ as a free variable.  The quantity
$kR_\mathrm{eq} = 2\pi f_2 R_\mathrm{eq}/c_\mathrm{air}$, where
$R_\mathrm{eq} = (a^2 c)^{1/3}$ is the equivalent-sphere radius, is
the Helmholtz number governing airborne coupling.

Three observations emerge.  First, the dimensionless frequency $\Pi_0$
is approximately constant ($0.065$--$0.072$) across a six-fold range of
body size, with a coefficient of variation of 4.5\%.  However, this
near-constancy is expected from continuity: the five input $\Pi$-groups
vary only modestly across species ($h/a$: $1.34\times$; $c/a$:
$1.08\times$; $\rho_w/\rho_f$: $1.04\times$; $P/E$: $1.67\times$;
$\nu$: held fixed), and $\Pi_0$ is a smooth function of these inputs.
A formal uncertainty propagation using first-order Taylor expansion
around the canonical human parameters shows that $\pm 20$\% uniform
uncertainty in each of $h/a$, $c/a$, $\rho_w/\rho_f$, and $P/E$
produces a coefficient of variation in $\Pi_0$ of approximately 3\%
(Appendix~\ref{app:uncertainty}).  This is comparable to the 4.5\%
inter-species spread, meaning the apparent constancy is statistically
indistinguishable from input noise at the assumed uncertainty level.
The constancy of $\Pi_0$ is therefore best interpreted as a consequence
of the assumed allometric constraints on the input groups, rather than
as an independent biological discovery; its practical value lies in
confirming that the scaling law~\eqref{eq:scaling_law} correctly
captures the parametric dependencies.

Second, the scattering coupling ratio
$\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{scat}$ varies
by only a factor of two (from $7.7 \times 10^3$ for humans to
$1.7 \times 10^4$ for rats), remaining firmly in the $10^3$--$10^4$ range.
For the present $n=2$ flexural mode, $\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{scat}$ is the
Rayleigh scattering component of the fuller ratio $\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{full}$
reported in Paper~1~\cite{Mace2026browntone}; higher-order modes inherit a
stronger $(kR_\mathrm{eq})^n$ penalty.  Airborne coupling is negligible for
all species studied.  Third, the
product $kR_\mathrm{eq}$ is of order $10^{-2}$ for all four species, placing every
organism deep in the Rayleigh regime.

This size-independence of $\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{scat}$ has a practical
implication:
if the assumed allometric constraints hold, animal models would exhibit
similar scaling behaviour to the human abdomen in
studies~\cite{vonGierke1971,ISO2631}, \emph{provided excitation is
delivered mechanically} (e.g.\ shaker table) rather than acoustically.  Acoustic experiments would require
compensating for the $(kR_\mathrm{eq})^n$ coupling penalty, which varies with body
size and frequency.

\section{The breathing mode is never infrasonic}
\label{sec:breathing}

The breathing mode ($n = 0$) is dominated by the fluid bulk modulus $K_f$
rather than the shell stiffness.  Its frequency is approximately
\begin{equation}
f_0 \approx \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{3K_f}{\rho_f R^2}},
\end{equation}
where $K_f$ is the fluid bulk modulus, $\rho_f$ is the fluid density, and
$R$ is the equivalent radius.  In the fluid-dominated limit, this gives the same scaling
$f_0 \propto \sqrt{K_f / \rho_f} / R$; evaluated at the canonical human
$R_\mathrm{eq}$ it yields $f_0 \approx \SI{2.57}{\kilo\hertz}$, while the full
fluid--shell model gives $f_0 \approx \SI{2490}{\hertz}$.  To bring $f_0$ down to
\SI{20}{\hertz}---the upper boundary of infrasound---would require an
equivalent radius of order \SI{20}{\metre}.  The breathing mode is
therefore irrelevant to infrasound for any biological organism, regardless
of species.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Equivalent-sphere approximation}

The scaling law~\eqref{eq:scaling_law} maps the oblate spheroidal abdomen
($c/a \approx 0.65$--$0.70$) to an equivalent sphere of radius $R = (a^2
c)^{1/3}$.  This preserves enclosed volume but not the curvature
distribution.  For the human abdomen, the eccentricity is $e = \sqrt{1 -
(c/a)^2} \approx 0.74$, giving $e^2 \approx 0.55$.  Mode frequencies of
oblate spheroidal shells differ from those of spheres at $O(e^2)$
\cite{Soedel2004,Leissa1973}, implying corrections of order 50\%.  The
companion paper~\cite{Mace2026browntone} addresses this fully using the
oblate-spheroid mode equations; the present scaling law should be
interpreted as establishing the correct order-of-magnitude and parametric
dependencies rather than precise absolute frequencies.  The dimensional
reduction itself---the identification of five governing $\Pi$-groups and the
functional form~\eqref{eq:scaling_law}---is exact regardless of shell
geometry; only the closed-form expression for $\Phi_n$ carries the
equivalent-sphere approximation.

\subsection{Limitations and future directions}

Three principal limitations should be noted.  First, the cross-species
tissue parameters (Table~\ref{tab:scaling}) are illustrative assumptions,
not measured data; the uncertainty propagation (Appendix~\ref{app:uncertainty})
shows that the inter-species constancy of $\Pi_0$ is indistinguishable
from input noise at the $\pm 20$\% uncertainty level.  Experimental
measurement of abdominal-wall elastic modulus, thickness, and density in
animal models would convert the present scaling predictions from plausible
estimates to testable quantitative claims.  Second, the equivalent-sphere
mapping introduces $O(e^2) \approx 50$\% frequency errors that are not
captured by the present $\Phi_n$; extending the scaling law to the full
oblate-spheroid equations is a natural next step.  Third, the scattering
ratio $\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{scat} = 1/(kR_\mathrm{eq})^2$ captures only the
Rayleigh geometric factor; the full airborne-to-mechanical ratio includes
impedance-mismatch and modal-participation corrections that may vary across
species in ways not captured by the present analysis.

\section{Concluding remarks}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Buckingham Pi analysis reduces the ten-parameter fluid-filled
viscoelastic shell model (eleven variables including the output frequency) to five governing dimensionless groups for
flexural modes.  The resulting closed-form scaling law collapses a
1458-point parametric verification with algebraic consistency and provides
cross-species predictions without re-solving the eigenvalue problem.
Under the assumed allometric constraints, the dimensionless frequency
$\Pi_0 \approx 0.07$ is approximately conserved across mammals, and the
scattering coupling ratio
$\mathcal{R}_\mathrm{scat} \sim 10^3$--$10^4$ is approximately size-independent,
supporting the use of animal-model scaling for abdominal resonance
studies under mechanical excitation.  The near-constancy of $\Pi_0$
follows from the continuity of $\Phi_n$ and the narrow range of input
$\Pi$-groups; it should be interpreted as a consequence of allometric
constraints rather than an independent universal constant.  The breathing
mode never enters the infrasonic range for any organism.

The dimensionless framework developed here has potential applications beyond
abdominal biomechanics.  In blast-injury research, the scaling law
may enable direct extrapolation from animal-model data to human injury
thresholds without repeating experiments at human scale.  In medical
ultrasound, fluid-filled organ models are routinely used to predict
diagnostic and therapeutic responses; the $\Pi$-group reduction offers
a systematic basis for cross-organ and cross-species
comparison~\cite{Abramson1966,FahyGardonio2007}.  In vehicle NVH
assessment, whole-body vibration standards~\cite{ISO2631} rely on
empirical transfer functions whose mechanistic basis could benefit from
scaling laws of the type derived here.  In underwater
acoustics, fluid-filled elastic shells are canonical targets for sonar
modelling~\cite{Junger1952,Junger1986}; the present dimensional
reduction applies directly to that class of problems.

\section{Uncertainty propagation for $\Pi_0$}
\label{app:uncertainty}

The dimensionless frequency $\Pi_0 = \Phi_n(h/a, c/a, \rho_w/\rho_f,
P_\mathrm{iap}/E, \nu)$ depends on five input groups.  To assess the
sensitivity of $\Pi_0$ to uncertainty in the cross-species parameters, we
compute normalised sensitivity coefficients
\begin{equation}
  S_i = \frac{\Pi_i}{\Pi_0}\,\frac{\partial \Pi_0}{\partial \Pi_i}
\end{equation}
by central finite differences at the canonical human values ($h/a = 0.056$,
$c/a = 0.67$, $\rho_w/\rho_f = 1.078$, $P/E = 0.01$, $\nu = 0.45$).
The resulting coefficients are $S_{h/a} \approx +0.22$,
$S_{c/a} \approx -0.41$, $S_{\rho/\rho} \approx -0.06$,
$S_{P/E} \approx +0.23$, and $S_\nu \approx +0.07$.  The dominant
sensitivities are to the thickness ratio and aspect ratio.

For independent uniform uncertainties of relative half-width $\delta$ in
each input group, the coefficient of variation of $\Pi_0$ is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CV}(\Pi_0) = \frac{\delta}{\sqrt{3}}
    \sqrt{\sum_i S_i^2}.
  \label{eq:cv}
\end{equation}
At $\delta = 0.20$ ($\pm 20$\%) in all five groups, this gives
$\mathrm{CV}(\Pi_0) \approx 6$\%.  In practice, however, $\nu$ is held
fixed across species and $\rho_w/\rho_f$ varies by only 4\%.
The most uncertain parameters are the tissue modulus $E$ and wall thickness
$h$; if only these carry $\pm 20$\% uncertainty while geometry ($a$, $c/a$)
and density are assumed known, then $h/a$ and $P/E$ each inherit
$\pm 20$\% uncertainty.  From the two dominant terms alone,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CV}(\Pi_0) \approx \frac{0.20}{\sqrt{3}}
    \sqrt{S_{h/a}^2 + S_{P/E}^2} \approx \frac{0.20}{\sqrt{3}} \times 0.32
    \approx 3.7\%.
\end{equation}
The observed inter-species $\Pi_0$ spread of 4.5\% is therefore
comparable to---and statistically indistinguishable from---the propagated
input uncertainty, confirming that the apparent constancy of $\Pi_0$
is a consequence of the narrow input range rather than evidence of a
deeper biological invariant.
