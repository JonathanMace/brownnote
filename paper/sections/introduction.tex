\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Few acoustic myths have proven as durable---or as evocative---as the ``brown
note'': a hypothetical infrasonic frequency, typically cited in the
\SIrange{5}{9}{\hertz} range, purported to cause involuntary loss of bowel
control by resonating the abdominal cavity.  The claim has circulated in
military folklore, audio engineering lore, and popular media for decades,
surviving at least one televised debunking \cite{Leventhall2007}.  Yet for
all its cultural staying power, the brown note has received remarkably little
rigorous analytical attention.  The hypothesis has been dismissed, but never
properly \emph{examined}.

This is unfortunate, because the underlying physics is neither trivial nor
implausible on its face.  The human abdomen does resonate.  ISO~2631
documents peak abdominal transmissibility at \SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} under
whole-body vibration \cite{iso2631,griffin1990handbook}, and occupational
health studies report gastrointestinal complaints among operators of heavy
equipment exposed to vibration in precisely this frequency band.  The question
is not whether abdominal resonance exists---it plainly does---but whether
\emph{airborne sound} can excite it to any meaningful degree.

No prior study has:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item derived abdominal cavity eigenfrequencies from first-principles
    shell theory with proper fluid--structure coupling;
  \item quantified the airborne acoustic coupling coefficient for flexural
    modes in the long-wavelength limit; or
  \item compared airborne and mechanical excitation pathways within a common
    modal framework.
\end{enumerate}

We address all three gaps.  We model the abdomen as a fluid-filled
viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shell, derive natural frequencies for both
breathing ($n=0$) and flexural ($n \geq 2$) mode families, and compare
airborne acoustic coupling---subject to a severe $(ka)^n$ penalty at
infrasonic wavelengths---against direct mechanical (whole-body) vibration
coupling, which suffers no such penalty.

The central finding is a coupling disparity of roughly $10^4$ between these
two pathways.  This single number resolves an apparent paradox in the
literature: whole-body vibration at \SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} produces
well-documented gastrointestinal effects, while airborne infrasound at
comparable frequencies does not.  The brown note, it turns out, is not so much
wrong as misattributed---the resonance is real, but the postulated excitation
mechanism is not up to the job.

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:formulation} develops the mathematical formulation.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents a parametric study and validates the model
against ISO~2631 data.  Section~\ref{sec:coupling} quantifies the coupling
disparity.  Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications, alternative
pathways, and limitations, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} draws conclusions.
