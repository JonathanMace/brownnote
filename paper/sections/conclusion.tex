\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have modelled the human abdomen as a fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate
spheroidal shell and identified two physically distinct mode families.
The breathing mode ($n = 0$), governed by the fluid bulk modulus, sits at
approximately \SI{2500}{\hertz}---far above any frequency that could
reasonably be called infrasonic.  Flexural modes ($n \geq 2$), in which the
shell changes shape without compressing the fluid, fall in the
\SIrange{4}{10}{\hertz} range for relaxed musculature and match the abdominal
resonance band documented in ISO~2631.  The abdomen does, in fact, resonate at
infrasonic frequencies.

The catch---and it is a substantial one---lies in the coupling.  Airborne
sound at these frequencies has a wavelength exceeding \SI{85}{\metre}, making
the abdomen acoustically negligible.  The $(ka)^n$ coupling penalty reduces
the effective driving pressure by four orders of magnitude, and the
energy-consistent analysis shows an absorption efficiency of $\sim 10^{-14}$.
Reaching the mechanotransduction threshold via airborne infrasound alone
would require approximately \SI{158}{\decibel}---a level at which structural
damage to the middle ear, blast lung injury, and building collapse would all
present more immediate concerns than any intestinal embarrassment.

Mechanical whole-body vibration, by contrast, couples directly and produces
displacements $10^4$ times larger at occupational exposure levels.  This
disparity---arising from basic physics rather than any exotic biology---provides
a clean, quantitative explanation for a longstanding asymmetry in the
empirical literature.

The brown note, then, is a half-truth.  The resonance it invokes is genuine;
the mechanism it implies is not.  What popular culture attributes to airborne
sound is, in reality, the province of mechanical vibration---a distinction
that matters considerably for occupational health standards and rather less
for horror film screenwriters.

Future work should validate the boundary condition assumptions through finite
element analysis of anatomically realistic geometries, investigate the role of
bowel gas pockets as localised acoustic transducers, and extend the model into
the nonlinear regime relevant to high-amplitude mechanical excitation.
