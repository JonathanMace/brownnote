\begin{highlights}
\item Fluid-filled shell model predicts abdominal resonance at 4--10 Hz
\item Breathing mode at 2900 Hz; flexural modes match ISO 2631 data
\item Airborne coupling penalised by $(ka)^n$; negligible at 120 dB SPL
\item Mechanical vibration couples 1000--10000x more efficiently
\item Coupling disparity explains WBV effects vs.\ absent airborne effects
\end{highlights}

\begin{abstract}
The ``brown note''---a hypothetical infrasonic tone said to cause involuntary
bowel movements---has persisted in popular culture without a proper mechanistic
examination.  We model the human abdomen as a fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate
spheroidal shell and show that it possesses two distinct families of vibration
modes.  The breathing mode ($n=0$), dominated by the fluid bulk modulus, sits
near \SI{2500}{\hertz} and plays no role at infrasonic frequencies.  Flexural
modes ($n \geq 2$), in which the shell deforms without compressing the
enclosed fluid, fall squarely in the \SIrange{4}{10}{\hertz} range for relaxed
musculature---consistent with abdominal resonance data from ISO~2631.

We then ask the critical question: can airborne sound actually excite these
modes?  At \SI{4}{\hertz} the acoustic wavelength exceeds \SI{85}{\metre},
imposing a $(ka)^n$ coupling penalty that reduces the effective driving
pressure by four orders of magnitude.  Energy-consistent reciprocity analysis
yields wall displacements of order \SI{0.01}{\micro\metre} at
\SI{120}{\decibel} SPL---two orders of magnitude below known
mechanotransduction thresholds.  Mechanical whole-body vibration, by contrast,
couples directly to the shell and produces displacements roughly $10^4$ times
larger at occupational exposure levels.  This coupling disparity, arising from
elementary long-wavelength acoustics, explains why whole-body vibration causes
documented gastrointestinal effects while airborne infrasound does not.  Monte
Carlo uncertainty quantification over physiologically realistic parameter
ranges confirms the robustness of these conclusions.
\end{abstract}
