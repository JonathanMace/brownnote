\begin{abstract}
The ``brown note''---a hypothetical infrasonic frequency purported to cause
involuntary bowel movements---has persisted in popular culture despite a lack
of rigorous scientific investigation. This study presents the first systematic
computational analysis of infrasound-induced resonance in the human abdominal
cavity using finite element methods. The abdomen is modelled as a fluid-filled
elastic ellipsoidal shell with material properties derived from published
soft-tissue data. Modal analysis is performed for the structure alone, the
acoustic cavity alone, and the coupled fluid--structure system using FEniCSx
with SLEPc eigenvalue solvers. Analytical solutions based on Donnell shell
theory and Helmholtz cavity modes validate the computational approach.
A parametric study examines the sensitivity of the fundamental frequency to
wall stiffness (\SIrange{20}{100}{\kilo\pascal}), thickness
(\SIrange{10}{20}{\milli\metre}), and cavity geometry. Results show that
the coupled fundamental frequency lies in the range
\SIrange{X}{Y}{\hertz}, which [is/is not] within the infrasound regime.
Harmonic response analysis at physiologically plausible sound pressure
levels (\SI{120}{\decibel} re \SI{20}{\micro\pascal}) reveals peak
displacement amplitudes of order \SI{Z}{\micro\metre}, which
[are/are not] sufficient to produce the claimed physiological effects.
\end{abstract}
