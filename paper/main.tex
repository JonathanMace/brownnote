\begin{highlights}
\item Coupling disparity of $\sim$$10^4$ explains vibration--sound asymmetry
\item Oblate spheroidal shell model predicts abdominal resonance at 4--10~Hz
\item Breathing mode at $\sim$2500~Hz; flexural modes at infrasonic frequencies
\item Airborne coupling penalised by $(ka)^n$; negligible at 120~dB SPL
\item Monte Carlo UQ confirms robustness; Sobol $S_T = 0.86$ for modulus
\end{highlights}

\begin{abstract}
Whole-body vibration at \SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} causes well-documented
gastrointestinal disturbances, yet airborne sound at the same frequencies does
not---even at extreme intensities.  We resolve this asymmetry by modelling
the human abdomen as a fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shell and
quantifying both excitation pathways within a common energy-budget framework.

The model predicts flexural modes ($n \geq 2$) at \SIrange{4}{10}{\hertz},
consistent with ISO~2631 abdominal resonance data.  At these frequencies the
abdomen lies deep in the Rayleigh regime ($ka \approx 0.01$); the resulting
$(ka)^n$ penalty and air--tissue impedance mismatch reduce airborne coupling
by four orders of magnitude.  Energy-consistent analysis yields wall
displacements of \SI{0.014}{\micro\metre} at \SI{120}{\decibel}
SPL---far below mechanotransduction thresholds.  Mechanical vibration,
transmitted through the skeleton, produces displacements roughly $7 \times 10^4$
times larger.  Monte Carlo uncertainty quantification confirms this disparity
across physiologically realistic parameter ranges ($S_T = 0.86$ for elastic
modulus).  The result explains the empirical record quantitatively and settles
the ``brown note'' question: the resonance is real, but airborne sound is the
wrong mechanism.
\end{abstract}
