\begin{highlights}
\item Fluid-filled shell model predicts abdominal resonance at 4--10 Hz
\item Breathing mode at 2900 Hz; flexural modes match ISO 2631 data
\item Airborne coupling penalised by $(ka)^n$; negligible at 120 dB SPL
\item Mechanical vibration couples 1000--10000x more efficiently
\item Coupling disparity explains WBV effects vs.\ absent airborne effects
\end{highlights}

\begin{abstract}
The human abdomen is modelled as a fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal
shell to investigate its modal response under low-frequency excitation. Using
Love--Kirchhoff thin-shell theory with Kelvin--Voigt viscoelasticity and proper
fluid--structure coupling, we derive natural frequencies for both breathing
($n=0$) and flexural ($n \geq 2$) mode families. The breathing mode, dominated
by the fluid bulk modulus, occurs at approximately \SI{2900}{\hertz} and is
irrelevant to infrasonic exposure. In contrast, flexural modes---in which the
shell deforms without significant volume change and the enclosed fluid acts
primarily as added mass---fall in the \SIrange{4}{10}{\hertz} range, consistent
with abdominal resonance data reported under ISO~2631. A comprehensive
parametric study over elastic modulus (\SIrange{0.05}{2.0}{\mega\pascal}),
geometry, wall thickness, and intra-abdominal pressure demonstrates that the
$n=2$ flexural mode robustly occupies this frequency band for relaxed
musculature, though the precise frequency is sensitive to tissue stiffness and
boundary conditions.

We then compare two excitation pathways quantitatively. For airborne acoustic
coupling, the long-wavelength condition ($ka \approx 0.017$ at
\SI{7}{\hertz}) imposes a $(ka)^n$ penalty on flexural mode excitation,
yielding displacements of order \SI{0.1}{\micro\metre} at \SI{120}{\decibel}
SPL---below known mechanotransduction thresholds. For mechanical (whole-body)
vibration, coupling is direct and produces displacements $10^3$--$10^4$ times
larger at occupational exposure levels. This disparity explains why whole-body
vibration at \SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} is associated with gastrointestinal effects
in the epidemiological literature, whereas airborne infrasound at comparable
frequencies is not. The analysis provides the first unified quantitative
framework bridging these two excitation mechanisms through a common modal
description.
\end{abstract}
