\opening{Dear Editor,}

We respectfully submit the enclosed manuscript, ``Modal Analysis of a
Fluid-Filled Viscoelastic Oblate Spheroidal Shell: Implications for the
Existence of the `Brown Note','' for consideration in the \textit{Journal of
Sound and Vibration}.

The ``brown note''---a hypothetical infrasonic tone said to cause involuntary
bowel movements---has circulated for decades in popular culture, military
folklore, and even the occasional engineering discussion.  Despite its
notoriety, no prior study has subjected this claim to a rigorous,
first-principles mechanistic analysis.  We present, to our knowledge, the
first quantitative comparison of airborne acoustic versus mechanical
whole-body vibration (WBV) excitation of abdominal cavity resonance within a
unified modal framework.  The result is decisive: airborne coupling is
approximately $10^4$ times weaker than mechanical coupling, resolving a
long-standing paradox in the vibration and infrasound literature.

The paper makes the following key contributions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fluid-filled viscoelastic shell model.}  We derive the
    eigenfrequencies of a fluid-filled oblate spheroidal shell representing
    the human abdomen, identifying two distinct mode families: a breathing
    mode near \SI{2900}{\hertz} (governed by the fluid bulk modulus and
    irrelevant at infrasonic frequencies) and flexural modes at
    \SIrange{4}{10}{\hertz} for relaxed musculature---in excellent agreement
    with ISO~2631 abdominal transmissibility data.

  \item \textbf{Coupling disparity quantification.}  An energy-consistent
    reciprocity analysis shows that airborne sound at \SI{120}{\decibel} SPL
    produces wall displacements of order \SI{0.014}{\micro\metre}---two
    orders of magnitude below known mechanotransduction (PIEZO1/2) thresholds.
    Mechanical WBV at routine occupational levels produces displacements
    roughly $10^4$ times larger.  This disparity arises from elementary
    long-wavelength acoustics and impedance mismatch, and is robust to
    material and geometric uncertainties.

  \item \textbf{Monte Carlo uncertainty quantification.}  A 10{,}000-sample
    Monte Carlo study over physiologically realistic parameter ranges confirms
    that the coupling ratio spans $10^3$--$10^5$, with elastic modulus
    accounting for 86\% of total variance (Sobol $S_T = 0.86$).

  \item \textbf{Novel inter-individual variability mechanism.}  Analysis of
    intestinal gas pocket dynamics reveals that large pockets
    (\SIrange{2}{5}{\centi\metre}) can experience the full incident pressure
    without the $(ka)^n$ penalty, offering a plausible explanation for
    sporadic individual sensitivity reports at extreme SPL.
\end{itemize}

We believe this work is well suited to \textit{JSV} for several reasons.  At
its core, the paper is a fluid--structure interaction and acoustic scattering
problem---shell vibration theory, Rayleigh-regime multipole coupling, and
energy-budget verification---squarely within the journal's scope.  Yet it
also bridges to biomechanics and occupational health: the results provide a
mechanistic rationale for the frequency weightings in ISO~2631-1 and for the
exposure limits codified in EU~Directive~2002/44/EC.  We are mindful that the
subject matter has a colorful reputation, but the analysis is conducted with
the same rigor one would apply to any acoustic--structural coupling problem;
if anything, the topic's visibility makes a careful quantitative treatment
overdue.

We suggest that reviewers with expertise in the following areas would be
well positioned to evaluate the manuscript:
%
\begin{itemize}
  \item Structural acoustics and fluid--structure interaction (shell
    vibration, scattering theory);
  \item Whole-body vibration and occupational biomechanics;
  \item Soft-tissue mechanics and viscoelastic constitutive modelling.
\end{itemize}

The authors declare no competing financial interests or personal
relationships that could have appeared to influence the work.  The manuscript
has not been published previously and is not under consideration elsewhere.
All analytical models and computational scripts are available in the
accompanying repository for reproducibility.

Thank you for your consideration.  We look forward to your response.
