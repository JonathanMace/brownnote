\begin{highlights}
\item Airborne acoustic coupling to the abdomen increases as $(ka)^n$ with frequency
\item At \SIrange{20}{80}{\hertz}, $ka$ transitions from negligible to $\sim$0.2
\item Concert sub-bass (\SIrange{100}{115}{\decibel}) produces tissue displacements far below perception thresholds
\item Mechanical (whole-body) pathway remains dominant even in the sub-bass band
\item The ``feel it in your gut'' sensation at concerts requires structural rather than airborne transmission
\end{highlights}

\begin{abstract}
Concertgoers commonly report ``feeling the bass in their gut'' at high
sound pressure levels, particularly during electronic dance music and
pipe-organ performances.  While whole-body vibration at
\SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} is known to excite abdominal resonances, the role
of airborne sub-bass sound (\SIrange{20}{80}{\hertz}) in producing
visceral sensation remains unquantified.

We extend the fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shell model
developed in our companion paper to the \SIrange{20}{80}{\hertz} band,
where the Helmholtz number $ka$ transitions from negligible
(${\sim}\num{0.01}$ at \SI{4}{\hertz}) to appreciable (${\sim}\num{0.23}$
at \SI{80}{\hertz}).  Using literature-derived concert sound pressure
level spectra for rock, electronic dance music, and pipe-organ venues,
we compute energy-consistent tissue displacements and compare them with
whole-body vibration perception thresholds from ISO~2631-1.

Despite the improved acoustic coupling at higher frequencies, the
off-resonance penalty (the abdomen's flexural resonance lies at
${\sim}\SI{4}{\hertz}$, not \SIrange{20}{80}{\hertz}) reduces
displacements by several orders of magnitude.  Even at peak electronic
dance music levels (\SI{115}{\decibel} at \SI{40}{\hertz}), airborne
sub-bass produces tissue displacements that are negligible compared with
perception thresholds.  The familiar sensation of ``feeling'' concert
bass therefore cannot be explained by airborne acoustic coupling to the
abdominal cavity; it is instead attributable to whole-body vibration
transmitted through the floor and seating, chest-wall motion, and
somatosensory stimulation of the skin.

These results close a gap between structural acoustics and
psychoacoustics by providing the first quantitative assessment of
airborne sub-bass coupling to the human abdomen at concert sound
pressure levels.
\end{abstract}
