\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Coupling parameter transition}
\label{sec:ka-transition}

Figure~\ref{fig:coupling-competition} shows the Helmholtz number $ka$,
the off-resonance transfer function $H(r)/Q$, and their product as
functions of frequency.  At the $n = 2$ flexural resonance
(\SI{3.95}{\hertz}), $ka = 0.011$ and $(ka)^2 = 1.3 \times 10^{-4}$.
By \SI{40}{\hertz}, $ka = 0.115$ and $(ka)^2 = 1.3 \times 10^{-2}$---a
hundredfold improvement.  At \SI{80}{\hertz}, $ka = 0.23$ and
$(ka)^2 = 5.3 \times 10^{-2}$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/fig_coupling_competition.pdf}
  \caption{Competition between improved $ka$-coupling (blue) and
    off-resonance penalty $H(r)/Q$ (red dashed) in the sub-bass band.
    The product (black) determines the net coupling efficiency.}
  \label{fig:coupling-competition}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Helmholtz number and coupling factor at selected frequencies.}
  \label{tab:ka-values}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lllll@{}}
    \toprule
    $f$ [\si{\hertz}] & $ka$ & $(ka)^2$ &
      Ratio to $f_2$ & Regime \\
    \midrule
    3.95 (resonance) & 0.011 & $1.3 \times 10^{-4}$ & 1 & Deep Rayleigh \\
    20               & 0.057 & $3.3 \times 10^{-3}$ & 25 & Rayleigh \\
    40               & 0.115 & $1.3 \times 10^{-2}$ & 100 & Transition \\
    60               & 0.172 & $3.0 \times 10^{-2}$ & 230 & Transition \\
    80               & 0.230 & $5.3 \times 10^{-2}$ & 408 & Transition \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Despite this substantial improvement, the coupling factor remains well
below unity.  At \SI{80}{\hertz}, the effective driving pressure is
still only \SI{5.3}{\percent} of the incident pressure for $n = 2$.

\subsection{Off-resonance attenuation}
\label{sec:off-resonance}

The SDOF transfer function $H(r, \zeta)$ (equation~\ref{eq:sdof})
attenuates the response far from resonance.  Table~\ref{tab:sdof} shows
$H$ at representative frequencies.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{SDOF transfer function at sub-bass frequencies

  \label{tab:sdof}

    \midrule
    3.95 & 1.00 & 4.00 & +12.0 \\

The off-resonance penalty dominates.  At \SI{40}{\hertz}, the
hundredfold improvement in coupling is offset by a hundredfold
reduction in transfer function, yielding net displacement comparable
to the resonance case.  At higher frequencies, the $1/r^2$ roll-off
wins convincingly.

\subsection{Tissue displacement at concert SPLs}
\label{sec:displacement-results}

Figure~\ref{fig:displacement-spectrum} presents energy-consistent
tissue displacement as a function of frequency for the three concert
spectra, compared with the ISO~2631 perception threshold.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/fig_displacement_spectrum.pdf}
  \caption{Airborne tissue displacement at concert SPLs (energy-consistent)
    compared with ISO~2631 whole-body vibration perception thresholds.
    The gap is $\sim$\num{2.5} orders of magnitude.}
  \label{fig:displacement-spectrum}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Electronic dance music} ($L_p \approx \SI{115}{\decibel}$
    at \SI{40}{\hertz}):  Peak displacement
    $\xi \approx \SI{0.002}{\micro\metre}$---approximately \num{2.5}
    orders of magnitude below whole-body vibration perception thresholds,
    reaching $\sim$\SI{0.35}{\percent} of the threshold.

  \item \textbf{Rock concert} ($L_p \approx \SI{108}{\decibel}$ at
    \SI{63}{\hertz}):  Peak displacement comparable to the EDM case.

  \item \textbf{Pipe organ} ($L_p \approx \SI{105}{\decibel}$ at
    \SI{40}{\hertz}):  Slightly lower displacement due to lower SPL.
\end{enumerate}

The peak ratio to threshold of $\sim$\SI{0.35}{\percent} (at

from a pressure-based calculation.

\subsection{Structure-borne pathway comparison}
\label{sec:pathway-comparison}

Figure~\ref{fig:pathway-comparison} compares the airborne and
structure-borne (floor/seat) pathways at \SI{40}{\hertz} and
\SI{115}{\decibel}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/fig_pathway_comparison.pdf}
  \caption{Pathway comparison at \SI{40}{\hertz}, \SI{115}{\decibel}:
    airborne displacement is ${\sim}40\times$ weaker than the
    structure-borne pathway.  Neither reaches the perception threshold
    under the conservative floor model assumed here.}
  \label{fig:pathway-comparison}
\end{figure}

At \SI{40}{\hertz} and \SI{115}{\decibel}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Airborne pathway: $\xi \approx \SI{0.002}{\micro\metre}$
    (\SI{0.35}{\percent} of threshold).
  \item Structure-borne pathway: $\xi \approx \SI{0.078}{\micro\metre}$
    (\SI{14}{\percent} of threshold).
  \item The floor/seat pathway dominates by a factor of ${\sim}40$.
\end{itemize}

The structure-borne estimate is conservative (assuming only
\SI{1}{\percent} floor--acoustic coupling efficiency).  On lightweight
timber floors or with tactile transducers, the floor pathway can easily
exceed the perception threshold.

\subsection{SPL sensitivity}
\label{sec:spl-sensitivity}

Figure~\ref{fig:spl-sensitivity} presents the ratio of airborne
displacement to perception threshold as a function of both frequency
and SPL.  The contour plot confirms that the ratio remains below unity
across the entire parameter space, with the highest values occurring at
low frequencies (near the flexural resonance) rather than in the
sub-bass band.

\subsection{Coupling transition band}
\label{sec:transition-band}

We define the ``coupling transition band'' as the frequency range in
which the ratio of airborne-induced displacement to perception threshold
exceeds \SI{1}{\percent}.  At \SI{115}{\decibel} SPL (the EDM peak),
no frequency in the \SIrange{1}{100}{\hertz} range meets this
criterion.  The peak ratio occurs near the $n = 2$ resonance
(\SI{3.95}{\hertz}), where the resonance amplification compensates
for the weaker coupling.

\subsection{Multi-modal contributions}
\label{sec:multimodal}

Higher-order flexural modes ($n = 3, 4, \ldots$) have resonance
frequencies closer to the sub-bass band but even weaker acoustic
coupling ($\propto (ka)^n$ with larger $n$).  Summing contributions from
$n = 2$ through $n = 6$ in quadrature increases the total displacement
by less than \SI{10}{\percent} at any frequency, confirming that the
$n = 2$ mode dominates throughout the sub-bass band.
