I write to nominate the Browntone research programme for consideration for the Ig Nobel Prize, in the spirit of achievements that first make people laugh, and then make them think.

### The Question That Makes You Laugh

Can sound make you lose bowel control?

The "brown note" — a mythical infrasonic frequency said to cause involuntary defecation — has circulated in military folklore, audio engineering lore, and popular media for decades. It was the subject of a *MythBusters* episode. It was the plot of *South Park* Season 3, Episode 17 ("The Brown Noise," 1999). It has been discussed in every acoustics lecture hall where a student has ever raised their hand with a grin. No one had bothered to model it properly.

We bothered.

### The Work That Makes You Think

We modelled the human abdomen as a fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shell — the same mathematical framework used for submarine hulls and spacecraft fuel tanks — and subjected the brown note hypothesis to first-principles analysis. The findings:

**The resonance is real.** The abdomen possesses a genuine flexural resonance at approximately 4 Hz, within the frequency range that folklore predicts and occupational health standards identify as problematic.

**The coupling mechanism is not.** At 4 Hz, the wavelength of sound in air exceeds 85 metres. The abdomen is 36 centimetres across. The sound wave is so much larger than the body that it wraps around without squeezing — a phenomenon governed by the long-wavelength scattering penalty. Mechanical vibration, transmitted through seats and floors, is **66,000 times** more effective at exciting the abdominal resonance than airborne sound. The brown note is a resonance looking for a mechanism that physics will not supply.

But the programme did not stop at debunking. From that single result, we built an eight-paper portfolio:

1. **The Brown Note** — why sound can't make you poop (66,000× coupling disparity)
2. **Gas Pockets** — why some people are more sensitive to low-frequency sound (intestinal gas as acoustic transducers; 9 dB susceptibility range)
3. **Scaling Laws** — predicting abdominal resonance from rats to horses (one equation, all mammals)
4. **Bladder Resonance** — why you feel every bump on a long drive with a full bladder (U-shaped frequency curve, 13.5 Hz minimum)
5. **Borborygmi** — what pitch is a growling stomach? (135–440 Hz predicted, 200–550 Hz observed; no fitted parameters)
6. **Sub-bass Perception** — why you "feel" bass at concerts through the floor, not the air (2,600× pathway disparity)
7. **Watermelon Ripeness** — can one hear the ripeness of a watermelon? (yes; same shell physics, closed-form stiffness inversion from a single tap)
8. **Kac Identifiability** — can you recover material properties from resonance frequencies alone? (yes, if the cavity is oblate; oblateness is nature's regulariser)

### The Arc

The programme begins with an urban legend about defecation and ends with a mathematical theorem about inverse problems, passing through bladder mechanics, stomach acoustics, and the physics of selecting produce at the supermarket. Every paper is published or under review in a serious peer-reviewed venue (*Journal of Sound and Vibration*, *Journal of the Acoustical Society of America*, *Postharvest Biology and Technology*, *Inverse Problems*). Every result is derived from first principles with full uncertainty quantification. The analytical framework is transferable to any fluid-filled biological cavity.

The trajectory — from poop to physics to produce — is, we submit, precisely the kind of research the Ig Nobel Prize exists to celebrate. People laugh when they hear that someone modelled the brown note with the mathematics of submarine acoustics. They laugh again when they learn it led to a theory of watermelon ripeness. They think when they realise that the same framework may enable non-invasive tissue characterisation — measuring organ stiffness from resonance alone, without needles, contrast agents, or ionising radiation.

> *"The body is full of resonators. The difference between myth and mechanism is usually an impedance mismatch."*

We began in folklore and ended in mechanism. We believe that is worth celebrating.
