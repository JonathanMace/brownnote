## 2-Minute Version (~300 words)

Let me tell you about the most productive bad question in acoustics.

The "brown note" is an urban legend — a mythical infrasonic tone said to cause instant loss of bowel control. It's been debunked on TV but never properly modelled. We decided to take it seriously, because the underlying physics is a legitimate question: can airborne sound make a fluid-filled body cavity resonate?

We modelled the abdomen as an oblate spheroidal shell filled with fluid — the same mathematics used for submarine hulls and spacecraft fuel tanks. The resonance is real: about 4 Hz, squarely in the band where whole-body vibration causes documented gastrointestinal effects. But airborne sound at 4 Hz has a wavelength exceeding 85 metres — over 470 times the abdominal diameter. In this regime, the sound wave cannot spatially "grip" the organ. The scattering penalty suppresses coupling by four orders of magnitude. Mechanical vibration, which bypasses the air entirely, is 66,000 times more effective. The brown note fails not because the resonance is absent, but because the coupling mechanism doesn't work.

That negative result turned out to be enormously generative. We showed that intestinal gas pockets create a secondary coupling pathway, explaining why some individuals are more sensitive. We derived universal scaling laws predicting resonance from rats to horses. We applied the model to the bladder, explaining why vibration triggers urinary urgency. We modelled stomach growling as constrained bubble oscillation. We showed that at concerts, you feel the bass through the floor, not through the air.

Then came the surprise. The same physics explains the watermelon "knock test": tap a melon, listen to the frequency, infer the flesh stiffness. And the theoretical capstone — inspired by Kac's "Can one hear the shape of a drum?" — proves that oblateness is what makes parameter recovery from resonance well-conditioned.

Eight papers. One framework. An arc from urban legend to non-invasive elastography — via watermelons.
