## Scientists Model the "Brown Note" — and Discover Why It Can't Work

**Researchers apply aerospace engineering mathematics to an urban legend, revealing a 66,000-fold gap between what airborne sound can do and what mechanical vibration actually does to the human body**

The "brown note" — the persistent rumour that a specific low-frequency sound can cause involuntary loss of bowel control — has circulated in military folklore, audio engineering, and popular culture for decades. It was tested (and debunked) on *MythBusters*. It was the plot of a *South Park* episode. But until now, no one had subjected the claim to a first-principles physics analysis.

Researchers behind the Browntone programme have done exactly that — and the results are both reassuring and unexpectedly rich.

Their study, submitted to the *Journal of Sound and Vibration*, models the human abdomen as a fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shell: essentially, a flexible sack of fluid, described with the same mathematics used to analyse spacecraft fuel tanks and submarine hulls. The model predicts that the abdomen does possess a genuine flexural resonance near 4 Hz — precisely within the frequency range that urban legend claims. So the resonance is real.

The problem is the coupling. At 4 Hz, the wavelength of sound in air exceeds 85 metres. The human abdomen is roughly 36 centimetres across. The sound wave is so much larger than the body that it effectively wraps around without squeezing — a phenomenon well understood in acoustics as the long-wavelength scattering penalty. The result: airborne sound at a very loud 120 dB drives abdominal wall motion of just 0.014 micrometres, roughly a thousand times smaller than a human red blood cell. Mechanical vibration — the kind experienced by truck drivers, helicopter pilots, and construction workers — is **66,000 times** more effective at exciting the same mode.

"The brown note is a half-truth," the paper concludes. "The resonance it invokes is genuine; the mechanism it implies is not. What popular culture attributes to airborne sound is, in reality, the province of mechanical vibration."

The finding resolves a long-standing puzzle in occupational health. Whole-body vibration at 4–8 Hz is well documented to cause gastrointestinal disturbance — it is codified in ISO 2631 international standards. Airborne infrasound at the same frequencies, even at extreme intensities tested by NASA in the 1960s (up to 154 dB), produces no gastrointestinal effects. The Browntone model explains this asymmetry quantitatively for the first time.

The analysis uses Monte Carlo uncertainty quantification with 10,000 samples to demonstrate that the coupling disparity is robust across physiologically realistic parameter ranges. Abdominal wall stiffness (elastic modulus) dominates the frequency response, accounting for 86% of the total variance — a finding with implications for non-invasive tissue characterisation.

The paper is the first in a programme of eight studies applying the same analytical framework to gas-pocket transduction mechanisms, cross-species scaling laws, bladder resonance, stomach growling, concert sub-bass perception, watermelon ripeness testing, and the mathematical foundations of parameter recovery from resonance. The programme demonstrates that a question which sounds like a joke can, when taken seriously, produce a transferable scientific framework.
