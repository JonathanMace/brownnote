The laughter is the hook; the mechanics, modelling, and clinical implications are the substance.

### Strategic Reading of Papers 1-5

1. **Paper 1** is the nomination centrepiece because it addresses the original myth directly.
2. **Paper 2** demonstrates that the lab did not stop at debunking; it found the physically plausible alternative pathway.
3. **Paper 3** broadens the programme into generalisable mechanics.
4. **Paper 4** translates the framework to a different organ with obvious real-world resonance.
5. **Paper 5** turns the programme from response to sound generation and gives the portfolio its most naturally memorable outreach paper.

The correct strategic framing is therefore **flagship + portfolio halo**: nominate around Paper 1, support the nomination with Papers 2-5, and keep Paper 5 ready as the most naturally media-friendly companion paper.

## New Paper Proposals

These should be treated as opportunistic follow-ons, not as a reason to redirect the lab away from the current five-paper portfolio.

### 1. Brown Noise, the Body, and the Bedroom: A Biomechanical Analysis of Spectrally Coloured Sound at the Tissue Level
- **Description:** Extend the Browntone coupling framework from single-frequency forcing to broadband coloured noise. Test whether brown, pink, and white noise at ordinary listening levels produce meaningfully different tissue-level responses, or whether the reported sleep and focus effects are better explained by neural and psychological mechanisms than by biomechanics.
- **Target journal:** *JASA Express Letters* or *Journal of Sound and Vibration*
- **Why it ranks first:** Maximum code reuse, immediate public recognisability, and a clean bridge from the existing brand to a contemporary mass-audience question.

### 2. Whale Song and Wet Suits: Acoustic Coupling of Cetacean Calls into the Submerged Human Body
- **Description:** Re-run the coupling problem underwater, where the air-tissue impedance mismatch largely disappears. Quantify whether whale calls in the 10-200 Hz band can couple appreciably into submerged human tissue at realistic encounter distances.
- **Target journal:** *Journal of the Acoustical Society of America (JASA)*
- **Why it ranks second:** It preserves the lab's acoustics identity, has a superb hook, and converts a subtle physics point into a vivid public-science story.

### 3. How High Is Too High? A Computational Fluid-Structure Model of the Human Belly Flop
- **Description:** Apply the lab's abdominal-wall mechanics to impulsive hydrodynamic loading, estimating how belly-flop height maps to pressure distribution, intra-abdominal loading, and injury risk.
- **Target journal:** *Journal of Biomechanical Engineering* or *Physics of Fluids*
- **Why it ranks third:** Exceptionally visual and accessible, with strong media potential, but it needs new SPH/ALE tooling and is therefore less immediate than the top two proposals.

### 4. Could Tesla's Oscillator Really Shake a Building? A Structural Dynamics Analysis of the 1898 "Earthquake Machine" Claim
- **Description:** Translate the lab's resonance and Q-factor expertise from organs to masonry buildings, testing whether Tesla's famous oscillator story survives a quantitative structural-dynamics treatment.
- **Target journal:** *Proceedings of the Royal Society A* or *Journal of Sound and Vibration*
- **Why it ranks fourth:** It is fast, memorable, and likely to travel well beyond the immediate field, but it is strategically more peripheral to the present body-acoustics identity.

### Conference Abstract

**Title:** From the Brown Note to Borborygmi: A Five-Paper Programme in Human Body Acoustics

The Browntone programme investigates whether memorable folklore questions in acoustics can be converted into rigorous biomechanics. The flagship paper models the human abdomen as a fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shell and predicts a lowest flexural resonance near 4 Hz. The central result is not that the resonance is absent, but that the airborne pathway is extraordinarily inefficient: at 120 dB SPL, energy-consistent abdominal-wall displacement is approximately 0.014 um, whereas equivalent whole-body vibration excitation is about 6.6 x 10^4 times stronger. The urban legend survives as a resonance problem but fails as an airborne coupling mechanism.

Four follow-on papers extend that framework. A constrained-bubble model identifies intestinal gas pockets as a plausible local acoustic transduction pathway. A dimensional-analysis paper derives cross-species scaling laws and a quasi-universal dimensionless frequency. A bladder-resonance paper translates the framework to a second organ with occupational-health relevance. A borborygmi paper develops a unified multi-mode model of bowel sounds and predicts clinically plausible frequency bands from measurable anatomical parameters. Together, the papers show how an apparently comic premise can support a serious analytical programme spanning structural acoustics, fluid-structure interaction, mechanotransduction, and clinical vibroacoustics.
