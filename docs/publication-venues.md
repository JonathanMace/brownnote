## 5. What Makes a Paper Publishable

### 5.1 Model Validation Requirements

Reviewers at all target venues expect rigorous validation:

| Requirement | Standard | How to Satisfy |
|-------------|----------|----------------|
| **Mesh convergence** | <2–5% change in key metrics between successive refinements | Run 4–5 mesh densities; plot convergence of peak displacement, von Mises stress, and modal frequencies vs. DOF count |
| **Analytical validation** | Exact solution comparison where available | Compare FEA natural frequencies against Lamb's classical solution for fluid-filled spherical shells |
| **Experimental validation** | Comparison with measured data | Cite and compare against published experimental modal data for tissue phantoms or cadaveric specimens |
| **Sensitivity analysis** | Systematic parameter variation | Vary material properties (±20%), geometry (wall thickness, aspect ratio), damping ratio, fluid properties; report effect on resonant frequencies and mode shapes |
| **Energy balance** | Total energy conservation check | Report kinetic/potential energy balance over simulation time to verify numerical stability |

### 5.2 Minimum Expectations by Journal Tier

**Tier 1 (JSV, Proc. R. Soc. A, IF > 3):**
- Analytical solution development AND numerical validation
- At least one form of experimental comparison
- Comprehensive sensitivity analysis (≥5 parameters)
- Mesh convergence study with quantitative convergence plot
- Statistical analysis where stochastic parameters are involved
- Clear statement of novelty beyond existing literature

**Tier 2 (JASA, J. Biomechanics, Applied Acoustics, IF 2–3):**
- Numerical model with analytical OR experimental validation
- Sensitivity analysis (≥3 parameters)
- Mesh convergence study
- Clear clinical or practical relevance articulated

**Tier 3 (PLOS ONE, Shock & Vibration, IF < 2):**
- Methodologically sound model
- At least one validation approach
- Basic sensitivity and convergence studies
- Reproducibility details sufficient for independent replication

### 5.3 V&V Reporting Checklist

Based on the Oefner et al. (2021) checklist for FEA in biomechanics:

- [ ] Imaging data source described (CT/MRI resolution, slice thickness)
- [ ] Segmentation method documented
- [ ] Element type and formulation specified
- [ ] Mesh generation strategy explained
- [ ] Mesh convergence study presented with quantitative metrics
- [ ] Material model justified (constitutive law, parameter sources)
- [ ] Boundary conditions physiologically justified
- [ ] Loading conditions specified with magnitudes and directions
- [ ] Solver settings documented (time step, convergence criteria, integration scheme)
- [ ] Results compared against analytical, experimental, or published computational data
- [ ] Sensitivity analysis covers material properties, geometry, and BCs
- [ ] Limitations section explicitly addresses model simplifications
- [ ] Software and version identified

### 5.4 Statistical Rigor

For parametric studies:
- Report mean ± standard deviation for stochastic material properties
- Use Monte Carlo or Latin Hypercube sampling for uncertainty quantification
- Present results with confidence intervals where appropriate
- If comparing models, use appropriate statistical tests (t-test, ANOVA)

## 7. Framing Strategy

### 7.1 The Golden Rule: This Is Not About the Brown Note

**Never** mention the "brown note" in the title, abstract, or introduction. This is a serious investigation into:
- Structural acoustics of fluid-filled viscoelastic shells
- Biophysical response to infrasound
- Occupational safety under low-frequency acoustic exposure

The pop-culture connection is interesting cocktail conversation. It has no place in the manuscript.

### 7.2 Recommended Framing by Venue

**For JSV / Proc. R. Soc. A (fundamental science):**
> "We present a semi-analytical and computational investigation of natural frequencies and mode shapes in fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shells subjected to external acoustic forcing in the infrasonic regime (1–20 Hz). The coupled fluid-structure problem is formulated using Love-Kirchhoff shell theory with Kelvin-Voigt viscoelasticity and solved via separation of variables in oblate spheroidal coordinates, with validation against three-dimensional finite element analysis. Parametric studies reveal frequency-dependent resonance amplification that may have implications for acoustic safety standards."

**For JASA (acoustics community):**
> "Infrasound exposure in occupational and environmental settings remains poorly characterized in terms of biomechanical tissue response. We develop a computational model of the human torso as a fluid-filled viscoelastic shell to determine natural frequencies, mode shapes, and frequency-dependent amplification factors under infrasonic excitation. Results are compared against ISO 7196 measurement standards and proposed exposure limits."

**For Journal of Biomechanics (biomedical focus):**
> "Low-frequency acoustic and vibrational exposure poses potential health risks in industrial environments, yet the biomechanical basis for tissue-level effects remains unclear. We present a finite element model of the abdominal cavity to characterize resonance behavior under infrasonic loading (1–20 Hz), validated against published tissue mechanical properties and analytical shell solutions."

### 7.3 Legitimate Scientific Motivation

The paper addresses genuine gaps in knowledge:

1. **Regulatory gap:** ISO 7196 (1995) defines G-weighting for infrasound measurement but provides no exposure limits. OSHA's noise standard (29 CFR 1910.95) uses A-weighting, which effectively ignores frequencies below 20 Hz. No universally accepted occupational exposure limits exist for infrasound.

2. **Occupational health concern:** Workers in industrial settings (turbines, heavy machinery, HVAC systems, transport) are routinely exposed to infrasound at significant SPL. Documented effects include nausea, dizziness, discomfort, and cognitive impairment.

3. **Biomechanical knowledge gap:** While animal studies (rats at 8 Hz, 130 dB) show altered gastric motility and mucosal blood flow changes, the biomechanical mechanisms remain poorly understood. No published FEA model characterizes human abdominal resonance under infrasonic loading.

4. **Modeling methodology gap:** Fluid-filled viscoelastic oblate spheroidal shells have not been rigorously analyzed for their infrasonic modal response. This represents a novel contribution to structural acoustics.

### 7.4 Key References for Legitimacy

- **ISO 7196:1995** — G-weighting characteristic for infrasound measurements
- **ACGIH TLV** — Proposed infrasound limits: 136 dB at 1 Hz to 123 dB at 20 Hz
- **NIOSH/CDC** — "Evaluation of Low Frequency Noise, Infrasound, and Health Symptoms at a Workplace" (HHE Report 2019-0119-3362)
- **Leventhall (2003)** — "A Review of Published Research on Low Frequency Noise and its Effects" (DEFRA report)
- **Persinger (2014)** — Infrasound, human health, and adaptation: an integrative overview (*Natural Hazards*)
- **MDPI Applied Sciences (2025)** — "Infrasound and Human Health: Mechanisms, Effects, and Applications"
- **Resting state network changes induced by experimental inaudible infrasound** (*Scientific Reports*, 2024)

### 7.5 Connection to Broader Research Themes

Position the paper within these established research programs:
- **Whole-body vibration (WBV):** ISO 2631-1 framework
- **Blast injury biomechanics:** Military/defense research on pressure wave effects
- **Acoustic metamaterials:** Emerging low-frequency shielding technologies
- **Digital twins in biomechanics:** Patient-specific computational modeling
- **Environmental noise policy:** EU Environmental Noise Directive

### 8.2 What a First Draft Needs

**Non-negotiable for draft review:**
- [ ] Complete introduction with clear novelty statement
- [ ] Full methodology (reproducible detail)
- [ ] Mesh convergence study (at least preliminary)
- [ ] At least one validation comparison
- [ ] Key results (modal frequencies, mode shapes)
- [ ] Basic sensitivity analysis
- [ ] Draft figures (even rough)
- [ ] Reference list (at least 30 citations)

**Can wait for revision:**
- Extended parametric studies
- Publication-quality figure formatting
- Supplementary materials
- Complete discussion of limitations
- Polished abstract and highlights

### 8.3 What Reviewers Will Want

**Common reviewer requests for FEA biomechanics papers:**

1. **"Insufficient validation"** — The #1 criticism. Reviewers will ask:
   - How does the FEA solution compare to analytical results?
   - Is there experimental validation? If not, why not?
   - What is the quantitative error between model and reference?

2. **"Unjustified material properties"** — Expect questions about:
   - Source of tissue properties (cite specific experimental studies)
   - Sensitivity to property values (±20% variation minimum)
   - Why a particular constitutive model was chosen (Kelvin-Voigt vs. Prony series vs. hyperelastic)

3. **"Mesh dependency"** — Reviewers will look for:
   - Convergence plot (key metric vs. element count)
   - Element quality metrics (aspect ratio, Jacobian)
   - Justification of element type (tet vs. hex; linear vs. quadratic)

4. **"Oversimplified geometry/BCs"** — Be prepared to defend:
   - Why an idealized geometry (oblate spheroid) vs. patient-specific
   - How boundary conditions represent physiological reality
   - Effect of geometric simplifications on results

5. **"So what?"** — The clinical/practical relevance question:
   - What do these resonance frequencies mean for real exposure scenarios?
   - How do results compare to known infrasound exposure levels?
   - What are the implications for safety standards?

6. **"Reproducibility"** — Increasingly required:
   - Software version, solver settings, hardware details
   - Sufficient parameter detail for independent reproduction
   - Consider sharing mesh/model files as supplementary data

- [ ] Title accurately reflects content (no sensationalism)

- [ ] All figures are high resolution (≥300 DPI)

## 9. Recommended Submission Strategy

### Primary Target: Journal of Sound and Vibration

**Rationale:** Highest impact factor (4.9), perfect scope alignment, strong precedent for fluid-filled shell vibration papers. Our analytical + computational approach matches their editorial preference for fundamental work with practical implications.

**Framing emphasis:** Modal analysis of viscoelastic shells; infrasound as the applied context; safety implications in Discussion.

### Backup Target 1: JASA

**If JSV rejects or requests major revision we can't accommodate.** JASA has the acoustics community readership that will most appreciate this work. Lower IF but higher topical specificity.

### Backup Target 2: Applied Acoustics

**If the work evolves to be more applied.** Strong IF (3.6), emphasis on practical acoustic solutions. Good if we develop the safety/regulatory angle more strongly.

### Backup Target 3: PLOS ONE

**If all specialist journals reject on novelty grounds.** PLOS ONE evaluates methodology, not perceived significance. Our rigorous computational approach should pass methodological review. Open access ensures broad visibility.

### Dual-Publication Strategy (if results are rich enough)

Consider splitting into two papers:
1. **Paper A (JSV/Proc. R. Soc. A):** Mathematical formulation and computational validation — the fundamental science of viscoelastic shell resonance
2. **Paper B (JASA/Applied Acoustics):** Application to infrasound safety — parameterized for human exposure scenarios, comparison to standards, safety recommendations

This maximizes output from the simulation campaign while keeping each paper focused.
