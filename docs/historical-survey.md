# Historical Survey: The Brown Note, Infrasound, and Whole-Body Vibration

> **Purpose.** This document surveys the historical origins, scientific investigations,
> and cultural persistence of the "brown note" claim — the idea that a specific
> infrasonic frequency can cause involuntary loss of bowel control. It distinguishes
> rigorously between primary experimental evidence and secondary folklore, and
> contextualises the claim within the broader literatures on infrasound physiology
> and whole-body vibration (WBV).

## 2. Military and Defence Origins

### 2.1 Vladimir Gavreau and the CNRS Experiments (~1957–1966)

Vladimir Gavreau, a researcher at the Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique
(CNRS) in Levallois-Perret, France, is often cited as the originator of infrasound
weapons research. The story begins around 1957 when Gavreau and colleagues
experienced unexplained nausea and malaise in their laboratory, eventually traced to
a faulty ventilator producing infrasound at approximately 7 Hz.

Gavreau subsequently built large resonant pipes — reportedly up to 24 metres long
— to study infrasound production and its physiological effects. His work was
popularised through articles in *Science Journal* (1966) and the *UNESCO Courier*,
which speculated about "acoustic weapons" and a "range of death" at 3–7 Hz.

**Critical assessment.** The primary experimental data from Gavreau's laboratory
are sparse and poorly documented by modern standards. Much of what circulates about
his work derives from secondary retellings, particularly Gerry Vassilatos's
sensationalised account in *Borderland Sciences Research Journal* (1990s), which
embellished claims significantly. Dick Bowdler's critical review, "A Short History
of the Dangers of Infrasound," notes that Gavreau's original publications do not
support the extreme claims attributed to him in popular accounts.

> **Source quality:** Secondary sources dominate. The original French-language
> publications are difficult to obtain and have not been independently replicated.

### 2.2 NASA Research (1960s–1970s)

NASA's interest in infrasound was practical: astronauts and ground crews were
exposed to intense low-frequency noise from rocket engines. Two key documents:

1. **Mohr, Cole, Guild, & von Gierke (1965).** "Effects of Low Frequency and
   Infrasonic Noise on Man." Subjects were exposed to infrasound at 1–100 Hz and
   sound pressure levels of 140–154 dB SPL in controlled chambers. Reported effects
   included chest wall vibration, a gagging sensation, disrupted respiration, and
   mild cardiovascular changes. **No gastrointestinal incontinence was reported.**
   Hearing threshold shifts were minimal and rapidly reversible.

2. **von Gierke (1974).** NASA Technical Note D-7620, "Human Response to
   Infrasound." Established systematic exposure criteria and safety limits,
   forming the foundation for later ISO standards and occupational guidelines.

> **Source quality:** Primary. Peer-reviewed and available via NASA Technical
> Reports Server (NTRS).

### 2.3 Non-Lethal Weapons Programmes

The U.S. Joint Non-Lethal Weapons Directorate (JNLWD), established in 1996,
investigated acoustic technologies for crowd control and area denial. However:

- **Jauchem & Cook (2007)** reviewed the evidence and concluded that high-intensity
  acoustic systems "lack useful systems" for military non-lethal applications. The
  practical obstacles include: inability to focus infrasound directionally, immense
  power requirements, and unpredictable effects on bystanders.

- **Altmann (1999)** provided a rigorous physics-based assessment at ETH Zurich,
  concluding that infrasound weapons were impractical at realistic field-use power
  levels and that most claims were exaggerated.

- The **LRAD (Long Range Acoustic Device)**, deployed since the early 2000s,
  operates in the *audible* frequency range (2–3 kHz), not at infrasonic
  frequencies. It causes pain and disorientation through loudness, not resonance.

> **Source quality:** Altmann (1999) and Jauchem & Cook (2007) are primary,
> peer-reviewed assessments. Popular accounts routinely conflate LRAD with
> infrasound weapons.

## 3. Scientific Investigations of the Brown Note Claim

### 3.1 What Has Actually Been Tested?

The specific claim — that an airborne infrasonic frequency causes involuntary
defecation — has been tested directly only once in a controlled (if informal)
setting:

- **MythBusters (2005).** Adam Savage was exposed to frequencies from 5 Hz to
  32 Hz at sound pressure levels up to approximately 150 dB using a large speaker
  array. Results: dizziness and mild discomfort, but **no loss of bowel control**.
  The myth was declared "busted."

The broader literature on infrasound physiology (Mohr et al. 1965; von Gierke
1966, 1974; Leventhall 2003, 2007) consistently reports the following effects
at extreme SPL (>130 dB):

- Chest wall vibration and respiratory disruption
- Dizziness and spatial disorientation
- Anxiety and feelings of unease
- Mild cardiovascular changes (heart rate, blood pressure)
- Nausea (at very high levels, >140 dB)

**Conspicuously absent from all controlled studies: involuntary defecation.**

### 3.2 Tandy & Lawrence (1998): The 19 Hz Ghost

Vic Tandy, a British engineer at Coventry University, investigated reports of
"ghostly" experiences in his laboratory. He discovered a standing wave at
approximately 18.98 Hz (close to the reported resonant frequency of the human
eyeball) produced by an extraction fan. The standing wave caused:

- Peripheral visual disturbances (grey shapes at the edge of vision)
- Feelings of anxiety and unease
- Physical sensations of pressure

Published as "The Ghost in the Machine" in the *Journal of the Society for
Psychical Research* (Vol. 62, No. 851, pp. 360–364), this study is significant
because it demonstrates real physiological effects of infrasound at modest levels,
while simultaneously showing that those effects are perceptual (visual, emotional)
rather than gastrointestinal.

> **Source quality:** Primary. Published in a peer-reviewed journal, though one
> specialising in psychical research rather than acoustics.

### 3.3 Leventhall's Critical Reviews (2003, 2007)

Geoff Leventhall has been the most persistent debunker of infrasound myths in the
scientific literature:

- **2003 DEFRA Report:** Commissioned by the UK Department for Environment, Food
  & Rural Affairs. Systematically reviewed all published research on low-frequency
  noise effects. Traced myths to misinterpretations of Gavreau's work and
  sensational media accounts. Found that significant physiological harm occurs
  only at SPLs rarely encountered outside rocket test facilities.

- **2007, "What is Infrasound?"** (*Progress in Biophysics and Molecular Biology*,
  93(1–3), 130–137). Comprehensive primer distinguishing evidence from folklore.
  Notes that the human body does not have a single resonant frequency that can
  be "targeted" by an external sound source.

> **Source quality:** Primary, peer-reviewed.

## 4. Whole-Body Vibration: The Contrast

While airborne infrasound has *not* been shown to cause bowel disturbance,
**mechanical whole-body vibration** transmitted through seats and structures
*has* well-documented gastrointestinal effects. This distinction is central to
our paper's thesis.

### 4.1 ISO 2631 Development History

- **ISO 2631:1974** — First edition. Provided basic guidance for evaluating WBV.
- **ISO 2631-1:1985** — Revised measurement methodology.
- **ISO 2631-1:1997** — Major overhaul: introduced frequency weighting (Wk, Wd, Wf),
  defined Health Guidance Caution Zone (HGCZ), and addressed comfort, health,
  perception, and motion sickness separately.
- **ISO 2631-5:2004 / 2018** — Focused on long-duration exposures and spinal
  health; improved precision for occupational risk assessment.

### 4.2 Occupational Evidence for GI Effects from WBV

| Population | Vibration Exposure | GI Effects Reported | Key References |
|---|---|---|---|
| Truck/bus drivers | 1–20 Hz, seated, chronic | Digestive irregularities, nausea, impaired GI motility | Griffin (1990), Mansfield (2005) |
| Helicopter pilots | 4–8 Hz rotor harmonics, chronic | Abdominal discomfort, nausea, altered digestion | Smith (2005), DTIC AD1098433 |
| Construction workers | Broadband WBV from machinery | GI complaints, reduced appetite | IOSH reviews, CDC field studies |

The critical difference: WBV is transmitted **mechanically through the body**
(via the seat, floor, or handgrip), coupling directly to visceral organs at
their natural resonance frequencies (typically 3–8 Hz for the abdominal viscera).
Airborne infrasound, by contrast, primarily loads the chest wall and middle ear;
it does not efficiently couple to deep abdominal structures at the SPLs
achievable in practice.

### 4.3 Key Studies

- **Griffin (1990)**, *Handbook of Human Vibration*: Foundational monograph
  covering all aspects of human WBV response, including GI effects.
- **Kitazaki & Griffin (1998)**, "Resonance Behaviour of the Seated Human Body":
  Demonstrated that seated humans exhibit resonance peaks at 4–6 Hz (vertical)
  and 1–3 Hz (fore-aft), frequencies common in vehicle environments.
- **Mansfield (2005)**, *Human Response to Vibration*: Updated treatment including
  occupational health data and ISO 2631 application.

## 5. Cultural History of the "Brown Note"

### 5.1 Origins of the Term

The precise origin of the phrase "brown note" is unknown. It appears to have
circulated in audio engineering and music production folklore from at least the
1980s or early 1990s, as a humorous in-joke referencing the supposed power of
very low frequencies. The name is a pun on musical terminology ("blue note,"
"brown" referring to the alleged consequence).

### 5.2 South Park (1999)

The episode "World Wide Recorder Concert" (Season 3, Episode 17, aired
12 January 2000 — often backdated to 1999 production season) brought the concept
to mainstream attention. In the episode, the boys discover a "brown note" that
causes everyone who hears it to lose bowel control simultaneously, played on
recorders at a global concert. This comedic treatment cemented the term in
popular consciousness.

### 5.3 MythBusters (2005)

The Discovery Channel programme devoted an entire segment to testing the claim
(Season 3, aired 16 February 2005). As described above, the myth was busted.
This likely represents the most widely viewed "experiment" on the topic.

### 5.4 Fiction vs Reality

Weaponised sound appears in numerous works of fiction (e.g., the Gom Jabbar
"voice" in *Dune*, sonic weapons in various video games and films). These
depictions consistently exaggerate the effects of infrasound. In reality:

- Infrasound at high SPL causes discomfort, not incapacitation
- Effective "sonic weapons" (LRAD) use audible, not infrasonic, frequencies
- No government has deployed an infrasound weapon operationally

## 6. Summary: Tested Claims vs Folklore

| Claim | Status | Evidence |
|-------|--------|----------|
| A specific frequency causes involuntary defecation via airborne sound | **Unsupported** | No controlled study has demonstrated this (Mohr 1965, MythBusters 2005) |
| Infrasound at extreme SPL (>140 dB) causes nausea and discomfort | **Supported** | Mohr et al. (1965), von Gierke (1966, 1974) |
| Infrasound at ~19 Hz causes visual disturbances and anxiety | **Supported** | Tandy & Lawrence (1998) |
| Gavreau built an "acoustic weapon" that could kill | **Unsupported/exaggerated** | Based on secondary retellings; originals don't support lethality claims |
| WBV transmitted mechanically causes GI disturbances | **Supported** | Griffin (1990), Mansfield (2005), occupational health literature |
| Military infrasound weapons are practical | **Unsupported** | Altmann (1999), Jauchem & Cook (2007) |
