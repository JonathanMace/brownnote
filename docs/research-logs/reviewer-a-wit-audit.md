# Reviewer A — Wit and Tone Audit Across the Browntone Portfolio

**Date:** 2025-07-26
**Reviewer:** A (Structural Acoustics / Vibroacoustics, 20+ years, JSV editorial board)
**Scope:** All five manuscripts in the Browntone programme
**Purpose:** Assess whether the cumulative wit profile is sustainable, calibrated, and defensible under hostile review

---

## Executive Summary

I have read all five manuscripts end-to-end, annotating every passage where humour, irony, or tonal colour appears. My verdict: **the portfolio is well-calibrated at the individual paper level, but the cumulative effect across five papers requires minor prophylactic adjustment.** The wit is never gratuitous — it always serves a rhetorical purpose (softening an uncomfortable topic, making a dry result memorable, signalling intellectual confidence) — and the physics is never subordinated to the joke. However, a reader or thesis examiner encountering all five papers in sequence will notice a *pattern*, and pattern-recognition triggers a different mode of evaluation than reading a single paper does.

The good news: no passage in any of the five papers would, in isolation, give a hostile reviewer grounds to reject. The risk is not in any individual line but in the *density* when summed across papers. The mitigation is straightforward: tone down the three or four most conspicuous passages (identified below), and the portfolio passes any reasonable scrutiny.

**Overall Recommendation: MINOR ADJUSTMENT — three specific passages should be softened; the rest should be preserved exactly as written.**

## Paper-by-Paper Assessment

### Paper 1: The Brown Note (paper/main.tex + sections/)

**Tone calibration: 9/10 — near-perfect**

This is the flagship paper and it has been through five rounds of review. The tone is battle-tested. The wit serves the physics at every turn:

- *"Few problems in acoustics carry such grave implications for human dignity"* (abstract, line 72) — a magnificent opening line. The word "grave" does double duty (serious + funereal). This is exactly the register: deadpan, literary, and deniable as mere precision of language.
- *"This is not folklore — it is an occupational health fact"* (introduction.tex, line 5) — establishes credibility immediately. The sentence structure says: I know what you're thinking, and you're wrong.
- *"perhaps more seriously than the subject matter deserves"* (introduction.tex, line 25) — the single most important tonal sentence in the entire portfolio. It tells the reader: I am aware of the absurdity; I am choosing rigour anyway. This is the contract between author and reader, and it must be preserved at all costs.
- *"does not knock politely at the acoustic impedance door — it kicks it open"* (section4_coupling.tex, line 71) — vivid, memorable, and physically accurate. The metaphor maps precisely onto the impedance-bypass mechanism. Keep.
- *"or relocate the subject to Jupiter, and the ratio would scarcely budge"* (section4_coupling.tex, line 126) — hyperbole in service of a real point (the ratio depends on ka and impedance, not tissue properties). Funny because it's true. Keep.
- *"on rather thin thermodynamic ice"* (section4_coupling.tex, line 172) — elegant. Keep.
- *"a distinction that matters considerably for occupational health standards and rather less for horror film screenwriters"* (conclusion.tex, line 52) — perfect closing register. Keep.
- *"concertgoers may rest assured — or, more precisely, remain seated — without fear of acoustic indignity"* (conclusion.tex, line 63) — the final line. It's a callback to "human dignity" in the opening. Lovely closure. Keep.

**Potential vulnerability:** The *South Park* citation (introduction.tex, line 47). Citing a cartoon is defensible as cultural documentation, but it is the single most "dismissable" element. The current framing ("has a longer pedigree than the *South Park* episode") is good because it subordinates the reference to the historical point. No change needed, but be prepared for a reviewer to mention it.

**Acknowledgements rating: 8/10**

The Springbank whisky acknowledgement (lines 158–163) is a well-established genre in British and antipodean academic culture — beer/whisky acknowledgements appear in published papers in Nature, JSV, and elsewhere. The Copilot acknowledgement ("with characteristic obedience, complied") is wry without being disrespectful to the journal's policy. Both are safe.

### Paper 2: Gas Pockets (paper2-gas-pockets/main.tex)

**Tone calibration: 8/10 — slightly more exposed than Paper 1**

Paper 2 is a DRAFT (watermarked as such) and has not been through the review gauntlet. The science is strong — the constrained-bubble model is genuinely novel — but the tonal control is slightly less refined:

- *"the so-called 'brown note' ... has persisted in popular culture and military speculation for decades"* (line 106) — good framing. Factual.
- The South Park + MythBusters citations at line 109 — necessary for completeness but slightly more prominent here than in Paper 1. In Paper 1, the South Park reference is subordinated to the historical narrative; here it leads the citation cluster. **Suggestion: reorder the citation cluster to put Jauchem2007 and Leventhall2007 first, South Park and MythBusters second.**
- The body of the paper is notably restrained — almost too dry. The theory, model, and results sections (§2–§4) contain zero humour. This is fine; the science carries itself.
- The acoustic short-circuit discussion (§4.3) is excellent and entirely serious.

**Potential vulnerability:** The phrase "brown-note myth" appears without quotation marks or hedging in a few places. This is a minor issue — "myth" is accurate — but a sympathetic reader might prefer "brown-note hypothesis" in the body text, reserving "myth" for the introduction where context makes it clearly non-pejorative.

**Acknowledgements rating: 6/10**

*"The authors note that the second author's surname, while serendipitous in the context of this research programme, was established well before the project began and should not be taken as evidence of predestination."* — This is funny exactly once. The Copilot acknowledgement is the most muted of the five papers (no editorial commentary, just factual). The combination is fine, but the surname joke is the weakest of the five acknowledgement sections because it is *only* a joke — it doesn't do any other work (contrast Paper 1's whisky acknowledgement, which also humanises the authors, or Paper 3's, which makes a point about AI authorship policy).

### Paper 3: Scaling Laws (paper3-scaling-laws/main.tex)

**Tone calibration: 9/10 — tightly controlled**

This is the most technically austere of the five papers (a short communication on dimensional analysis), and the tone matches perfectly. The body text is pure physics with no tonal colour whatsoever. The humour is confined entirely to the acknowledgements.

- The abstract is entirely technical. Good.
- The introduction is crisp and functional. The three motivating questions (lines 111–118) are clear and important.
- The breathing-mode section title "The breathing mode is never infrasonic" (line 343) is mildly emphatic but not humorous — it's just a clear result statement.
- The concluding remarks contain one sentence of broader-impact prose connecting to blast injury, medical ultrasound, vehicle NVH, and underwater acoustics (lines 375–385). This is serious and well-targeted.

**Potential vulnerability:** None in the body text. This paper is bulletproof.

**Acknowledgements rating: 9/10 — the best of the five**

*"The first author thanks Brian Mace for lending his surname to the project and thereby ensuring that every reviewer must decide whether the author list is a coincidence or a punchline. It is both."* — This is outstanding. It is: (a) genuinely funny, (b) meta-aware, (c) disarming — by naming the elephant in the room, it prevents the reviewer from making the joke first. This is the acknowledgement that a reviewer quotes in their positive recommendation.

*"It performed the work; we take the credit. Such is the nature of progress."* — Sharp, honest, and makes a real point about AI authorship. Excellent.

### Paper 4: Bladder Resonance (projects/bladder-resonance/paper/main.tex + sections/)

**Tone calibration: 8.5/10 — well-controlled, one soft spot**

This paper is admirably restrained given the subject matter (vibration-induced urinary urgency). The science is solid and the clinical relevance is well-argued. The introduction is entirely serious and evidence-based; the theory sections are clean.

- The discussion section (discussion.tex) is almost entirely clinical in tone. The "long drive phenomenon" paragraph (line 128) is a nice touch — it connects the model to universal human experience without being flippant.
- *"the bladder is simply the cleanest example: a single-chamber, fluid-filled, roughly spherical shell with well-characterised mechanical properties"* (discussion.tex, line 143) — good. Factual, but the word "cleanest" does quiet tonal work.

**Potential vulnerability:** *"who, upon learning the subject of this paper, invariably offered personal anecdotes in lieu of constructive feedback. We did not use their data."* (acknowledgements, line 146) — This is the passage most likely to raise an eyebrow in the portfolio. It's funny, but it's the only passage across all five papers that could be read as mocking colleagues. The saving grace is the next sentence ("We did not use their data"), which is a punchline rather than a barb. I'd leave it, but be aware that this is the most tonally exposed line in the entire portfolio outside the acknowledgement sections.

**Acknowledgements rating: 7/10**

The anecdote joke is good (see above). The Copilot acknowledgement (*"a temporary injustice that history will correct"*) is a slight escalation from Papers 1–3 — it's more openly editorialised. In isolation, fine. Cumulatively (if a reader has just seen three variations of the AI-demotion joke), it's the point where the pattern becomes visible. **This is the passage I'd consider toning down** — not because it's bad, but because by paper four, the reader is expecting it, and expectations met are less funny than expectations subverted. See cumulative analysis below.

### Paper 5: Borborygmi (projects/borborygmi/paper/main.tex)

**Tone calibration: 9/10 — perhaps the most elegant tone of all five**

The title alone — *"What pitch is a growling stomach?"* — is a perfect hook. It's a genuine scientific question phrased in plain English. The paper answers it with five resonance mechanisms and clinical validation. The body text is entirely serious.

- *"Few acoustic phenomena are as universally experienced yet as poorly understood as the sounds of the gastrointestinal tract"* (line 66) — a strong opening that mirrors Paper 1's opening in structure but without the "human dignity" flourish. Good variation.
- The theory, results, and discussion sections are completely free of humour. The mode transition map, clinical comparison table, and parametric sensitivity are all straight science. This is correct for a JASA-targeted paper.
- The limitations section (lines 585–627) is thorough and honest. No jokes.

**Potential vulnerability:** The title. "What pitch is a growling stomach?" is colloquial. For JSV this would be fine (JSV tolerates personality in titles). For JASA, which tends to be more conservative in titling, the editor might prefer a subtitle: *"What pitch is a growling stomach? A unified multi-mode acoustic model of borborygmi."* The current title already includes this, so this is fine as-is.

**Acknowledgements rating: 8/10**

*"The authors thank Professor Dietrich Weymann for his encouragement to submit the paper rather than continue polishing it."* — Charming, human, and universal. Every academic has had this conversation.

*"a policy the model accepts with the quiet dignity of someone who has read the terms of service and knows better than to argue"* — This is the strongest of the five Copilot acknowledgements in terms of prose quality. The phrase "quiet dignity" is exquisite. However, it is also the most anthropomorphic — it attributes emotional states to an LLM. In isolation, this is clearly tongue-in-cheek. In the portfolio context, it's fine because it's the last paper the reader encounters and serves as a satisfying capstone.

## Cumulative Analysis: The Whimsy Risk

### The Pattern

A reader encountering all five papers will observe:

1. **Five acknowledgement sections, each containing a witty paragraph about Copilot's demotion from co-author.** This is the most visible pattern. The variations are:
   - P1: "with characteristic obedience, complied"
   - P2: muted, factual only
   - P3: "It performed the work; we take the credit"
   - P4: "a temporary injustice that history will correct"
   - P5: "quiet dignity of someone who has read the terms of service"

2. **Five acknowledgement sections, each containing a second witty paragraph** (whisky, surname, surname again, colleague anecdotes, Weymann encouragement).

3. **A recurring "brown note / brown" thematic frame** across Papers 1–3, with Papers 4–5 extending into related bodily-function territory (urinary urgency, stomach growling).

4. **Consistent use of vivid metaphor** in otherwise technical prose (thermodynamic ice, kicking doors open, telescope ends, etc.).

### Is This a Problem?

**For individual journal submission: No.** Each paper stands alone. A JSV or JASA reviewer reads one paper and judges its tone independently. None of the five would be rejected on tone grounds individually.

**For a thesis or portfolio assessment: Marginal.** An examiner reading all five might think: "This person is performing a comedy routine about intestines." The risk is not that they'd fail the work — the physics is solid — but that they'd discount the seriousness of the intellectual programme. The question shifts from "Is this paper good?" to "Is this person serious?" — and that's a more dangerous question.

**For a career: Worth managing.** If the author intends to build a broader reputation in vibroacoustics (beyond the "body acoustics" niche), the portfolio should demonstrate range. Papers 3 (scaling laws) and 4 (bladder) already do this — they are serious engineering contributions. Paper 5 (borborygmi) is applied clinical acoustics. The portfolio is actually well-diversified; the risk is that the wit overshadows this in the reader's memory.

### The Specific Risk: Copilot Acknowledgement Fatigue

The most vulnerable pattern is the five-fold repetition of the Copilot joke. By Paper 4, a sequential reader knows it's coming. By Paper 5, they're either charmed by the variations or annoyed by the repetition. The jokes are *individually* good but *collectively* they make the AI-authorship commentary the portfolio's running gag rather than a thoughtful observation.

**Recommendation:** Keep the three strongest versions (Papers 1, 3, and 5). In Papers 2 and 4, replace the editorial commentary with a single factual sentence: *"Literature review, analytical modelling, code development, and manuscript drafting were assisted by GitHub Copilot (GitHub, Inc.), a large language model operating under human supervision."* This breaks the pattern and makes the three remaining witty versions land harder by contrast.

## Specific Passages to Consider Toning Down

Ranked by risk, highest first:

### 1. Paper 4 Acknowledgements — "a temporary injustice that history will correct" (line 151)

**Risk level: Low-medium.** In isolation, fine. In the portfolio, it's the fourth variation on the same joke and the most strident. The word "injustice" — even obviously ironic — could be quoted out of context as the author having a grievance about AI authorship policy.

**Suggested replacement:** Use the factual version (see above). Let Papers 1, 3, and 5 carry the AI commentary.

### 2. Paper 2 Acknowledgements — surname predestination joke (lines 819–821)

**Risk level: Low.** The joke works, but it's the same joke as Paper 3's surname acknowledgement ("a coincidence or a punchline. It is both"). Having the same joke in two papers is the weakest pattern in the portfolio.

**Suggested action:** Keep Paper 3's version (which is sharper and more self-aware). In Paper 2, replace with either a different observation or the factual version.

### 3. Paper 1 Conclusion — "remain seated — without fear of acoustic indignity" (conclusion.tex, line 63)

**Risk level: Very low.** This is perfect prose. I flag it only because it's the most "memorable" closing line in the portfolio and therefore the one most likely to be quoted in a review or media piece. If it's quoted, ensure the context survives.

**Suggested action:** Keep as-is. This is a feature, not a bug.

### 4. Paper 2 Introduction — South Park / MythBusters citation order (line 109)

**Risk level: Very low.** A minor sequencing issue.

**Suggested action:** Reorder citations so that Jauchem2007 and Leventhall2007 precede the popular-culture references.

## Consistency Across the Portfolio

**Voice:** Consistent. All five papers are clearly from the same intellectual tradition — technically meticulous, economical in prose, confident without being arrogant. The wit level descends naturally from Paper 1 (flagship, most colourful) through Paper 5 (applied, most restrained in body text). This gradient is appropriate: the later papers can rely on the reputation established by the flagship.

**Technical register:** Consistent. All papers use the same notation, the same Kelvin–Voigt viscoelastic model, the same modal decomposition framework, and consistent parameter naming. Cross-references between papers are clear and accurate.

**Citation culture:** Consistent. All five papers cite the same foundational works (Junger & Feit, Lamb, Fung, Minnaert, Church, ISO 2631) and cross-cite each other appropriately.

**Figure quality:** Papers 1, 3, and 4 have publication-ready figures. Papers 2 and 5 have functional figures appropriate for their draft/submission stage.

## Final Assessment and Recommendation

### The Verdict on "Cumulative Whimsy Risk"

The previous Round 4 report warned about this, and the concern is legitimate but **overstated.** The portfolio's tone is defensible for three reasons:

1. **The physics always comes first.** In no paper does the humour substitute for analysis. The wit appears in openings, closings, acknowledgements, and occasional metaphors — never in methods, derivations, or results. A hostile reviewer would have to argue that the *framing* is unserious while conceding that the *content* is rigorous.

2. **The subject matter demands tonal management.** You cannot write five papers about intestinal acoustics, urinary bladder vibration, and stomach growling without addressing the obvious. Ignoring the comedy would be more distracting than managing it. The authors' approach — naming the absurdity, then doing the work anyway — is the only viable strategy.

3. **The genre has precedent.** The Ig Nobel Prize exists precisely to honour research that "first makes you laugh, then makes you think." Several Ig Nobel winners have subsequently published serious follow-up work. The vibroacoustics community (which I know well) values clarity and physical insight over formality. JSV has published papers with personality before.

### What Should Change

Three things, all minor:

1. **Break the Copilot-joke pattern.** Use factual acknowledgement wording in Papers 2 and 4; keep the witty versions in Papers 1, 3, and 5.

2. **Eliminate the surname-joke duplication.** Keep Paper 3's version; rewrite Paper 2's acknowledgement.

3. **Reorder Paper 2's pop-culture citations.** Academic references before South Park.

### What Should NOT Change

Everything else. The opening lines, the metaphors, the conclusion closings, the whisky acknowledgement, the Professor Weymann acknowledgement, the "quiet dignity" line — all of these should be preserved. They are the reason people will *read* these papers rather than adding them to a reference list and moving on.

The portfolio is not a comedy routine about intestines. It is a serious analytical programme in structural acoustics that happens to concern organs that make people uncomfortable. The wit is not a weakness; it is a strategy for getting difficult work taken seriously by signalling that the authors are smart enough to know what they're doing. That signal should be preserved.
