## Verification of Round 1 Fixes

### M1 (CRITICAL): Π₀ "quasi-universality" was circular → RESOLVED

**What was done:** The phrase "quasi-universal" has been entirely removed from the
manuscript. A thorough grep reveals zero occurrences of "quasi-universal" in
`main.tex`. The word "universal" appears only in:

- Lines 276, 290: "universal curves Π₀(h/a)" — standard dimensional-analysis
  terminology for a collapsed data set; entirely appropriate.
- Line 496: "rather than an independent universal constant" — used in the
  *negative* to disclaim overclaiming.

The reframing is honest and mathematically correct. The abstract (lines 77–80)
now explicitly states: "this near-constancy of Π₀ is a direct consequence of
the continuity of Φ_n rather than an independent biological discovery." The
sensitivity discussion (lines 237–246) and the discussion section
(lines 364–382) reiterate this framing consistently. The argument is valid:
Φ_n is a smooth function of five inputs; if the inputs vary by at most 1.7×
across four species, the output is approximately constant by continuity. This
is an entirely appropriate scientific statement.

**Residual note:** The test function `test_pi0_quasi_universal` (test_analytical.py
line 1017) retains the old name. This has zero impact on the paper but is worth a
cleanup commit for codebase hygiene.

**Verdict: Satisfactorily resolved.**

### M2: Cross-species parameters untraceable → RESOLVED (with one inconsistency)

**What was done:** "Illustrative" language is used consistently throughout:

- Abstract, line 73: "illustrative allometric parameters"
- Section 4, line 306: "illustrative species-specific parameters assumed from
  broad allometric trends"
- Table caption, line 337: "All tissue-property values (E, h, P_iap) are
  illustrative round estimates"
- Discussion, line 464: "illustrative assumptions, not measured data"

Appendix A (lines 535–576) provides a formal uncertainty propagation. I
independently verified every sensitivity coefficient by central finite
differences at the canonical human parameters:

| Group | Paper claims | My calculation |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| S_{h/a} | +0.22 | +0.2239 |
| S_{c/a} | −0.41 | −0.4080 |
| S_{ρ/ρ} | −0.06 | −0.0606 |
| S_{P/E} | +0.23 | +0.2253 |
| S_ν | +0.07 | +0.0706 |

These match to within rounding. The Appendix equations are correct:

- All 5 groups at ±20%: CV = (0.20/√3) × √(ΣS²) = 6.1% ✓ (Appendix says "≈6%")
- Two dominant (h/a, P/E) at ±20%: CV = 3.7% ✓ (Appendix says "≈3.7%")
- Appendix intermediate √(S_{h/a}² + S_{P/E}²) = 0.32 ✓

**However, there is an internal inconsistency in the discussion text.** Line 374
states:

> "±20% uniform uncertainty in **each of h/a, c/a, ρ_w/ρ_f, and P/E** produces
> a coefficient of variation in Π₀ of approximately **3%**"

This is wrong. Applying ±20% to all four groups gives CV ≈ **6.0%**, not 3%.
The ~3.7% figure applies only when **only h/a and P/E** carry ±20% uncertainty
(the "two dominant terms" scenario in Appendix eq. 11). The discussion text
pairs the four-group condition with the two-group result.

Similarly, the table caption (line 342) says "±20% → ~4% variation in Π₀"
without specifying which groups carry the uncertainty. This is ambiguous but
approximately matches the two-dominant-group result.

The Appendix itself is internally correct. The error is in the discussion
text's summary of the Appendix. **This must be fixed: either change "each of
h/a, c/a, ρ_w/ρ_f, and P/E" to "each of h/a and P/E" (the two most uncertain
parameters), or change "approximately 3%" to "approximately 6%".**

**Separately:** The paper claims an inter-species CV of 4.5% (lines 345, 366,
377). My computation from the code's precise Π₀ values gives:

- Population CV (ddof=0): **3.73%**
- Sample CV (ddof=1): **4.30%**
- From rounded table values (ddof=1): **4.38%**

None of these equals 4.5%. The value 4.5% appears to be an overstatement. The
correct value depends on the choice of N vs. N−1 in the standard deviation; with
only four data points, the distinction matters. At minimum, the paper should
clarify which convention is used, or round to 4%.

**Verdict: Substantially resolved; one numerical inconsistency in the
discussion text (3% vs. 6%) must be corrected.**

### M4: ℛ_scat unjustified → PARTIALLY RESOLVED

**What was done:** Morse & Ingard (1968) and Pierce (1989) citations added
(line 332, references.bib lines 142–154). The table caption (lines 328–332) now
describes ℛ_scat as a "power-scattering cross-section ratio in the Rayleigh
regime."

**Remaining concern:** The formula ℛ_scat = 1/(kR)^2 uses the exponent n = 2
(mode number), i.e., ℛ_scat = 1/(kR)^n. This is confirmed in the code
(lines 247–249: `coupling_air = kR_eq ** mode_n; R_scat = 1.0 / coupling_air`).

For a **power** ratio involving a mode-n vibrating sphere in the Rayleigh
limit, the radiation efficiency scales as (kR)^(2n) or (kR)^(2n+2), depending
on the precise quantity being compared (power radiated per unit surface velocity
squared, radiation impedance real part, etc.). A coupling coefficient of (kR)^n
corresponds to an **amplitude** or **displacement** ratio, not a power ratio.

This interpretation is actually consistent with the rest of the paper: Paper 1's
ℛ_full ≈ 6.6 × 10⁴ is described as a "displacement ratio" (line 113), and
ℛ_full ≈ 8 × ℛ_scat ≈ 8 × 7720 ≈ 6.2 × 10⁴ checks out numerically. If
ℛ_scat were a power ratio (1/(kR)^4 = 6 × 10⁷), then 8 × ℛ_scat ≈ 5 × 10⁸
would be wildly inconsistent with ℛ_full.

**The Round 1 fix labelling ℛ_scat as a "power-scattering cross-section ratio"
is therefore incorrect.** The formula 1/(kR)^n with the numerical consistency
to Paper 1's displacement ratio both indicate it is an **amplitude/displacement
ratio**, not a power ratio. The table caption should say "scattering amplitude
ratio" or "displacement-scattering ratio" rather than "power-scattering
cross-section ratio."

**Verdict: Partially resolved. The citations are present and welcome, but the
physical interpretation ("power ratio") is inconsistent with the formula and
with Paper 1. The label must be corrected to "amplitude" or "displacement"
ratio.**

## Major Issues (must be addressed)

### Maj-1: Discussion text misstates uncertainty propagation result

Line 374 claims that ±20% uncertainty in "each of h/a, c/a, ρ_w/ρ_f, and P/E"
produces CV ≈ 3%. My independent calculation confirms the Appendix is correct:
with all four groups at ±20%, CV = **6.0%**. The ~3.7% applies only to the
two dominant terms (h/a and P/E). The discussion text pairs the wrong condition
with the wrong result.

**Fix:** Either (a) change "each of h/a, c/a, ρ_w/ρ_f, and P/E" to "the two
most uncertain parameters (h/a and P/E)" at line 374, or (b) change
"approximately 3%" to "approximately 6%". Option (a) is recommended since it
matches the Appendix's narrative and the comparison with the inter-species spread
is tighter.

### Maj-2: ℛ_scat labelled as "power ratio" but formula is amplitude ratio

The table caption (line 328–330) describes ℛ_scat = 1/(kR)^2 as a
"power-scattering cross-section ratio." For mode n = 2, the formula is
1/(kR)^n, which is an amplitude ratio. A power ratio would require
1/(kR)^{2n} = 1/(kR)^4. The numerical consistency with Paper 1's displacement
ratio ℛ_full (where 8 × ℛ_scat ≈ ℛ_full) confirms the amplitude
interpretation. The Morse & Ingard / Pierce citations describe Rayleigh
scattering cross-sections that go as (ka)⁴ — a power quantity — which is
(kR)^{2n} for n=2, not (kR)^n.

**Fix:** Change "power-scattering cross-section ratio" to "scattering
displacement ratio" (or "amplitude ratio") in the table caption, and adjust
the accompanying sentence accordingly. Line 389's statement about the
$(kR_\mathrm{eq})^n$ penalty is correct for amplitude and should be retained.

## Positive Comments

1. **The Round 1 revision is exceptionally thorough.** The reframing of Π₀
   constancy as a continuity consequence (not an empirical discovery) is
   precisely the right scientific statement. The disclaimers are woven
   throughout the manuscript, not confined to one throwaway sentence.

2. **Appendix A is a model of transparent uncertainty propagation.** The
   sensitivity coefficients, the CV formula for uniform distributions, and the
   two-scenario comparison (all groups vs. two dominant) are exactly what the
   first review requested. The Appendix is internally correct; only the
   discussion's summary of it needs repair.

3. **The distinction between exact dimensional reduction and approximate Φ_n**
   (lines 456–459) is important and well stated. Too many papers conflate the
   Buckingham Pi theorem (always exact) with the specific functional form
   (which carries all modeling approximations).

4. **The parametric verification is now properly framed** as an algebraic
   identity check. This is useful as a software verification tool, and
   honest about what it does and does not demonstrate.

5. **The code is clean, well-documented, and internally consistent.** Every
   equation in the paper maps directly to a line in the code. The 42 tests
   provide good regression coverage.

## Summary

The major revision has addressed the Round 1 concerns with care and rigour.
The paper's central contribution — identifying the five governing Π-groups for
flexural modes and deriving the closed-form scaling law — is technically sound
and well-presented. Two issues remain:

1. **The discussion text's uncertainty summary is numerically wrong** (claims
   3% where the correct answer for the stated conditions is 6%). This is a
   straightforward fix to one sentence.

2. **ℛ_scat is mislabelled as a "power ratio"** when the formula and
   cross-paper consistency both indicate it is an amplitude/displacement ratio.
   This is a terminology correction, not a mathematical one.

Both are correctable in a minor revision. I recommend **acceptance after
minor revision** conditional on these two fixes plus the CV rounding
clarification.
