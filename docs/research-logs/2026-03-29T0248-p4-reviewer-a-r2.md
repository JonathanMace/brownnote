## Verification of Round 1 Issues

### Issue 1: Reframe around fill-dependent physics ✅ FULLY ADDRESSED

The transformation is impressive:

- **Title** now leads with "Fill-Dependent Resonance" and names the physical
  mechanism ("Competing Geometry and Stiffening"). This is a title that tells
  you what the paper discovers, not just what it models.
- **Abstract** (lines 75–100 of `main.tex`) opens with the U-shaped relationship
  and the competing mechanisms, quantifies the minimum, then builds to
  sub-resonant dominance and coupling disparity. This is the correct narrative
  hierarchy.
- **Highlights** lead with the U-shaped curve and minimum (Highlight #1),
  explain why (Highlight #2), then deliver the practical punchline (Highlights
  #3–4). Well ordered.
- **§3.1** ("The U-Shaped Frequency–Volume Relationship") explicitly declares:
  "The central finding of this study is that the $n = 2$ frequency follows a
  distinctive U-shaped curve." Good — the reader knows within the first
  paragraph of Results what the paper contributes.
- **§3.2** (Stiffness and Mass Decomposition) is the deepest analytical
  addition. The condition d(ln K)/dV = d(ln m)/dV (Eq. 11) provides an
  elegant, general criterion for the frequency minimum of any fluid-filled
  shell with volume-dependent parameters. This is the kind of result that
  other researchers can use. Figure 3 (decomposition) is the best figure in
  the paper — panel (d) with the crossing log-rates is genuinely insightful.

The paper now has a clear identity: it is a study of *fill-dependent resonance
physics*, not merely a parameter substitution exercise.

### Issue 2: Separate model/hypothesis/conjecture ✅ FULLY ADDRESSED

The three-tier discussion (§4.1–4.3) is cleanly executed:

- **Tier 1 (Model Predictions)**: U-shaped curve, coupling disparity,
  sub-resonant dominance, displacement magnitudes. All are direct model
  outputs. The comparison to Aδ afferent thresholds (5–15 cmH₂O) is
  properly framed as a model prediction about sub-threshold status, not a
  clinical claim.
- **Tier 2 (Biological Hypotheses)**: Dynamic strain rate, PIEZO2
  mechanotransduction, C-fibre recruitment. Each is flagged as "plausible
  but unverified" with appropriate hedging ("We hypothesise," "it is
  conceivable," "whether... remains unknown").
- **Tier 3 (Clinical Conjecture)**: Urgency in drivers, pelvic floor weakness,
  temporal summation. Each ends with an explicit caveat ("remains to be
  demonstrated experimentally," "provides no direct evidence," "remains
  entirely conjectural").

There is no bleeding between tiers. This is a model for how speculative
discussion should be structured in an analytical modelling paper.

### Issue 3: Sub-resonant forced response ⚠️ PARTIALLY ADDRESSED — TEXT-FIGURE INCONSISTENCY

The new §3.5 is a valuable addition and the physical argument is correct in
principle. However, I find a **significant quantitative inconsistency** between
the text and the figure/code:

**The problem:** The text (§3.5, lines 293–302 of `results.tex`) reports that
at 6 Hz the wall displacement is "approximately 160 μm" and that this is
"less than one-third" of the at-resonance value of ~47 μm — i.e., a 3–4×
ratio. I verified computationally:

- y_seat(6 Hz) = 0.5/(2π×6)² ≈ 352 μm ✓
- T(6 Hz) = 1.97 ✓
- y_pelvis = 352 × 1.97 ≈ 694 μm ✓

The text's ~160 μm corresponds to the **relative** (modal) displacement
transfer function H_rel = r²/√[(1−r²)² + (2ζr)²] ≈ 0.22 at r = 0.43, giving
694 × 0.22 ≈ 154 μm. This is the wall *deformation* relative to pelvic base
motion — physically the right quantity for assessing tissue strain.

But the **code** (`bladder_model.py`, line 484–485) uses the **absolute**
transfer function H_abs = 1/√[(1−r²)² + (2ζr)²] ≈ 1.20, giving
694 × 1.20 ≈ 833 μm. The figure (`fig_sub_resonant_response`, panel c) shows
~800 μm at 300 mL, matching the code. The ratio in the code/figure is ~17.6×,
not 3–4×.

At resonance (r = 1), both transfer functions give Q = 2.5, so the at-resonance
displacement of ~47 μm is consistent either way. The discrepancy is purely
sub-resonant.

**The resolution is straightforward:** The relative displacement is arguably
the more physically meaningful quantity for assessing wall strain and
mechanotransduction potential. But the text and figure must be consistent.
Either:
(a) Change the code/figure to use H_rel (and the ~160 μm / 3–4× narrative
    holds), or
(b) Change the text to match the code (~833 μm / ~18× ratio), but clarify
    that most of this is rigid-body following, not modal deformation.

I recommend option (a), as the relative displacement is the right quantity
for this paper's clinical narrative. Regardless, the authors must state
explicitly which transfer function they are using and why.

**Assessment of the physics:** Despite the inconsistency, the core insight is
correct and important: sub-resonant WBV at 6 Hz produces larger wall
*deformation* than at-resonance WBV because the input (seat displacement ×
pelvic transmissibility) is so much larger in that band. The $1/f²$ scaling
of seat displacement with frequency is the dominant effect. This is a
non-trivial and practically relevant finding.

## Significance and Novelty

The paper's contribution is now clearly articulated across three levels:

1. **The U-shaped f₂(V) curve and its analytical decomposition** (the
   headline result). The condition d(ln K)/dV = d(ln m)/dV (Eq. 11) is
   general and applies to any fluid-filled shell with volume-dependent
   parameters. This has potential application beyond the bladder.

2. **Sub-resonant dominance as the operative mechanism for WBV coupling**
   (the practical finding). The insight that the bladder is driven more
   effectively below resonance — because of the 1/f² input scaling and
   pelvic transmissibility peak — challenges the naive assumption that
   resonance is always the worst case.

3. **Quantitative coupling disparity** (~6,400× mechanical over airborne)
   at a second organ scale, reinforcing the general conclusion from Paper 1.

Contributions 1 and 2 are genuinely novel and independently publishable.
Contribution 3 strengthens the case for the general framework. The paper
is no longer an appendix.

## Major Suggestions (would substantially improve the paper)

### MS1. Resolve the text-figure displacement inconsistency in §3.5

As detailed above, the text reports relative (modal) displacements (~160 μm,
3–4×) while the figure and code plot absolute displacements (~833 μm, ~18×).
This must be made consistent. I recommend using relative displacement
throughout, as it is the physically relevant quantity for wall strain.
Update the code, figure, and the `test_sub_resonant_exceeds_resonant` test
accordingly. Add one sentence explaining the choice: "We report wall
displacement relative to the pelvic base, as this quantity determines the
modal strain in the bladder wall."

### MS2. Add a sentence on the generality of the d(ln K)/dV = d(ln m)/dV condition

Equation 11 is more general than the bladder. It applies to any fluid-filled
biological shell whose parameters vary with a single control variable (fill
volume, inflation pressure, etc.). One sentence in §3.2 connecting this to
other organs (lung alveoli, swim bladder, cardiac chambers) would
significantly broaden the paper's appeal and citation base. This costs one
sentence and gains a much wider readership.

## Minor Suggestions

### ms1. Table 2 missing dagger markers

The theory section (§2.1, line 23) states that results below 150 mL are
"marked with a dagger" in Table 2. But Table 2 (results.tex, lines 59–77)
has no daggers on the 50 mL and 100 mL rows. Add them.

### ms2. Missing references from Round 1

Several references I suggested in Round 1 remain absent. The most important:

- **Leissa (1973)**, *Vibration of Shells* — the canonical reference for
  shell vibration, relevant to the constrained-sphere discussion in §4.6.
- **Abrams et al. (2002)**, ICS standardisation of lower urinary tract
  terminology — needed to source the "150–250 mL first desire to void"
  claim that underpins the clinical significance of the V* = 222 mL
  minimum.
- Fish **swim bladder** resonance literature (e.g., Love 1978, *Resonant
  acoustic scattering by swimbladder-bearing fish*) — the best-studied
  biological fluid-filled shell resonance system. A single sentence in
  §4.4 or §4.6 connecting the bladder framework to swim bladder acoustics
  would strengthen the paper's positioning in the structural acoustics
  literature.

### ms3. The "early need-to-go sensation" claim needs a reference

Results §3.1 (line 33–34): "a fill state corresponding to the early 'need to
go' sensation in most individuals." This was flagged by Reviewer B in Round 1
(m4) and still lacks a reference. Cite Abrams et al. (2002) or Wyndaele &
De Wachter (2002).

### ms4. Panel (b) of Figure 1 — dual y-axes remain

I noted in Round 1 (m2) that dual y-axes with different colours are hard to
read. The revision retained this design. It is acceptable for JSV, but
consider whether normalising both to their 50 mL values on a single axis
would be clearer. Not a requirement.

### ms5. Broader applicability paragraph

The paper's framework (fluid-filled viscoelastic shell with state-dependent
parameters) has potential applications beyond the bladder and abdomen:
medical diagnostic ultrasound (bladder vibrometry as a compliance measure),
blast injury biomechanics (rapid pressure loading of fluid-filled organs),
vehicle NVH (vibration transmission to soft-tissue organs). A short paragraph
in the Discussion or Conclusion noting these would enhance the paper's
visibility to adjacent communities. This was a Round 1 recommendation (M5
venue discussion) that was partially addressed through the JSV-focused
analytical deepening, but the broader applicability statement is still
missing.

### ms6. Kitazaki & Griffin (1998) is cited but never referenced in the text

The `references.bib` includes `Kitazaki1998` and `mansfield2005human`, but
neither appears to be `\cite{}`d in the manuscript. The pelvic resonance
parameters (f_pelvis = 5.5 Hz, ζ = 0.25) need provenance — are they from
Kitazaki & Griffin, from Mansfield (2005), or from ISO 2631? Cite the
specific source in §2.6.

## What I Liked

1. **The three-tier discussion is exemplary.** I will point other authors to
   §4 of this paper as a model for how to separate model results from
   biological hypotheses from clinical speculation. The explicit scaffolding
   ("The following results are direct, quantitative outputs... The following
   hypotheses... are plausible but unverified... The following scenarios are
   speculative") is exactly what reviewers and readers need.

2. **Figure 3 (stiffness/mass decomposition) is publication-ready.** The
   four-panel design — stiffness components, mass components, frequency curve
   with annotated minimum, log-rate crossing — tells the complete physical
   story. Panel (d) is the key insight figure: it shows *why* the minimum
   exists in terms that any structural dynamicist can immediately parse. This
   figure alone justifies the paper as a standalone contribution.

3. **The pelvic constraint discussion (§4.6) is now physically correct and
   quantitative.** The application of Rayleigh's theorem, the estimated
   15–25% frequency shift, and the observation that this *reinforces* the
   sub-resonant character of the response are all well-argued. This was
   incorrect in Round 1 and is now a strength.

4. **Equation 11 is a keeper.** The condition d(ln K)/dV = d(ln m)/dV
   is simple, general, and powerful. It distils the entire fill-dependent
   physics into a single line. This is the kind of result that gets cited
   by people working on other organs and other shells.

5. **The sensitivity analysis (§3.3, Figs 4–5) is thorough and honest.**
   The tornado chart immediately communicates that E dominates everything
   else, and the minimum-shift analysis shows the prediction is robust to
   all parameters except E. This was completely absent in Round 1 and is
   now a major strength.

6. **The therapeutic WBV section (§4.5) is shrewd positioning.** It connects
   the model to an active clinical research area (pelvic floor rehabilitation)
   and makes a testable prediction (therapeutic frequencies are supra-resonant).
   This is the kind of observation that attracts citations from outside
   structural acoustics.
